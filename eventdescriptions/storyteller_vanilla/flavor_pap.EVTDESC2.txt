In fifteen forty, Pope Paul the Third recognized the Jesuit Order, created in fifteen thirty-four by the Spaniard Don Iñigo López de Recalade, better known as Ignatius of Loyola. He became one of the most devoted counter-reformists and the number one enemy of the Protestant movement. Loyola's soldier background made its mark on the order, which was organized almost as an army. High priority was given both to educating missionaries and also counselors for European royal courts. This made the Jesuit order very powerful in the political scene. In seventeen seventy-three, the Jesuit order was dissolved by Pope Pius the Sixth due to pressure from Christian countries in the age of enlightenment. In eighteen fourteen, the Jesuit order was reinstituted, however, just as a faint shadow of the powerful order it once had been.
