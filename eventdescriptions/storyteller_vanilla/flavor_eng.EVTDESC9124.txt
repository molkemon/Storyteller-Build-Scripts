The sixteenth century saw the number of members in the House of Commons increase, while the House of Lords remained significantly smaller. The power base in Parliament shifted to the House of Commons, who were well aware of the fact. Elizabeth the First had a mix of determination, majestic presence, and kindness towards the members of Parliament that her successors Jacob the First and his son Charles the First did not. The Parliament wanted to have more say in matters of the Church, justice, and taxes, to which the King naturally was opposed. When Jacob the First tried to propose marriage of his son to a Spanish and later a French princess, both Catholic, they had enough. The Parliament of sixteen twenty-eight voiced the Petition of Right, which forbade arrests and taxation ordered by the King. In sixteen twenty-nine, the Parliament voted for resolutions to condemn religious and tax politics, and the King opposed the Parliament and tried to dissolve it. Eleven years of dictatorship ensued. In sixteen forty-two, the Civil War divided the country into Cavaliers, or Royalists, versus Roundheads, the Parliament's wigless.
