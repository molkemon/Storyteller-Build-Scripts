Peder Griffenfeld was perhaps the most brilliant advisor a Danish king has ever had. Peder was born Schumacher to a wealthy Copenhagen family. After study and travel abroad in sixteen fifty-four, he returned to enter state service as royal librarian. Soon winning the favor of the absolutist king Frederick the Third, he became secretary of the king's chamber, in which post he drafted the sixteen sixty-five King's Law, which was a justification of absolutism. In sixteen seventy, the year of the king's death, Schumacher was made Count Griffenfeld. By sixteen seventy-four he had risen to the post of high chancellor of Denmark under King Christian the Fifth.
