While heathen religions can be tolerated, there is no place in the country for kafirs who believe in no God at all. The state church has charged the Jains with being kafirs, nonbelievers in God, and has held a debate before the monarch to settle the matter. The state church argues that the Jains hold no belief in one who is pure, without physical form, changeless, sinless, emancipated from rebirth, passionless, independent, a slayer of sins, and the architect of all joy. This conception of God, they argue, is shared between Muslims and Hindus but absent among Jains. The Jain monks present at the debate fervently repudiate this view. Opting for flexibility, they put forth a multitude of Jain doctrines to demonstrate their theism from the worship of Jina to the very concept of Dharma. Ultimately, it falls upon the monarch to decide the fate of Jainism in the country. We must consider which argument we find most compelling.
