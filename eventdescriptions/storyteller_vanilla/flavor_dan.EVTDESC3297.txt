Absolutism in Denmark was a result of the lengthy political crisis and the acute state of emergency which resulted from the last of the Karl Gustav wars against Sweden in sixteen fifty-seven. Despite his weak position when elected king in sixteen forty-eight, Frederik the Third's political skill allowed him to succeed in ousting two of his main adversaries in the council as early as the sixteen fifties. The two were the seneschal Corfitz Ulfeldt and the governor of Norway Hannibal Sehested, who were both married to Frederik's halfsisters. The king's heroic conduct during the siege of Copenhagen in the winter of sixteen fifty-nine had, in addition, made him widely popular at a time when the nobility and the council were increasingly being discredited. In October sixteen sixty, these events led the estates, the nobility only reluctantly, to create a hereditary monarchy. The new system meant that the king was no longer dependent on the council, and he immediately used his new power to introduce absolutism, which was temporarily established on January tenth, sixteen sixty-one in the Hereditary Monarchy Act before being fully set out in Kongeloven, the King's Law, of sixteen sixty-five, the basic law of Danish absolutism.
