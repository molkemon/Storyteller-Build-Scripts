Many factors certainly contributed to the downfall of the Hanseatic League during the first half of the fifteenth century. Be it disease, misfortune, divine retribution, or the Bullion Famine, the court lost its old splendor and its standing as a powerful mercantile union of hundreds of merchants across the Germanic cities and beyond. In its stead, the scheming merchants of the Dutch court attempt to usurp the position and surpass the league in terms of influence across the markets of Europe. Such behavior is grounds for just wars, and these petty men of Holland must be put in their place lest the league risk inevitable demise.
