Our old friend Casmil had always possessed a talent for magical harmonics. He could follow the logic of the magic he sensed, like one of the strange lizardfolk to the far south who possess a third eye for such things. I recognised the slow rhythm of his technique, the way he probed each ten metres for magical energy, then the next, then the next. It was a ready distraction from the chattering of the lovebirds, and we made swift progress. I did not have time to think about my petty loneliness, about the future of the domain, about the life he would live which was complete without me. I remembered, for a perfect while, that I could possess a future of the mind, an existence no less real. We arrived in a great chamber, in which the corpses of the dead had been arranged as if at council, six bodies seated in six stone thrones. Their heads had been replaced with those of animals – here a leopard, here a crane, here a horned bull. They wore all the finery of ancient Bulwar, their linens pristinely preserved. One could still make out the gorgeous threading at the hems, the symbols which, to my eye, looked almost priestly. I wondered if even the moths avoided the strange song that even now filled our heads, louder than before – the sympathetic ringing of old magic. I whispered to myself, "Erragal?", but my comprehension came too late.
