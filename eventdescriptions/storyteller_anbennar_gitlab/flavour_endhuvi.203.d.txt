The room was the definition of serenity; you could almost hear and feel the leaves falling to the ground, the silence and calm as they tapped, so softly, against the flagstone. The sunlight entered at the perfect spot, illuminating just right, and the temperature was ideal. Such a place, one felt, would be perfect for a debate or a deep discussion, which was, of course, the message the artist wanted to convey with the painting. Unfortunately, it hung on the wall of one of the most bustling little eateries in Endhuvi. It was the busiest hour of the day, dozens of customers crowding the spot. Humans, Harimari, even some Gnolls – all were screaming and yelling for their orders as the workers shouted and hurled plates at the clients, a cacophony of languages and slangs passing over one another. The air was a mixture of food, sweat, and the outside, all mixing into an impossible-to-describe aroma that permeated the place and made any appetite at all a miracle. There, on a balcony just above the kitchen, surrounded by bodyguards, stood the city's ruler and the philosopher, who showed little concern for their surroundings. The philosopher was an older Harimari, missing the right arm and eye, taking tea, speaking, his voice almost deafened by the noise of the place. "True enlightenment and debate can happen in any place. If someone needs a calm, serene place, isolated from all, to think and pursue philosophy, then they are making a mistake. Philosophy must not be taught in a place disconnected from the real, but in a place that reflects reality." The Harimari finished his speech, drank his cup of tea, and poured another one.
