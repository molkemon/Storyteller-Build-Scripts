A new fancy has spread among the common folk, a play dubbed “The Cow of Cloth”. No traditional Khetist tale, the story, originally gnollish, is told through dance, song and pageantry. Usually presented by a group of gnolls who seek to entertain the public, it combines both piety and comedy. A humble gnollish herder owns a strong and gentle cow. One year, the cow falls ill, threatening the livelihood of the herder. Desperate, the herder turns to Nirakhet, praying to her for mercy. But it isn’t the simple story that attracts folks; it is the manner in which it is reenacted. When the gnoll dressed as the cow collapses, dancers and musicians mimic the herder’s grief, while mischievous gnolls play side roles, sometimes moving among the audience, making sly comments or thieving morsels from the watchers’ hands. Greedy merchants try to take advantage of the scenario, foolish priests scorn the farmer for his faith in Nirakhet, but just as all seems lost, Nirakhet blesses and restores the cow to health. Thus the common folk are reminded that mercy and devotion are stronger than greed and cruelty. Laughter and tears accompany applauds as the tale unfolds. Some priests bless performances such as these, seeing them as a celebration of divine compassion. Others comment that such humor must be sacrilege, for who dares to make a cow the centerpiece of a story on the divine? The Clergy comes to them on the matter.
