He is so beautiful, a tall man like those of Fangaula, from the forest beyond our desert. He has made himself beautiful, our KammenasKamyun, to confess, to apologize, for he believes he is the Ashfall itself, made of the Ash; he collapses in our arms and we hold him, we hold him. We do not flinch when his mouth bleeds; it bleeds for us. They told me I was one of them, Komina says, after some stretch of silence. Fey, or something close. The Vile One looks up, surprised. You expected better disguises? Are not the deals familiar, the bargains? Other strong things merely impose themselves. Komina is clearly pleased to have the upper hand on secrets; to have a point in the conversation where she is once again strong. She sips at her oversweetened tea. And so you were their little sister, as they told it. A similar thing, a thing brought through when they came through. Komina’s eyes narrow; her expression is sardonic. Alone in my amnesia. The Vile One gives her a skeptical look, and Komina laughs. Have you ever seriously considered disproving one of Hireet’s obvious lies? How exactly would one go about that? The Vile One allows a small smile. And then they are laughing; at Mama and Granny, at the absurdity of a clandestine meeting hidden from two who have been told in advance it is happening; of the privacy they have somehow nevertheless maintained; of a tea break in a war in which the two of them are sworn to kill each other. But it is not enough; the Vile One can see that. Komina does not truly imagine an existence without them. You must introduce me to the other one, Komina says, finally, as if it is small talk over breakfast. You know.
