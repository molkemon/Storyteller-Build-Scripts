Since the initial status report on Pain Wards, a great number of variants on the ritual have been tested. The initial version simply siphoned the life energy from the soul powering the ward to create a field of pain in a straightforward but inefficient manner. Iteration upon the core concept swiftly led to increases in duration, but the most promising innovation was made by another promising young necromancer, unburdened by Magisterial norms and propaganda. It was realized early on that the Pain Ward does not actually need to generate pain from nothing, as it can simply project the pain that the soul powering it is already suffering. However, this line of research was discarded, as initial experiments showed that this projected pain was not sufficient to deter determined vermin. The innovation lay in deliberately mangling the process of binding the soul to the granary, while still just barely succeeding. Doing so significantly increased the pain suffered by the soul and projected by the ward. While this process does speed up the unravelling of the soul, and is thus usually undesirable, even for ork souls, wards simply projecting the pain felt by such souls last between two and three times as long as souls siphoning life energy and turning that into pain. While it will likely take decades before the ritual is considered perfected, the lead Necromancers reckon that a few more years of testing and iteration should be sufficient to make the Pain Ward a viable option for widescale deployment. Of course, this timetable could be sped up were additional resources diverted. Monthly report of the Black Sepulcher, this month of this year.
