Atukh grabbed the throat of the deer and held it aloft. It was a good catch, fine fur left mostly unblemished by his arrow; his mother’s lessons with the bow had paid off. He slung the animal over his shoulder, and began to walk. It had been many years since his father, escaping the brutality of the Hobgoblin Command, had fled down the Kharunyana with his brother and sister, to a small fishing village in Naibptaa. There he had gotten a job, gotten drunk with the pay, and was then graciously helped home by perhaps the most patient woman to ever live. He always loved how differently each of them told him that story; his aunt possibly had his favourite retelling. Atukh knew that outside the village, they would regard him as something monstrous, a scion of the barbarous north. But then again, Bhuvauri viewed both his halves with suspicion, so he cared not. Perhaps one day that would change, but for now, it was getting dark, and he had promised his mother he would have the materials for his father’s boots before his birthday.
