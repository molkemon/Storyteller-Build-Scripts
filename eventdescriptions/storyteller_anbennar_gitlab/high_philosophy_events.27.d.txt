Death is one of few universal human experiences; one that, in some part, touches each and every one of us. This is true for the ruler as one of their close friends passes into the darkness beyond life; and hopefully into Purtara. In this time of loss, the ruler meditates on the dichotomy of life and death. In the High Temple of Lanjincun, a series of steles within the inner temple wall depict Nermali, the High God of healers, tending to a variety of different diseases. Sometimes she is shown to cure the sick with a wave of her hand, on other occasions one of the snakes upon her necklace descends to the dying and sucks fluids from their veins, and in many more she kneels by the bedside of the dying and administers a salve or potion. Most of these diseases and afflictions are known commonly to the peoples of Haless, as are some of her cures; yet there is one stele, perched upon a stone coffin lined with silver metal that depicts something different. The upper quartile is lined with images depicting Nermali attempting several different curing methods to no avail, until finally, in a large image taking up the rest of the stele, she seems to pray. Her stoppered flasks lie broken upon the ground, her hand only faintly glowing, and her snakes join their master’s hands in defeat raised over a body in seeming exultation. The body itself is coated in sickly black lines. Thin wisps of smoke rise out of the brilliant orange hues which surround it. The ruler looks upon this image, replicated in a handpainted tome, and considers its meaning.
