The monarch was standing in a city – the people strange, the buildings stranger. The monarch saw black ships sail high above – and then to the east, the gilded storm on the ground. The monarch recognized them as their own people – as Taychendi. The strangers around the monarch turned to watch – then turned to flee. But the wave overcame them. The scene changed – the world burned, the world bled. Now, the monarch saw the apocalypse, blurred but so vivid, so real. Corpses were piled high, the blood pooled up to their ankles, millions, desecrated and defiled. Wood and stone burned and broke apart. Then the monarch saw another city destroyed, then another, then five and ten and twenty more. Then a parade of shackled slaves, a hundred abreast, that stretched for miles and miles – they were each brutalized, bloody, missing limbs, blinded and wailing and retching and groaning. The monarch realized they was not alone, for besides the slaves a gilded ruler stood watching – one with their own face. When a brilliant blue light arose and bathed the bloodied world in its warmth, slave and emperor both turned to watch. Tacenie rose above the horizon. Bright, glorious. The last relic of the past. Then it fell. Even a disembodied voice, the monarch felt the heat and the power and the pain. The sounds of tens of millions screaming at once in terror. The monarch merely watched on, even as the Ruincomeagain flayed the skin off their face. The monarch looked so, so proud. The ruler snapped awake from the communion with a start, staggering back. “My ruler,” the Threadbearer pleaded, steadying his fall. “You looked in such pain– I thought you weren’t going to make it. You must tell me, what did you see?” Only now did the monarch notice the fastpaced beating of their heart, the sweat that drenched their body, the lingering burning sensation on their skin. The ruler wrenched them from the Threadbearer’s hand, and turned to leave.
