I couldn’t help but dart my eyes about, trying to take in as much of my surroundings as I could. It must have been at least ten years since I had been on this trail, long before the orks had come. Yet in such a short time, so much had changed. The path had been widened. A number of crude markers had been carved into a number of trees. Much of the nearby brush and undergrowth had burned away. The orks of Sapchopper had left the same scars on the land as our people. The realization leaves me with a frown. “I’d thought it would feel better,” Narawen suddenly spoke, breaking the long silence between us. “Seeing this all again. Knowing it was over. That we had won.” I turned to look at her. Gone was the gentle kindness I knew my cousin for. In its place was a hardness, the same edge I saw in Elaria; rage. My own face grew hotter at the sight of her anguish. “But, I don’t feel better,” she continued softly. “Even though the bastard’s dead. His host of murderers scattered. I just feel…” Narawen paused. “The druids teach that hate has no lasting place in life. The Seelie forgive and forget. The Unseelie fight to overcome. But, what of the Ashenfey? Don’t they hate?” I pursed my lips. Every elf knew of the Ashenfey. Cruel and vicious, their dark prince had spent thousands of years plotting vengeance against some forgotten foe. They were not ones to follow. “I think I hate them, Alara,” she said, looking past me. For taking my parents. For ruining our lives. But, I don’t want to hate. I don’t want to be like the Ashenfey.” Narawen looks at me with a lost gaze, “Can we really live with them? After everything that has happened?” I glance at woods I once knew then back to her. My head spun with emotion.
