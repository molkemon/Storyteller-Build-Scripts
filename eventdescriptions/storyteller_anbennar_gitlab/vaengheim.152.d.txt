As the monarch and their host took upon the odd hunt, did they travel across and beyond Vængheim. Many skalds did they find and call to the halls of the dragonkin, to sing a new song of Elkaesal, and bring them relief. But the dragonkin heard pain in every word, and no skald could sway them from their cause. Many in Vængheim counselled war, and the monarch said, “Hark now, for my heart grows heavy. I take wing alone, to seek guidance in the sky. When I return, I shall give you war or peace.” And then they left their roost. As they flew did they hear a song on the wind. Northbound they flew, into a mighty blizzard, which cut at their flesh and tore at their wings. Death nearly took them then, but the song pierced the wind, and guided them to a fire, kept by a onearmed man. The winds were calm here, and as the monarch took warmth by the fire, did the song mend their bruises and kindle their heart. And they said, “You are wellversed, skald. I owe you much.” The skald, copperskinned with sunbleached hair, shook his head and said, “You owe me nothing, wingmaiden. If my song pleases you, I thank you, for I give it freely. But I have been alone for a long time, and would owe you in return, if you would sing with me.” And so did the skald and the monarch sing together until the blizzard abated. Then the monarch told the skald of the plight of their realm, and he said, “You share the pains of your people, and your burden is mine. Let us return to your hall, and sing to your people of peace.” Then did the monarch and the skald return to Vængheim, and together did they sing to the dragonkin, who found new cause in Elkaesal's slumber, and they were at peace.
