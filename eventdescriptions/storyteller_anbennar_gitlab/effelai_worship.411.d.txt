"Do not linger," said Nitayno, our local guide for this stretch of our journey. "Behold, the darkness. Behold, the rasping breath of what should not be. Behold, the Leechdens." We have trekked deeper into the sinister heart of these wilds. Here, the very earth seemed to ooze with malevolence, as vile swamps and sinking mires stretched endlessly before us, infested with insects of grotesque proportions and other parasitic life. The air hung heavy with the oppressive stench of decay, and the ground beneath our feet gave way to a squelching morass of mud and muck, making each step a torment. A pitter patter, footsteps far more nimble than ours, could be heard just beyond the tall grasses that held the swamp just above the muddy water. No speech was heard, no songs of war or cries of battle. Just wretched, skulking creatures; faces contorted into circular maws made of rotting teeth, moving forward with grotesque ease as they picked off the old, the infirm, and the dying among us. We tried to act, we tried to save them, but their fate was some unspeakable sacrifice to some unknown, malign deity. Their atrocities were a nightmarish sight, as if evil itself had birthed this congregation. The air grew thick with unnatural energies and the blood of the slain, causing the very ground to tremble beneath us. In the heart of the Leechdens, faced with the horrors of this forsaken mire, the relentless malevolence of the Leechmen was a chilling reminder that there were even darker forces at play in this world, and that perhaps Tswohvwoh'ii was a kinder mistress than what lay beyond. As we continue on our pilgrimage, the thought carries with it little comfort.
