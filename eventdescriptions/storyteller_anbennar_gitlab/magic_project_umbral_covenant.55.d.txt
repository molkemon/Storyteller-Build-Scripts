Ikomeye joins against her will. It is Cheni and the Vile One’s cruel trick. They know Ikomeye will join their lattice if they ask her to focus her mind on the Tetrahedron alongside the two of them for long enough. The peeling of secrets from the Tetrahedron, they have come to realize, is just as Ikomeye said. a matter of will, unstoppable and inevitable if one’s focus does not waver. The Genies, so Cheni theorizes, were capable of concentrating a year of will into a moment of Wish. They were unlike other sentients, who to yearn must add effort and time to achieve what they wish. The Genies could yearn enough, and focus enough effort, and simply have things be so. The Vile One cannot yearn so much alone. She needs the will of many; the will of the lattice. Thus, Ikomeye, wilful girl, is next to join. She wanted herself out of her family of misery, wanted herself out of her backward little village, wanted herself into revenge, wanted herself to the side of the Vile One Herself. Yet Ikomeye, in the end, does not get what she wants. After eighteen hours of focus, she feels the connection form exactly as it happens. The strange mental sinew forms between herself and Cheni and the Vile One, and at once her thoughts and feelings and memories flow away from her and back. She sees Cheni’s childhood, a placid fourth son refereeing boisterous older brothers, the magic that finally made anyone look at him for once. She sees the Vile One’s childhood, an endless impression of classrooms and training and striving. She looks at them, at their looks of profound pity, and she knows they can see her childhood too. Ikomeye shrieks. She shrieks for so long that her voice cracks, and then she is fleeing out of the palace. Days and days she courses across the territory of the Vile One. No matter how far Ikomeye runs, she is a part of the lattice now, too. And despite her distress, her fierce will contributes to the unravelling of the Tetrahedron; the Vile One learns more to tell Komina.
