A Gnollish Playwright named Inykh Wisetongue has written a remarkable play about morality filled with laughter, trickery and divine mercy. In his story, a clever commoner named Ghafer together with his fearful friend Cheoz wander through the winding alleys and sunbaked courtyards of Toesah. Surviving through their wit alone, they deceive greedy priests, corrupt merchants and even pompous Khets, not out of malice but out of desperation to escape hunger, poverty and injustice. This everyday life is disrupted when the dreaded raider Xheneva Peasantcrusher storms Toesah, chaos and death follows. Ghafer and Xheneva and others except Cheoz all meet their end, yet the tale continues. On the other side of life, the other side of the Sorrow, sits Elikhet on a shining throne. All the dead characters from the story appear before him. Ghafer, Xheneva, the greedy priest, corrupt merchant and even a Khet. As Elikhet is judging said souls and weighing their sins, Nirakhet appears, pleading for mercy. His words speak not of punishment, but that of compassion, that any being may find redemption if their heart is true. In the end, due to Nirakhet’s plea, the characters are forgiven and go to the afterlife, and Ghafer through his wit is granted a second chance in life. He wakes just as Cheoz is wrapping him in bandages for his funeral. In the end, Ghafer and Cheoz leave the town of Toesah to spread their message of forgiveness. The play has spread across taverns and temples of Southern Kheterata, spreading laughter and sometimes even prayer. Yet some priests call it sacrilege, a jest that dares to place the mercy of the divine in the mouth of fools. How shall we respond to this work of faith?
