In the end, it is so simple. Komina does not incant some grand ritual. She does not walk before Mama Hireet and Granny Ilfa and scream herself hoarse at their treachery, at what they have hidden from her, of how they have allowed her to suffer without any understanding of her plight. What happens now is a feeling, not an act. A thousand times in the last millennium and a half, Komina has stormed out of a meeting with Ilfa and Hireet, cursing their names, and nothing has happened. She could not truly imagine an existence apart from them, and so she could not create one. Here in a quiet place, Aunty Komina betrays her covensisters. It is but a thought, a moment of sincere desire to be done with them forever, and the bond is sundered. What a terrible thing, that something so powerful should die in silence. How strange it is that the ground does not shake. They could never have done this, you know. Auntie Komina is easily brought to tears; this, at least, has always been the impression she gives. But Auntie and Niece are sincere with one another. They always have been. We don’t eat the same things, the Vile One says, and Komina laughs. You’re going to kill them, then? For your Auntie? If it makes sense to, the Vile One replies, and Komina nods. They hold each other for a while, and the Vile One feels out the strange, sedimentary layers of her Auntie’s being. The hidden note of bad breath, the sour under a gorgeous perfume. Both ultimately disguise the roiling beneath, the endless churning emotion of a poltergeist daughter, born of the Day of Ashen Skies. I can feel them, Komina says, softly. Your friends. Then I am part of your lattice now? The Vile One merely squeezes her shoulder; a confirmation, and a reminder that no words are needed. Komina is a strange, broiling nebula in the constellation of minds, tugged gently towards Cheni’s even calm. He is what she has always dreamed of; a moment of peace.
