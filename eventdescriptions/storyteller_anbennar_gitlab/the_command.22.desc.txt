A trained warrior can slay a dozen of his foes in a given battle. An elite worgrider might near a hundred kills, contingent on a well-timed charge. But how many lives did the ruler take today, with the orders they just signed? Thousands. Tens of thousands, if they erred, or had a stroke of brilliance. Such is the burden and responsibility of leadership, and of being the monarch of The Great Command. That thought does not fade as the ruler returns home from the command tent in the capital, but it does become less salient. The ruler checks the house, but it is empty, despite the late hour. With a smile, he walks around the dwelling to the dojo behind it. There, he is the victim of a savage ambush—a trained warrior runs at him, weapon in hand. The ruler's smile widens, and he strokes his moustache. His four-year-old son comes to a sharp halt a few feet away, eyes widening. The training sword—just a little longer than a dagger—drops to the young man’s side, and he gives his father a solemn bow. As his son kneels, the ruler’s eyes sweep across the dojo. He sees his wife, sketching blueprints in the late afternoon sun. He sees his nephews, sparring paused, heads bowed in proper respect to the monarch. He waves them back to their training. And then, with a laugh, he picks up his son and swings him through the air. The sun is warm, his son's own laughter is infectious, and life in The Great Command is as it should always be.
