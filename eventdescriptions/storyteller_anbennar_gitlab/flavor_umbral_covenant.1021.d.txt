The rift opens, and I can see. Granny looks like a woman. I blink, I glimpse again. Still, she is simply a woman. This cannot be right; it offends the eye. I blink again. It is as if the other part of her, the hag, has been teased out like fat splitting from oil; it lies atop her in the weft of things, instead of being integral to her. What strange place is this? Where are we? I recognize the carvings on the wall from my studies; these are Storm Queen petroglyphs, stuff for which my Granny never had much regard. These are things I have learned about on my own time, since I left her exclusive tutelage. I suddenly realize Granny herself is staring, much as I am; she, too, is spellbound. She is looking into a plane mirror; a thing which allows one to look, though not by any means as mundane as sight. What I have called staring is inadequate; it is a thing done in the language of magic. I know that she is looking somewhere I cannot see. That blindness is perplexing in itself. I perceive, gradually and suddenly, that she is inexpressibly sad.
