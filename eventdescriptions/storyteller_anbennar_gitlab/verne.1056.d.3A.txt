The mages in the room stared at the scrying mirrors, wide-eyed and dumbstruck. The Vernmen in the dragon chamber looked to be doing about the same thing. The Vernmen had packed their bodies together, hidden behind anything they could use to guard against the dragon’s blows. An aide from the mages, a cry of “TOGETHER NOW” and “HOLD FAST,” and the battering strikes of the Challúmaton golem’s claws, tail, and wing, had been beaten back. Some of the Vernmen had fallen victim to it—been grabbed from the line, flung away, slammed against walls—but the adventurers gritted their teeth, closed ranks, and readied for the next blow. It worked. It cost them dearly, but it worked. The clerics had completed their spell—exorcised the spectres within the dragon’s bonewhite frame. The Challúmaton lay lifeless on the chamber floor. Well, more lifeless than it already was, being undead and all. Ramujith had been glued to his seat as he watched the Vernmen’s victory—saw fifteen hundred years of work evaporate in an instant. But Jalinwathi, who had overseen half a century of that work? He had leapt out of his chair, hands on his head, disbelieving—shocked silent, but in a different way to the others. They didn’t know it, at first. But they did. For the archmage could contain his pure, unadulterated happiness no longer. “FREEDOM. I’M FREE. I’M FREE.” The archmage looked at his colleague, as did the mages around them. “But—Jalinwathi—the dragon. The beast Sulathrezhil subdued—the great work of the Blue House?” Jalinwathi turned, shook his head, softly. He had tears of joy in his eyes. “Ramu, be honest. We were never getting anywhere anyway.” The two archmages turned back to the scrying mirrors—an army of guards had rushed in now. The Vernmen held their hands up, thoroughly and inescapably caught. Captured. “So,” Ramujith said. “What happens now? It’s just a hunk of dead metal, now.” His colleague simply shrugged. “Well, we both have projects we need to fund, right?”
