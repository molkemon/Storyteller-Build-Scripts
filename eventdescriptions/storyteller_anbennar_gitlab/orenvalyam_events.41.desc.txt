Into the domain, three High Kavalali advanced. Bramashdar of the Kamrayakvalists, Cara Lafquén of the Kavalalists, and Lokemeion of the Monasticists. Some time after they had entered, the lords of the domain made their move. Vines sprouted, trees twisted, and the Kavalali were soon separated, entrapped within a judgment court, a brilliant chorus, the seasons assembled. To each priest, a million different voices spoke, in swirling union. “We look out as dawn advances,” “And worry how your cause denounces.” “For even as you speak for day,” “For fireflame your soldiers pray.” “Now answer us these three questions,” “So we may see sincerity.” “What do you value most?” Bramashdar replied, “Liberty.” Lokemeion stated, “Peace.” Cara Lafquén answered, “Whatever the Kavalakuttam decides I must.” “Why do you spill blood?” Cara Lafquén asked, “For justice.” Bramashdar responded, “For vengeance.” Lokemeion said, “Because we are fools who do not know better.” “How can we trust you?” Lokemeion asked, “I hold no ill intent.” Cara Lafquén replied, “My peers guide my actions.” Bramashdar countered, “You cannot. If I had my way, I would burn you to the ground.” At each answer, the Fey chorus chattered amongst itself, pixie against faerie, nymph against dryad. And at each difference, at every contradiction, the forest’s song grew more discordant, more divided. The domain descended into delirium. It stayed there for hours before the Kavalali realized they could not make up their minds. And so they gave their own chorus’ pact. approved by the Chamber, benches packed. They spoke as three, but dealt as one. “You have naught to fear when we are done.” “For we are legally obligated to state we cannot act without debate.” “Thus we offer you a shield, and where you forbid, we will not build.” “So let us preach, and let us proffer; we have many gifts that may fill your coffers.”
