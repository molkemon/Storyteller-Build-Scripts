And while I see impending death approach, despite the flames, I cannot help but laugh. For future generations glimpsing back, will in our perils read a comedy. The curtain dropped on the actor playing Kong Fitzwick the Third, and a flood of applause erupted within Nimsnoms Theatre. In the VIP section overhead, Hierarch Pordolt turned to his friend Duke Torwick of Southroy. "So what did you think? The original ending was much more dreary, but I suggested they make the King's speech end on a more positive note. Good to keep a bit of hope, yeah?" "It's meant to be a tragedy, you know." Duke Torwick rose from his seat, lazily stretching his arms. "Overall, it was pretty good. I liked the decision to keep the Reverians completely offstage. It fits the theme of Iochand's fall ultimately being an internal affair, despite the invasion." "You portrayed my father in far too positive of a light," came the voice of Fitzwick the Fourth, the late ruler's only surviving son, from behind the pair. "They called him 'the Fool' for a reason. If not for him, Iochand would still be united to this day, and I'd be sitting on my rightful throne instead of this," he gestured to the theater chair behind him, which had clearly seen better days. "Oh lighten up, Fitzwick. If anything, the soliloquy works better because of it. It comes off as ironic." This was his cousin Orra, the only other living member of the síl Iochand dynasty. "You're right about the chairs, though. Surely Portnamm can't be this broke, Pordolt?" "We'll be fine, as long as this play's a success," sighed Pordolt. Torwick gave him a sympathetic glance. It was true that the two surviving rump states of Iochand had seen much better days. Still, Pordolt had felt the fictional speech of a mad king ignite a tiny spark of hope within him. He'd worry about it later, though. For now, there were more plays to produce if Portnamm was to avoid destitution. Perhaps he'd try a comedy?
