A merchant watches as his cargo is unloaded, eager to see his work off and vote in the city election. A guard stands outside his general's chambers, safe in the knowledge that he shares in the grand designs of his officer's homeland. A philosopher debates another, stuns his foe with arguments drawn from a millennium of strife and conflict, the wisdom of those who once stumbled. A landowner dines with a settler's family; noting the way they look askance at his blonde hair, despite the fact that his skin is only a shade of blue away from theirs. Since a leader led his soldiers into a domain, the rule has relied on collaborators from that domain. People who, even if they did not believe in the new way of life, acquiesced to the rule, upheld their laws; then, as time went on, and they began to value security over cultural liberty, began to place their faith not in the gods, but in the elven mind, in reason. These collaborators began to wear the new clothing, speak the new language, adopt the new philosophy; and marry women of the new culture, granting their children the marked teal skin of their subjugators. The latter isn't a requirement for joining the religious order yet; though there are those in the governing council who would like it to be. There are many people from the domain; settlers and officials, generals and administrators; who view this new converted class with suspicion, question if they truly hold the ruler's ideals in their hearts, if they truly can become part of the new culture. The religious order, however, already know the answer. They already give their all on the battlefield as guards, fill the ruler's coffers and granaries as landowners and merchants, vote in local elections as citizens of their domain. In every way, those who were once from the domain have finally taken up a vital place in the empire; whether the people like it or not.
