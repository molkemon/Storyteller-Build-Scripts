In the lands of the Western Territories, it has long been customary that sects serve in place of the state. Across the region, local sects manage forests and settlements generation after generation, training disciples, maintaining order, settling disputes, and providing moral instruction. Though nominally under national rule, internal affairs are often dictated by sect law, forming self-contained systems. Now, a wanted fugitive has crossed into these lands and is said to have been taken in by a local sect, possibly even accepted as a disciple. The sect claims this is an old custom. "Many have atoned through service; this is nothing new." Local authorities demand immediate arrest per national law. The sect, however, warns against "violating the sacred boundary of the sect wall." The matter sparks intense debate. Should the state respect local tradition and avoid conflict, upholding the law and asserting authority over all? Or should it seek compromise, earning cooperation through diplomacy rather than force?
