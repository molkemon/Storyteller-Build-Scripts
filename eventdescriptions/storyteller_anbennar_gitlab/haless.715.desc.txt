In recent years, a gifted administrator trained in eunuch academies has gained renown. Skilled in tax calculation and judgment, he has, in three years, reorganized granaries, surveyed lands, repaired canals, and reformed labor and levy rules—each task meticulously overseen to completion. Grain yields have risen, rivers run clear, and accounts balance. Merchants and assemblymen praise him. "Ten years' work in three—truly a statesman." Some urge his methods be compiled into a new Administrative Canon; others see him as a model to solve the shortage of capable officials. Yet concerns grow. Power concentrated in his hands leaves little oversight; he commands appointments, levies, decrees, and judgments alone. One warns, "A eunuch may nourish the vessel; but if he becomes the lifeblood, who restrains him?" Another says, "When rule by man outweighs rule by law, statutes bend to one will." Shall his authority be embraced and spread, or curbed before excess takes root? The choice is yours.
