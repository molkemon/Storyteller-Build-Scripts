And how different are we, really? The Chendhyan priest asked. You know that we aren’t slavers. We are the children of Munazhlan; you know we won’t fall to Darkness. They had sequestered themselves into a side chamber of the conclave, due to the discussion’s sensitivity. Still, the debate would not hold here for long. many Chendhyans and Mteibhar were asking for religious autonomy, in essence, to reject Nayiru as a being of worship. Honoured Sadaun, surely you understand, the High Kavalal of Sthanan began. Nayiru’s word, his role in the world, in the cycles of history, in Dawn, it is what has guided us all. There are many ways of interpreting his nature. We would allow you to pursue them. But, without his guiding frame... The Taychendi just struggled to comprehend. I mean, how could we live in Dawn when you do not? It makes no sense. Maybe to you, the Chendhyan replied. The Kheionai can pretend they see Nayiru as some sort of philosophical concept all they want, but we know things of this type can exist unseen. But to raise this being, of great Aed, soul, no doubt, to the role of god? It is just not in our nature. And, his Mteibhar counterpart added, I know on our part you say that we can still worship our Nahrei, but surely you know what your request sounds like? You are asking us to raise your god to the same status as our great guide, our protector. What if, the High Kavalal of Royakottar offered, Nayiru and Nahrei were the same thing? The Mteibhar delegate just sputtered a laugh, quickly fading, of course, as he shook his head dismissively, half astonished. I don’t even—you can’t just say that. It’s not some negotiation point. Our understanding of the world is not some sort of incomplete answer, ready and waiting to be filled in by your own. You say you are not interlopers—why, then, do you seem to hold your own conceptions of the world so highly?
