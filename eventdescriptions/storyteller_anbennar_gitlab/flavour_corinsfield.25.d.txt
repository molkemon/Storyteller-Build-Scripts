Reverend Langley sat in the seat of the accused, and thought to himself that things did appear very different from here. He did not quite remember when his doubts began to crystalize. Perhaps it was fifteen years ago, when he was tasked with hunting down an old enemy of the mayor’s, who had just recently been accused of witchcraft. Perhaps it was eight years ago, when those five yearold twins had been accused of communing with their minds. Perhaps it was somewhere else along the way, when the sounds of screaming began to erode the certainty that he was doing good. He had spent decades as a witch hunter and pastor, and this long span of service earned him an unusually kindly demeanor from the judge. “Reverend,” the judge said, “This is surely some form of misunderstanding. Simply enter a guilty plea and we will speed you along the road of penitence so that you may one day resume your work.” The Reverend sat up straighter. “I do not stand accused of witchery,” he said with the ringing righteousness of a lifetime’s worth of sermons, “but merely sympathy with those who have been.” He leaned forward in his seat, and wished he had his old pulpit here again. “The Blessed Book tells us to be righteous, but it also tells us to be kind. Corin’s justice must be tempered by her mercy, and if we cannot have both? Then neither is worth having.” The eyes of the judge began to fog over. “I see I was mistaken. Langley, your mere statements here are enough to find you guilty of heresy, and I sentence you to life imprisonment.” “I am sorry.” cried the Reverend. “I am sorry for what I have done and what I have failed to do. I am sorry for calling myself your Reverend when I was least among you, and I am sorry for the pain I have inflicted.” They carried him away, then.
