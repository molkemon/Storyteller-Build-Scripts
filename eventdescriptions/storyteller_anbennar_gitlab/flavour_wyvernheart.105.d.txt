The monarch scanned the great grey expanse of the sky with a falconer’s seasoned eyes and waited. Clouds pressed down from above while wisps of early morning fog hemmed from the sides. Fog often rolled off the domain this time of the morning, but today was supposed to be clear. The monarch would need to speak to the Court Diviner about his weather prognostications. “The bird has been gone too long, my liege,” the mage accompanying the monarch remarked absentmindedly. Dressed in the robes typical of one blessed with the spark, this mage was more green than they knew. Faint wing flutter rose the monarch’s hope for a successful outing. Faint flutters morphed into deafening waves and dashed any last hopes of a hunt that morning. An adult wyvern, large even for its age, flew overhead; the mage threw herself from her horse to the ground in surprise. As quickly as it arrived, the majestic beast flew off into the morning mists, gone without a trace. The monarch sighed. “Our forebears mastered those beasts. Made them their servants and conquered a country with them. Perhaps we should take the time to do the same,” said the monarch. “That’s stupid,” said the mage matter-of-factly. The monarch snapped their head to the mage. Before they could chide the mage for speaking to their ruler in such a way, the oblivious mage continued as they remounted their horse. “Why limit ourselves to mimicking what was done centuries ago by those with a fraction of our knowledge and power? You are too great a monarch, and Wyvernheart too great a realm to settle for merely matching the accomplishments of old. If we can make the Burnbloods from spare orks and prisoners, we can go much further using wyverns.” The monarch looked at the mage, then up at the foggy sky the wyvern had disappeared into. How could they not have seen the possibility?
