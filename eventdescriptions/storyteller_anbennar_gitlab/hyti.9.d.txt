What we expected were ruins of complex machinery of obscure operating principles, made up of thousands upon thousands of ruined little parts, a puzzle from hell that would take decades to solve. What we got was water. Water and pipes, water and hoists, water and slopes. Water as a counterweight, water as a transport, water as a storage of energy. It is clear there were also advanced machines connected to the hydraulic system of HulJorkad, exploiting its power or helping make it run more efficiently, but nothing survived the fall of the hold. It does not matter, though; the genius of it is in the skeleton of the system, the myriad applications and sheer size. We have reconstructed what we could, and will substitute what we do not with magic instead. There is still old power in the system, the memory of water flowing here for thousands of years. We have flowing water going from the foot of the mountains down in its depths and up along its face, bringing luxury to the homes of goblins and harpies alike. HulJorkad will become a dual city, wonderful above and below, with the two halves mingling during the day and sleeping peacefully at night.
