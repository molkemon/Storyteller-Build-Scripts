The trees liked Frandhi at first. Swept up in the immense and ethereal beauty of the glade, he poured a little of his liquid anise onto a tree root. It was this sort of thing that made him an oddball; too much curiosity for the woods, entirely insufficient reverence for the stone. They pitched camp, rested their weary bones. Stirring at daybreak, Frandhi watched sundappled leaves dance their way east in playful invitation. But Frandhi was a better dwarf than his kin thought him, and he stuck to his plans. Painting an X in runic ochre onto a nearby trunk, the group walked north for twelve hours, and at day’s end found themselves precisely where they had started. The next day, suspecting feycraft, they laid down defensive circles of mithril-treated magebane and swallowed tinctures of insight, near emptying their herb pouches in the process. But again their path led only back to the glade. Morning came once more. Frandhi said to his group, “I have devised a CounterStepping protocol which will allow us to evade this fey nightmare. We go east, drawing a warding rune with our movements.” Instead he simply followed the beautiful leaves, which even by this third morning still bore enough patience to guide him on his path. This was how Frandhi found the temple which promised to make them all so rich. As his comrades toasted, Frandhi sat nursing his flintlock, staring into the spaces between the trees. What lay now between this expedition and starvation? His willingness to deceive his friends; his willingness to follow the dictates of unseen fey. For the first time in his life, Frandhi understood why these beings must be subdued.
