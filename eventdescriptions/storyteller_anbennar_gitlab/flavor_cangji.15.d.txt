Much of what we have learned about Levaajan beliefs includes stories of the Supreme Spirit, the central figure of their faith who split his soul into fragments in order to create the world. These teachings are shared with religions such as Umun Omi of the Besholgi, a common faith among the people of Moduk. But as we continue to explore the archives and religious texts of Kalsyto, we have begun to uncover some older texts relating to the three goddesses of the lakes, and their fused incarnation known as Kalyin. In the teachings of those who follow Kalyin, the primary tenet of their beliefs is that everything in the world originated from Kalyin as part of her creation. This central axiom is eerily similar to those shared by the followers of the Supreme Spirit, and by tracing this shared belief down the annals of history we have discovered that the two entities are nearly identical in their respective religions. Shocking the clergy when first discovered, with time this revelation began to lead to Levajaan and High Philosophy scholars alike flocking to Yarikhoi, where they studied the faith of the lakes and how it relates to what we understand. In our haste to obliterate the magical discrimination present in Kalsyto, we failed to realize how similar these people were to our own citizens. After meeting with officials of the faith of Kalyin, our two cultures quickly began to form a camaraderie now that our image was not that of tyrannical mages. We learn more from our people every day, and our foray into the lakes has proven to not be an exception.
