Self-improvement takes root in the soil of resistance. Just as a heavy weight calls itself to the stone underfoot, so too does the buoyant body expel itself from the sea. These phenomena are the means by which the Haku and Moa themselves attempt to better us. through struggle, our abilities are honed, and those same honed abilities allow us to better embody the virtues of the world. Practitioners of the Calm Mind of Sea and Stone begin their training with simple swimming, focusing on both speed and stamina. This initial phase focuses on mere survival; by acclimating to the body heat loss of prolonged time in the water and developing a strong stroke, the faithful prepare themselves for a life amongst the waves. After proving their foundational skills, believers begin simple diving tests, gradually transitioning from merely touching the sea bed to retrieving increasingly heavy items. These exercises intend to assist the aspiring ascetic to undertake challenges while helping contribute to their communities; pearl divers, oyster harvesters, and coral jewelers all practice this phase especially rigorously. After a dedicated practitioner succeeds in returning twice their weight in seafloor baubles in two trips, they are deemed ready to begin the final test of the Calm Mind of Sea and Stone. underwater meditation. Contrary to common presumption, seafloor meditators do not aim to stay in place underwater; it is not the nature of Moa to remain still. Rather, no matter where the current drags the diver, the expectation is that their previously acquired skills will guide them back to shore, while the meditation itself protects them from the onset of panic while they hold their breath.
