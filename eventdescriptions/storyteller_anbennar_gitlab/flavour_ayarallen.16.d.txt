Sister. The night was still and heavy, even so far up the spire. The monarch chuckled, perching on a credenza. Aren’t we past that by now? The Firanyaleni matriarch shrugged. There were no bars or tethers between them and freedom; only the knowledge that a harpy outlaw could never truly land again. The queens moved their pieces across the great Bulwari board with ruthless calculation, but it was only right to concede when one’s game was finished. What would it say if the last independent Firanyaleni matriarch fled like some scrub vole? Sisters are sisters. The monarch nodded slowly, their folded wings twitching slightly in agitation. They wished for a breeze, some stimulus that would offer them a pause, but the whole complex was silent. As in any respectable harpy household, the servants had left the spire for the night. You’re wondering if I’m more dangerous than demonstrative if left alive, the matriarch said, and the monarch gestured to concede this. Well, one doesn’t give an enemy advice. The monarch laughed. We’ve always been competitive. Yes, said the matriarch, combing out their hair as if they were chatting with a lady’s maid. Don’t think I forget that you usually won. I did, replied the monarch. The question was implicit. The matriarch smiled, setting the brush on a table, and sighed. Well, after all; you only usually won.
