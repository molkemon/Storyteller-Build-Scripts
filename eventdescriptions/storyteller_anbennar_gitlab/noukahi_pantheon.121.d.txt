The monarch and their party make their way through the empty town until they reach the center square, where a crowd of villagers watches the three people gathered with rapt attention. A man bedecked in the finery of a merchant and a woman in the simpler garb of the common townsfolk stand before the third figure, a welldressed man seated upon an elegantly carved chair, his status as chief clear to all. Listening in, the monarch realizes this is a trial. The merchant, a traveling trader, sold a fine jewelry box to the villager for a hefty sum, to serve as the centerpiece of her daughter’s dowry. But to the woman’s horror, when she opened the box it contained the rotting carcass of a rat. The putrefying corpse ruined the velvet lining and rendered the box useless for even holding her daughter’s embroidery. The merchant defends himself, claiming he sold his wares in good faith and knew nothing of the rat’s presence. The villager scoffs at his protest; how could he not have smelled the foul miasma it emitted? Before the merchant can reply, the chief notices the monarch among the crowd. Falling to his knees, he calls out. ‘Oh great monarch, I humbly ask you to lend us your wisdom and resolve this dispute.’
