The fortress of Degumwamba is a squat thing, half embedded into the side of a mountain and still bearing signs of its recent siege. Circling one last time, Mlima tucks in her wings and dives, landing gently in the yard. As she shifts back to her human form, another dire eagle lands alongside her, wearing a loose necklace with a copper icon of a windlord. Her daughter, Irara. The local guards bring them robes of roughspun cloth, and together they descend into the depths of the fortress. The passageways wind deep, carved halls giving way to living rock until, after what feels like an hour, they at last reach the fortress’s heart. a peaceful grotto, a pond fed by mountain springs trickling down the walls. There, unmoving upon a mosscovered ledge, rests a small frog. Rufaro. His eyes are ancient and unblinking as he looks upon Mlima, and it feels as though he peers into her very soul. Mlima begins to speak, but her carefully planned speech quickly crumbles under Rufaro’s gaze, dissolving into a stream of consciousness. She speaks of a land trapped in endless conflict, of a cycle that is bleeding the land dry as the brutalities worsen with each passing year. She speaks of Death Bringing Cobra and the horrors they found in those mountains. She speaks of monsters stopped and lives saved. She speaks of peace. Rufaro listens, and once Mlima finishes speaking, they sit together, the humans and the frog, for several long minutes, the silence broken only by the slow drip of water into the pool below. Irara fidgets with her necklace, frustration visible upon her face. At last, he speaks. The warlord era, Rufaro agrees, has run its course, and while he remains skeptical of the Mengi sky god, an end to the violence is worth some compromises. He will lend his support to Ibekisedara’s cause, and shall send an emissary to the royal court.
