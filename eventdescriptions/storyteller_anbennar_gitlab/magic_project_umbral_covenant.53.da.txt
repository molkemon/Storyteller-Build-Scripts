Not merely grief but passion too, our Kammen, who is a stoic matron and a vengeful son both. He dances barefoot under the endless evening, Prince of Beauty, and weeps with us for the world lost beyond the endless Ash. The great romantic, who knows all feeling as her dominion and all desire as his garden. Komina looks, and then she looks again. The tetrahedron hums; it sends its record as a thought, as speech, as a moment of imagination. “It is one of your names, Auntie. Untouched. And you are talked about alone.” “I am often talked about alone,” Komina sniffs, but it is a feint. “They do not call you hag. According to these people, you have no sisters. They say you are born of ash.” “I have passed as an only child in many places. And been referred to by many metaphors.” “But not there. And not with your name, Auntie.” The Vile One has gotten to her feet, agitated. “And you don’t remember it, do you?” Auntie Komina hesitates. “You told them you were coming here, yes? Your sisters in magic. You told them you would pretend to listen.” The Vile One makes a noise, exasperated. “I’m not offended, Auntie, I’d have done the same. Well this is what I’m offering. surprise them. Don’t tell them what I’ve shown you here. Pretend, the way you do, to be flighty and inconsequential. And count the lies, Auntie, just for a week or two…” Komina regards her evenly. “And the invitation, Niece?” The Vile One, Daughter of the Coven, simply shrugs. “I have met so many of your friends, Auntie. I want you to meet a few of mine.”
