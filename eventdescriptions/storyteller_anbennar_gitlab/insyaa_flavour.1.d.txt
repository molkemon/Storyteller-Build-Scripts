When an oceangoing whale reaches the end of its lifespan, its body will slowly drift to the bottom of the sea. There, its flesh and bones will become a rich deposit of seafloor nutrients for years to come, in a fitting finale to the circle of life. These deepsea windfalls are affectionately and colloquially known as “whalefalls.” By contrast, whalefalls on Insyaa are somewhat less poetic mementos. Skywhales usually stay aloft via helium-filled bladders. However, when a skywhale passes, its remains no longer retain the capability to vent helium to descend. Instead, gut bacteria begin to consume the body and release hydrogen gas, which drags the corpse further aloft until pressure within the carcass exceeds that of the surrounding air, and a leak springs. The descent of a deceased skywhale is somewhat more rapid than that of a waterwhale, and accordingly more noticeable.
