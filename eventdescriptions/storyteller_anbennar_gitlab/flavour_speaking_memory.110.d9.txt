It is as hard to believe there was a time before Wavesong as to believe there was a time before waves, and yet there was. This was the time of Namauhi, who was once a simple sailor. He had a voice to coax a pearl from an oyster shell, and he loved to sing as he worked. But while the other sailors sang in coarse, chorusing voices, Namauhi sang of quiet loss in haunting tones. When he sang, all the others listened. As Namauhi grew older, his songs only grew more mournful. More and more migrated to his lands from the south, and soon the fish that had once fed everyone grew harder and harder to catch. Namauhi’s own family died of starvation. He sang each of them off. Namauhi had by this time become captain of his own vessel, but he grew more distant from his crew with his family dead. He withdrew to the most remote of caverns, but even with nobody around to hear him, he spent most of his time sailing on the open waves, singing of his loss. One night alone on his ship with only the stars to keep him company, Namauhi began to compose a new song to honor all the dead of Kanolowele, and to condemn the injustice of famine. His song echoed low and quiet across the Sunlight Reef, but every syllable rang out with passion, and soon the waves were bearing him across the sea to the tune of his song. Namauhi was shocked, and stopped singing, so the waves stopped. Then he began again, and the waves started again. He grew louder, and the very sea yielded to his command, so strong were his words. So Namauhi told the sea to bear him back home, where he taught all the other sailors the secrets of Wavesong. Soon, the sea itself carried fish to the shores of Kanolowele.
