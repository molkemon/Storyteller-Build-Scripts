Around noon, the monarch arrives at the docks. The sun shines high above the newly expanded shipyards of the capital. Despite their relative freshness, the whole place already reeks of sap and tar. “Mind the sawdust,” mutters the chief shipwright, an old Derranic sailor, approaching the monarch. “She’s already taking shape.” All around them mallets ring against pegs. Adzes bite into the oak. Ropes creak as ribs of timber are hoisted upright like the ribcage of some colossal seabeast. “Keel first,” the shipwright says, patting the long spine laid firm along the blocks. “This lovely piece of timber supposedly comes from the Reaper’s Coast, strong like an ogre bastard.” The monarch walks the length of it. Freshcut timber; no rot, no foreign maker’s mark burned into the grain, no lingering smell of blood on the wood. All along the keel, frames rise. Curved ribs lock into place, one after another. Caulkers follow close behind, hammering oakum into seams. Tar bubbles in giant iron pots. The hull begins to take form, wide in the belly, lean at the bow. “Built to carry weight,” the shipwright knocks on the wood. “Gold hold below, powder middeck. A compartment for rum, so those boys don’t grow thirsty on the seas. And enough guns to argue with an Amber Dwarf and win,” he slaps massive copper and iron mortars. Nearby, carpenters shape the gunports; evenly and tightly, reinforced with beams that brace the broadside. On trestles, masts lie ready, already stripped and smoothed. Sailmakers stretch canvas across the yard and cut cloth for sails that will carry only our insignia. The shipwright stops to look at the ship and says in a reverential halfvoice, “She’s no borrowed prize, captain; she’s ours. You know captain, a ship is measured by the blood it spills, gold it carries and salt it withstands.”
