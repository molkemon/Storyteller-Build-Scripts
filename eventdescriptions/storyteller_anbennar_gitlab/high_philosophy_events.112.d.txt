Suhan's Praxis teaches many beliefs and practices which seem antithetical to the more traditional schools of High Philosophy. In particular, Suhan's Praxis dictates that the lavishness of the High Temples; their velvet cushions, golden candles, and luxurious trappings, should be treated with borderline iconoclastic fervor. To them, the addition of mortals to those temples in the form of luxury goods is a despoilment of these holy places, tainted with the temptations of man. Philosophers who follow Suhan's Praxis will often make a point of removing these items and melting them down, or selling them where possible. In practice, they should leave the murals, statues, and artifacts left by the High Gods alone, but this is not always the case. The ruler has just received disturbing reports from a group taking this doctrinal principle too far, and in the process of removing luxuries defaced and removed the gems and metals from the wall of a minor High Temple. The local population, in particular members of the Silk Turban school, are outraged and are demanding harsh action. Yet, for some reason, these men have turned themselves in and are begging for forgiveness; a small group of Suhan's Praxis practitioners follow with them, advocating for leniency.
