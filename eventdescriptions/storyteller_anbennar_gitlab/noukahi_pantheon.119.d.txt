The monarch comes to a bridge that has been swept away by a recent flood. The ruler surveys the riverbanks and sees that the repairs are starting, with materials gathered, but the builders idle in their camp. The monarch soon arrives at the riverbank and notices two people arguing. An older woman shakes her head at the younger, pointing to a schematic draft that differs from the one the youth holds up. "Surely you see how useful a bridge like this is, Grandmother. It won’t be swept away in the next flood." "It is untested, and you should know better than to risk the lives of our kinfolk with something new." "It is only new here. I have seen it used on the coast and in the northern mountains." The monarch asks around, learning this argument has lasted for days now. The young builder has a plan for a suspended design that is less likely to be swept away by floods and popular with the younger folk. But the mayor insists that a traditional design must be chosen because it is trusted to be more secure and stable by the older townsfolk who need the bridge. Noticing the monarch’s retinue and crown, the mayor and builder walk over, asking for the monarch to settle their dispute.
