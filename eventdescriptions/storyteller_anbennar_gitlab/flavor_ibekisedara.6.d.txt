The Second Council of Reflection is a quieter affair. Instead of everyone of any small importance cramming themselves into the halls of Meworetiketema, it is a smaller group seated around a table in Mengamsipe. Here sit a large contingent of the Semayebaka, a handful of generals and their aides, the frog Rufaro with his attendant, making a rare journey outside Degumwamba. Fewer nobles, and almost no merchants. And near the Mezhi in a place of honor sits Vitaragomo Mlima Ishimwe, shadowed by her heir Irara. The council commences with a prayer and a discussion of their successes in Bamaliwuni. The land is being restored, towns rebuilt and fields resown. No more bandits plague the countryside; it has been months since the last warlord attempted to rise. Each day more and more of the Dakinshi flock to the new sky temples and embrace the light of Amilak. Ibekisedara has taken these wartorn lands and rebuilt them into Madrimalak come again. With the past out of the way, Mezhi, the ruler, shifts the council's focus to the future of the realm. One of the Semayebaka quickly puts forth the question on everyone's mind. Why stop at Bamaliwuni? To the south are the Tanizu tribes, caught in their own cycles of war, unable to resist the creeping advance of Yrilak’s swamp. To the west, the Sitewosi would rather battle their fellow Mengi than unite against that same encroachment from our ancient enemy. Why should Ibekisedara not stem this chaos as it did in Bamaliwuni? The room is filled with murmurs of agreement, and one by one the council members rise and proclaim their agreement with these crusades. Irara is one of the first to shout her support, rising even before her mother. Rufaro simply watches, quiet as ever, his thoughts opaque to all but himself.
