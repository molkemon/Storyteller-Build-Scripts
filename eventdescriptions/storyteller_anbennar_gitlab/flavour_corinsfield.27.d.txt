Elias Proudfoot fidgeted on the seat of the accused, where several books had been placed on top of the chair seat to allow him to see over his table. If only he were simply allowed to explain the nature of his ministry to the people, surely they could be made to see the light. “How do you plead, Goodman Proudfoot?” the judge barked suddenly. He peered over his bench. “You stand accused of weaving vile enchantment into the songs you sing to your flock, and further witchery in your methods of leading them away from Corin.” “Goodman, if you would only allow me my lute, I could show you my methods of praising Corin, and how they have brought the ruinborn to worship.” Proudfoot was cut off by a shout somewhere between the words of a man and the growling of a beast. “You have spoken too long already. Every moment you dare to sit here as if there is a possibility of your innocence is a moment this audience is being corrupted by the illusions you are no doubt weaving at this very moment.” Proudfoot was struck by the sudden thought that the judge’s face appeared to be turning the exact same shade of red as was used on the flag of Corinsfield. “IF YOU THINK I WILL ALLOW A HALFLING, AND A WITCH HALFLING BESIDES, TO COME TO MY COURT AND LECTURE ME ON THE PROPER WORSHIP OF CORIN, YOUR ENCHANTMENTS HAVE FINALLY HOLLOWED OUT YOUR FEEBLE BRAIN.” The judge paused to take a gulp of air as if this would be his last chance. “There will be no trial today, for the danger to this community is too great as it stands. The witch is guilty. Burn this witch in the town square, but before you do so, have his vocal cords ripped out and displayed to his disciples, so that they might see what his witchery has earned him and they may be returned to the faithful.” That appeared to be the trial, then. As Proudfoot was led away, he dwelled for a moment on an old saying of his mother’s.
