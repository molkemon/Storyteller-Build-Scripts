Haku tells how to act on land. prove nurturing, like the soil. Trees must cast their roots in the dirt, and turtles must lay their eggs on land. Birds must alight on the shore to nest and rest, just as we must return to land to make ships and homes. Even volcanoes burgeon with the flow of magma. Haku is defined by building and growth, and when one steps on land one becomes part of the stone, too. One must focus on improving oneself to cultivate strong Haku. Moa says how to act at sea. flexibly, like water. The water chooses many ways to show its forms; at times it dances as a wave, and other times it hovers as a cloud. On some days it even bites as hail. Water fills the shape of whatever container holds it, and in its domain the world constantly changes. We should also be so willing to sculpt ourselves anew, so quick to adapt to our surroundings. The sea can be dangerous, but when we allow ourselves to become as the waves, we move within the flow of danger, rather than against it. Lai prescribes how to treat others. be constant, like the sky. No matter where your feet lie in the world, be it on swell or soil, the sky will reign above. It is indestructible, inexhaustible, and necessary. Be unto others a constant source of fresh air, an undeniable force of revitalization.
