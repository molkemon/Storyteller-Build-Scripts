Dotted across Haless are the High Temples, symbols of worship for followers of High Philosophy. Their exact date of construction is unknown, but they have shaped the religion as it exists today. The Raheni believe these High Temples to be structures left behind by their gods, and consequently have turned them into centers of reverence. This concept of the temples being structures left by the gods was brought to the forefront of the religion by Harimar the Great. Harimar sought to both expand his empire and seek out all the High Temples left behind by the gods. Seeking to emulate these High Gods, Harimar considered the temples sacred structures containing divine revelations. By studying the temples, the philosopher-priests of Haless sought to uncover the path towards spiritual unity as ordained by the High Gods. Ultimately, Harimar, after conquering nearly the entirety of Haless, would enter the High Temple of Tianlou in seventy-nine AA and never be seen again. While the Noukahi Pantheon cares more for heroes and epic stories than abject reverence of the gods, Azaly believes that he can fashion a better home for the griots by referencing the architectural design of the High Temples. In this endeavor, he was assisted by Harimari guides eager to follow in the path of the High Gods. The temple structures themselves extend both deep below ground and up towards the heavens, and are nonuniform in their distribution throughout Haless. Within, he studied the etchings, murals, and friezes depicting the High Gods in various scenes, noting how the scale could be adapted to tell the stories of Noukahi heroes. He has returned alongside several Harimari artists, who will help him ensure that the temples he builds within Bivuzena are created with a suitably inspiring art style and spark lively philosophical discussion about divinity and heroism.
