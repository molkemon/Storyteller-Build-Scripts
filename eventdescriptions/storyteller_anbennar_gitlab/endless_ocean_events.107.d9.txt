Some cultures liken the barrier between life and death to a veil. a thin piece of fabric, partially obscuring the sight just beyond its curtain. In some regards, this is akin to cultural beliefs about Kiamoa Worship. Gaze into the Kiamoa, and one might see the briefest flashes of lightning, or shadows without origin. The sea flows into its whorl—why wouldn’t the sea persist beyond? At times, the skywhales drift overtop its thunderheads, and stars rain past its perimeter during Tearfall. Once, our ancestors came to this land accompanying the great Dragon Turtles on their journey here from the afterlife. There is no doubt to be had. somewhere, something lies beyond the Kiamoa. For a storm, it is almost polite. Its boundaries are clear, despite occasional offshears, and the constant counterclockwise sweep of its winds have done as much to deliver sailors as they have to sink them. Nevertheless, the vast power of the Kiamoa cannot be overstated. Those same Dragon Turtles who once found refuge beyond its path have also found themselves lost to its surge. Those same ships who have ridden its winds have dissolved in the force of its grasp. For an era, the great typhoon has loosed its furious gale around Insyaa, awesome and terrible. Fitting, that the boundary between life and death is so tantalizingly thin, yet incontrovertibly impermeable. A veil, indeed.
