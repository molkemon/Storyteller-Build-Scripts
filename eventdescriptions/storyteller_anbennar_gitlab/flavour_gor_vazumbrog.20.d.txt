The grand plaza of Gor Vazumbrog was a spectacle of triumph. Hundreds of dwarves filled the square, eager to hear the ruler's proclamation. Overhead, banners bearing the sigils of reclaimed glory swayed gently in the cavern draft. At the raised dais in the centre stood the ruler, their figure radiant in ancestral armour, their ceremonial sword resting against the small balustrade, as they began to speak. Today, we honour the blood and sweat— The words froze in the air as the fires dimmed, their flames guttering inexplicably. A soundless tension gripped the chamber, a suffocating stillness that pressed against every chest. Then, without warning, the eastern gallery shattered inward, masonry exploding in a cascade of dust and debris. From the void beyond, she emerged. Shrouded in a fleshy purple veil that seemed to writhe like living mist, the dragon moved without a sound, her monstrous form obscured, yet unmistakable. Her double lower jaw parted, revealing rows of impossibly long fangs that glistened like wet obsidian. The sickle-shaped claws on her toes and wingtips glinted, slicing through the air with a predatory grace. The hold's bells rang out, but too late, their peals drowned by chaos. In the erupting panic soldiers scrambled to form ranks, but their movements were frantic, disordered. The dragon surged forward, her clawed wings slicing effortlessly through the first ranks, leaving bodies split and armour crushed. Her veil billowed, and where it brushed the dwarves, they faltered—eyes wide, weapons slack, terror flooding their faces. The poisonous miasma came next, seeping from her jaws in an eerie silence. It crept through the chamber, invisible at first, until the first dwarf collapsed, choking. Then another. And another. Then a soldier in one of the back lines lowered his axe, staring blankly at the bloodsoaked stone. His lips moved, silent, before he screamed—a raw, guttural sound—and lunged at his comrade howling 'Traitor' and swinging wildly. The other dwarf barely managed to block, his face twisted in confusion. Around them, the chaos spread like fire. The ruler leapt from the dais, sword raised high, but the dragon moved too quickly. Her claws tore through the masterwork armour as if it were parchment. The ruler fell, lifeless, their body crumpled at her feet. With one last sweep of her massive form, the dragon tore through the hold gates and disappeared into the darkness of the Serpentspine. The city lay shattered, its people broken. The bells, late and feeble, tolled in the distance, a mournful echo of a hold undone.
