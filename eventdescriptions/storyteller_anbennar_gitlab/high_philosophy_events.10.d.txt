The true path to Purtara is to emulate the High Gods’ penchant for strength and dominance. It is through those facets that the High Gods made war; their greatest accomplishment; and ensured peace, prosperity, and monumentality; their second greatest accomplishment. One must strive to be strict yet generous with their clients, hawkishly protect their charges, and subsume their enemies. This is the Kartya most truthfully espoused by the High Gods and their temples. Sarika Mrudaya of the Unbroken Claw School, the scholar from the Unbroken Claw school, read that passage inside the ruler's chambers, but it had resonated through the halls of the ruler's home ever since. The reverberations passed into the countryside, to the petty lords and the army officers. Provincial governors cracked down on detractors and recruited local power holders into their patron-client relations. Mayors encouraged normal drilling of local militias, and the cities and towns under this new stricter rule prospered. Today a group of generals and lower ranking officers came before the ruler. They proclaimed that the banners swelled with new recruits and that it was time for war. At first the ruler hesitated, and the gathered crowd grew rowdy. Some soldiers whispered complaints. Some spoke openly that the ruler cared little for Pichka and only paid it lip service. One irate general shouted that the ruler wished to prevent them from achieving Nitikamarja. At this claim, the Unbroken Claw Scholar, who stood beside them, held up a hand and interjected, "It is the will of the High Gods that these men display their prowess of arms, discipline, and of course strength, through battle. They are willing and ready to face your enemies and emerge victorious. One should not deny them the opportunity to achieve Purtara. What is your command?" The military advisors and soldiers of the court looked to the ruler with stern faces; they nervously awaited the ruler's proclamation.
