The rift opens, and I can see. The boy’s steps are heavy and dull for a child so young. He is emaciated. Nevertheless, he clambers up onto the outcrop, simply to be somewhere which is not the endless, miserable noise of the refugee camp. Nobody will admit that their people are dying; nobody will say anything but to parrot niceties about Surakel. Such adult opinions in one so young, such cynicism. The gnolls hound them everywhere they go, and that fool Maqet’s ramblings convince him not a bit. Even his mother has become contemptible to him; endlessly crying as if she has lost all her sons, as if he is not still here right in front of her, as if he is no compensation at all. Sometimes he is scared of how much he hates her. He finds that he would rather starve in the quiet. He shapes a lens or two in the air, refracting the harsh light of the sun; rainbows form in the air, more party trick than true craft. Yet it shows promise. When Komina approaches, a few moments from now, she will not pressure him. He will be the first to ask if she can take him away, for his clever gaze will notice the cleanness of her clothes, the uncanny elegance with which she moves. When she finally offers, he will readily abandon his people.
