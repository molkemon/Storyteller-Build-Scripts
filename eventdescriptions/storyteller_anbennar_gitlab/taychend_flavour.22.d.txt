The monarch stalked through the jungle, whiteknuckled hands gripping their spear. Blood spatters covered their armor, and as their heartbeat pounded, the loudest sound in a still forest, a thousand regrets raced through their mind. The monarch should have taken a larger hunting party. The monarch should have brought those Chendhyans they saw on the way here. The monarch should have taken a mage. The monarch should have struck the beast when they had the chance – while it was devouring their bodyguard Galinpan. The monarch should have kept a better grip on their sword – shouldn’t have dropped it. The monarch should have retreated after Varamesh died. The monarch should have never tried to hunt the beast at all. But it was too late for that now. The monarch was on their own, far from rescue, with a spear in their hand, bloodied armor, and a monster beyond comprehension on their tail. It all made sense now, at the end of everything. The monarch was not hunting the ghost in the hills – it was hunting them. The monarch thought they heard a faint breath to their right. The monarch snapped around, heart catching as they readied their spear – but there was nothing. There didn’t seem to be anything. But the beast was a ghost – it could hide in plain sight, pounce from anywhere. The monarch stepped forwards slowly, carefully, one foot in front of the other. The monarch brought their spear low and began to sweep before them, their eyes darting from place to place, hoping to see any sign of disturbance. The ruler’s gaze was turned to the left when they suddenly felt the end of their spear dip down. A heartbeat later wood cracked, and the weight lifted. The monarch’s eyes snapped back to the now broken spear – and the feline paw laid over the spear tip on the ground. The warlord looked up to the Dey Vettaga’s head, as it stared at them, its camouflaged coat replaced with a brilliant white, its piercing blue eyes narrowed, its teeth bared. It looked like it was smiling.
