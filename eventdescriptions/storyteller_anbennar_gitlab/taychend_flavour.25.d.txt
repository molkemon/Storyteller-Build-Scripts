The monarch stumbled through the forest trail, clutching their sabre with whiteknuckled hands. Blood was spattered across their armour, but it coated the edge of their sword as well. Their bodyguards, Galinpan and Varamesh, were dead, but they did not die in vain. They had slashed the beast across the back with their sword as it was mauling Galinpan, a sure hit, though it darted away before they could attack again. Injured but not mortally so, but the actual damage did not matter. They had hit it, and that meant blood. Their heart pounded as they searched the forest floor, moving from one bloodspecked leaf to the next. It had to be cloaked, its fur invisible, but not untrackable, not anymore. The ruler stopped, tracked the trail with their eyes but did not move. The blood drops grew heavier as their gaze followed them. They had to squint, but they could just make out a break in the light foliage that carved the forest floor. The brown of the dirt looked wet, darker than that beneath their own feet. The monarch silently thanked the gods for their good eyes. Now, they needed to pray that they granted them courage. The ruler took a deep breath, raised their sabre, and charged with a cry. They had only made it a few sprinted steps before the Dey Vettaga pounced from its cloaked position. With an earsplitting roar, the forest in front of the ruler was replaced with white fur and piercing blue eyes and terrible claws. The monarch swung their sword with all the might they could muster. SlaverNoble steel flashed, then the beast crashed into the warlord with enough force to barrel them over. When they finally came to their senses, their front was covered in blood, and when they pushed the Dey Vettaga’s corpse off them, they found the source, as a gaping hole stood where the beast’s head had once been. The monarch laughed as they collapsed from the exhaustion. The ghost in the hills had been slain.
