The hero is not carved away to nothing by war; this is the first surprise. Barrelchested and proud, the hero’s belly is still soft despite his obvious strength. The hero is a little shorter than the person expected, and he does not swagger as the heroes from stories do; indeed, there is a melancholy to him, even as the iron discipline of his movements demands the same in response. The initiates dance the practice steps of battle under the hero’s watchful eye, the stance and the sweep and the jab. The person is not a hero. He has never been especially exceptional. He is a mage, which marks him out somewhat, but his only real affinity therein is to the manipulation of soil, the growing of plants. He stammers over combat mantras and has never successfully cast Ironskin in the heat of combat. Such a pacifism is the preserve of the sages, so his friends reassure him, but it counts for little when he is scolded for his weak guard. Indeed, the only special courage the person has ever shown is that he is the first to approach the hero, two gruelling weeks in. “You fought in Shamakhad?” the person asks. The hero nods. “My friends are eager to go,” the person says, carefully. “Important for most soldiers to feel that way,” the hero replies. “What about cowards?” the person asks. He cannot help but read the hero’s eyes closely; he has come here for forgiveness, after all. Nothing in the person’s life seems as important as to have this man specifically absolve him. “They survive no less often,” the hero says, not unkindly. “And no more.”
