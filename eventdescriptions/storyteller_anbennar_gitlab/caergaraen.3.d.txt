Narha downed the rest of the bottle, then threw it off the side of the ship. The sacred isle of Assakadail, a low mountain rising from waters of the Taras’su Ervan, loomed in the distance. “Ceremony, ritual, history – I should be out there… fighting. Raiding– liberating. Isn’t that what this damned sword wants from me?” “Mother.” A disappointed voice to her right. Narha gave a glance to her daughter Kelhana, scoffed as she turned back, even as a purr from her jaguar, lounging on the deck nearby, betrayed her feelings. “This is not just ceremony – this is governance. You are not a pirate any more, you’re a queen. And the threats we face—” The Waterclaw laughed suddenly. “They’ll be vanquished by blood and battle.” She drew Saerasyar from its quenchingsheath, letting the flickers of green near her face. “Larankar, Ameion, the Bloodcloaks– we don’t beat them sitting on our asses, hiding in our coves. We go out, and we win.” Kelhana crossed her arms, gave a pout. “We win? That’s it? Why haven’t we just won before?” Narha looked over the blade, the flamewreathed silversteel cutting across her daughter. “Because the old kings were weak? Ineffectual? Immoral fools, who rested on their freedom while Gophira burned, and Taychend opened itself up to us?” “Because we do not think. We charge ahead, unmoored, then isolated, then annihilated. Did Munazhlan believe his paradise would come about in a day? Please mother, think of what happened to Saerraeg– to Geladhlan the Liberator, who once wielded the sword in your hands. He burned bright, yes – and then he burnt out. Slain, and his empire with it. Is that what you want?”
