The hobgoblins rounded up everyone from our village and made us touch a rock. It burned when I touched it, and left a red mark on my palm. That must have meant something because I was clasped in shackles and dragged away. Gods, they hurt; it's like any part of me they touch dies. It's bearable through the clothes, at least. I don't know why they did this; is it because of the revolt? I explained I wasn't a part of it, but they wouldn't listen. Oh mother forgive me, oh river bless me, I don't know when I will be home again. Today we arrived at a series of tents surrounded by simple wooden walls. The hobgoblins call this place the "hetenapuzo," in our language it means "place of penance" or "camp of contrition." I suppose we're guilty, though of what I know not. There are people here from places farther away than I. We talked at mealtime, though I didn't get much from it. everyone is so jumpy, it's like we're all waiting for a storm to break. A few days ago we were marched out of the camp. After several miles, the din of battle could be heard. At the front lines, our guards unclasped us and shoved us forward. Someone tried to flee; he managed to get his hands on a guard, and in moments she was on fire. After that, he ran until his back was pierced by an arrow of black stone. Then the enemy was upon us, and in moments the battlefield was chaos. Oh Nermali preserve me, oh Dipar have mercy. I stretched out my hands to defend myself, and the man on the other side withered away to bone and ash. Then they took someone else and chained him until he was screaming. They dragged him up the hill to the fort walls, and in moments, a bright light shone and a shockwave blew us all back. Then the fort walls had a hole in the side.
