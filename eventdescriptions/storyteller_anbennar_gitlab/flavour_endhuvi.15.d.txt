No one noticed at first; catfish were a common sight along the Kharunyana, after all, and a lone few swimming up the freshly dug channel towards the now flooded halls of the Great Temple were hardly a surprise. There was probably plenty for the little fellows to scavenge and feed off of in the freshly dug earth, various bits of interesting debris to inspect in the wake of the channel project. A week later, however, two or three catfish had become a vast school, slowly patrolling the flooded temple grounds. People began to gather at the shore, marveling at such a peculiar sight. So captivating was the vast school that the shifting waters escaped people's attention at first; that down the channel, at the river's edge, water had begun to flow. Spreading from the point the channel merged with the river, the waters began to move once more, a small tide formed. The water lifted boats gently, pushing those downriver along a bit faster than their captains probably expected. Upstream too, like a long rope being tugged, the waters began to flow along and movement returned to the river.
