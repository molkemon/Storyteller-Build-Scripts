Not all songs I have to sing are about happy things; some are songs of terror. But you need not be afraid, because the jelli know that for every song of terror there can come a song of redemption. I sing of redemption to you now. In days gone by, Kanhati watched over the Kaino, and made sure the rain fell on their fields and the sun warmed their backs. But that all changed when the Kaino fell under the sway of evil, and began to use the souls of their ancestors for selfish means. They disrupted the cycle of life and death and defied the law of the gods. Their cruelty and pride knew no bounds. Kanhati the Black Antelope was a god of death, but he was a god of death justly received, and he recoiled with horror at the actions of his people. He tried to warn the Kaino that they disrupted a crucial balance, but they closed their ears to Kanhati, and he fell into despair. But in his sadness, Kanhati found the words to express all he felt, and he wrote a great poem about the way things should be. It fit on a tablet but had all the soul of all the oceans, and Kanhati returned to the Kaino to give them this one last gift he had for them as their patron. He first gave his poem to the leader of the Kaino, whose eyes glowed a terrible green and who was borne about his palace by servants who should not have been. The man read Kanhati’s poem. Then he set it down, and he wept, and he could not say why. The poem spread across the lands of the Kaino like a fire burning through dead wood, and everywhere it reached the Kaino repented. Kanhati was glad that the Kaino had seen the error of their ways, but he could not watch over a people who had done such vile things, not even a people redeemed. So he disappeared into the jungle, and the wails of the Kaino did not convince him to return. The Kaino went into the jungle in their sorrow, and they live there still. They live in peace with the animals and they kill nothing, all in the hopes that Kanhati will return to them.
