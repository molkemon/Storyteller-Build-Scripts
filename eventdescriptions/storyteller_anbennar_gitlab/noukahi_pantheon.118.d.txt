The monarch pauses to listen to a pair of griots who have set up in the town square. Their narration begins with a summary of the history of humans in Sarhal, but soon shifts to a debate. The first argues, ‘In the Lay of the IronHearted Soldier, we hear of his valor in battle, his many conquests, and his skill at arms, yet not of his rulership after the wars ended.’ The second replies, ‘Ah, but that is because his wife is the Flame Dancer, and her dancedrama relates their careful stewardship over the conquered lands, and her cunning in destroying the scheming mage who struck a deal with a Hag.’ The first counters, ‘Yet the Song of the Giantkiller emphasizes their cunning and wit in defeating a stronger foe, not the hospitality they showed to gain the magic hen.’ The second replies, ‘And again you are wrong, for the tale of the Warrior who climbed the Mountain of Glass and Fire shows that she only succeeded in claiming the fruit to heal the chief by humbly asking the three clans who lived on it for aid.’ They turn to face the monarch. ‘Perhaps the monarch can decide.’ ‘Yes perhaps. What is your opinion on the lesson we should learn from the First Story, Ardim’s travels from the north to these lands. Is it that a person must conquer to gain power? Or is the one who is generous and kind to their neighbors the one who will best emulate Ardim?’
