Sulish stood at the front of the gathered farmers as the procession came to a stop. He had not seen his brother in many years, and for a moment he forgot the picks and the mountain dust. "Sozyr." "Sulish." Sozyr stepped forward, and the brothers embraced. There were new scars on their scales that hadn't been there before. But the farmers behind Sulish were murmuring, and the miners behind Sozyr were watching with flat eyes, and so they stepped apart. "You lead them now," Sulish said. "I speak for them. There's a difference." Sozyr looked at his brother, and the farmers behind him. "We've come to talk about the chieftains," Sozyr said. "We said no to them. But we still need to eat, and you need metal for your tools, and neither of us is getting a fair price." He spread his hands. "So here we are." The murmuring grew. Sulish heard the words he expected. miners, cheat, stranger. He raised a hand and the farmers settled, reluctantly. "The miners have always kept the best of what comes out of the mountain," Sulish said. "Farmers have always kept the best of what grows in the valley. I know. I grew up there." Sozyr stepped closer, and he lowered his voice so that only his brother could hear. "They need each other up in the mines. The man who pulls the cart respects the man who swings the pick, because he's done it himself. It could work the same way between us. The chieftains take a cut of everything we sell or buy because we don't trust each other otherwise. What if we did? What if we spread across the land, and made sure we could survive on our own? What could we do then?" Sulish was quiet. Then one of the miners unslung a sickle and held it out to an old farmer near the front, hilt first. "We made a few of these on the road down. Figured we'd need something to talk with." The old farmer took it and turned it over in her clawed hands, testing the edge with her thumb. It was good work. Sulish looked at his brother. Sozyr looked back at him. "Alright," Sulish said. "Let's talk."
