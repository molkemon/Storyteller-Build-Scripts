The strife of the past centuries has unfortunately blocked access to one of the greatest wonders of the High Philosophy tradition; The Godswall. While most can no longer get there safely, there are, luckily for them, innumerable hand painted replicas within several different Brahmkalaas. The massive structure spans the gap between Maruvad and the Ascension Plateau, and contains innumerable examples of architecture from the High Gods and ancient human civilizations. It is widely understood that the bulk of this work was done by the High God of Artists, Pathim, thanks to a set of three carved slabs resting at the bottom right hand side of the monument. The first granite slab shows the cliffside as it once was; a beautiful wooden landscape defined by sheer rocky outcroppings. In the second, Pathim is shown at several different locations carving away stone and clearing foliage; working upon the cliff face as though it was a block of marble. Finally, the third carving shows The Godswall when Pathim finished the project. Statues carved from the earth line the cliffside as steep steps twist and turn between their forms. Below the image of the structure, seemingly within the earth itself, lies the High God herself in a meditative stance surrounded by lotus flowers. Interestingly, through a quick comparison, one can see that much has been added to the structure since the High God finished her project. new temples and footpaths dot the landscape, lotus gardens now adorn the various terraces, and statues in several different styles grace the cliff’s peak. To meditate on The Godswall is a truly impossible task, and many have devoted their life to the attempt. However, the construction of the monument is a different, much more tractable, matter to ponder. What does the ruler make of Pathim’s masterpiece?
