Look, over there. In the far corner of the crowd, where they thought this old jelli would not notice them or sing of them, are a young man and a young woman doing what young men and young women do. How fitting this is, that they give thanks to Mojobaa in this place where Mojobaa’s fires blaze hottest. I will sing you the tale of the mortar and pestle. It is a tale of Mojobaa the Great Forge, it is true, but it is just as much a tale of ourselves and our origins. In the days after the death of God, the genies ruled over heaven with an iron fist, and the land that once belonged to all the people became theirs alone. But we must give thanks for one thing. the genies needed servants and slaves, and so they kept us alive as their playthings. But there were not many of us, and the genies knew not how to make more. So they went to Mojobaa the Forge of God, who it was said could make anything. Mojobaa grieved his creator. But grief had not burned away his kindness from within him, and when the genies made their demand, he saw a crack of light in a dark place. The people suffered, but perhaps they need not suffer in every way. In the beginning, there were no men and women. the place between our legs was as smooth as the backs of our hands. So from one type of human, Mojobaa made two. he gave men their pestle and women their mortar, and he made it so they could find great happiness in each other. Something which the genies could never know. And this is why across the lands of Fangaula, when the men and women come of age, they dance around the fire and give thanks to Mojobaa the Forge of God. First as men, and then as women, but then they come together, for the whole is greater than each of its parts. Always remember. without a pestle a mortar is but a bowl, and without a mortar a pestle is but a stick. But together they can make the most wonderful things.
