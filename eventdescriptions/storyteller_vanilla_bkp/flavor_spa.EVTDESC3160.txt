In the southern parts of Spain, a group of people, mostly converts from Islam to Catholicism, lived. Many of these converts, however, secretly continued to practice their original faith. They sometimes provided information to the Ottoman Turks, which helped the Turks launch attacks on the Spanish coast. These converts, known as Moriscos, were persecuted by the Spanish Inquisition and faced strict laws. Despite this, they rose up in a violent rebellion in 1569, but were put down by Philip II with the help of Don John of Austria. The Moriscos had been thriving, contributing to Spanish agriculture, trade, and industries. However, in 1609, Philip III, influenced by the Duke of Lerma, ordered their expulsion from the kingdom, forcing them to leave with only what they could carry. Everything else was taken from them. This decision had a lasting impact on the Spanish economy for generations.
