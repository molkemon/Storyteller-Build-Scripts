John Knox became a prominent figure for Protestantism in Scotland during the 1540s. Initially, he accompanied George Wishart on his preaching tours, but Wishart was captured and handed over to Cardinal David Beaton in St Andrews. There, he was tried and burned at the stake in 1546 for spreading Protestant heresy. Three months later, Cardinal Beaton was murdered by Protestants in revenge. When John Knox arrived in St Andrews about a year later, he was persuaded by scholars at the university to preach in the town. In June 1547, French assistance arrived to fight the Protestants, and the castle of St Andrews surrendered on terms that were later broken. Knox and others were taken to the French galleys as slaves but were released 19 months later due to English intervention. However, he could not return to Scotland to continue his preaching and instead stayed under English protection, influencing the development of English Puritanism. He remained in England until Catholic Mary Tudor ascended the throne, prompting him to flee to the continent. There, he formulated his view that God-fearing leaders have the right and duty to resist a ruler who threatens true religion, if necessary by force. In 1559, he returned to Scotland and was amazed by the progress of the Reformed cause and the warm welcome he received from all classes in the community.
