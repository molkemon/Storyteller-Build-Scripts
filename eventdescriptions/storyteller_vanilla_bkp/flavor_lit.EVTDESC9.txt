During the 1630s and 1640s, the nobles found life in the kingdom unbearable. To escape the egalitarian notions of the government and the king, many of them began to interfere in local politics. Many of them owned lands on both sides of the border. They freely swapped lands with their Polish brothers, thus strengthening the cause in both Poland and Lithuania. During the Deluge, the nobles of the Ukraine and the Wild Fields fought everyone. Poland was in chaos, and the Lithuanian king had to accept that he could only control the northern part of his lands. At that point, King Alexander II mounted a huge campaign against the Tatars, Russians, and Crimeans, while also suppressing rebellious nobles. He chose not to interfere with the Polish government, calling King Wladyslaw IV of Poland 'the last sane mind on that side of the border.' The subjugated nobles had two options: they could either accept a tough reform or have all their lands confiscated and pushed over the border to Poland. The choice was easy. They stayed, but the reform was harsh. Only the state could now own land; the user only owned the right to use it. However, to keep that right, the user had to pay a user's fee decided by the king. With this policy, the king started to break up the large estate holdings of the nobles, changing the structure of society once again. His son continued the reform policy after the king was murdered by a Ukrainian noble on the 30th of March, 1654.
