After the two waves of refugees had arrived, the nobility again craved compensation for accepting that the religious status quo had been broken. His Majesty Mikolaj II Krzysztof, now an elder and a more experienced man, refused to comply with their threats. Instead, he used his right to push a proposal onto the floor of the parliament, which was a compromise, but one that suited the king. The rights to hold office were expanded, but were now also made a duty, and only the king had the right to appoint. The nobility was given a say in the making of foreign policy as a second house for non-noble members was instituted in the parliament, which held legislative power of taxation. In many ways, these reforms would decrease noble power even further as the fabric of society slowly tilted to the advantage of the middle class and free farmers.
