In 1809, Austria had been defeated and Prussia was under occupation. France was at war with England but had managed to make Spain a vassal. Russia was her ally, and everything looked perfect until 1812, when the Russians failed to uphold their obligations in the Continental Blockade against England as they had grown wary of French power. In June, France invaded Russia with more than half a million men. The Russians made a slow retreat to even the odds as the French army was shrinking from attrition. The Battle of Borodino started as a bloody struggle, but thanks to luck and a diversion attack by Marshal Ney, the Russian Army was annihilated. The ruler then decided to hold his Grand Army, spreading it out for winter. Supply lines were secured, and a smaller task force called L'Armee Baltique moved up occupying the Baltic coast up to St Petersburg, which it took by assault. When late spring came, the ruler again had more than half a million men in Russia and was advancing. During the winter, the ruler had restructured Poland under Poniatowski and recreated the Kingdom of Ukraine under Chielmkowski, easing the supply effort. On 30 June 1813, Russia surrendered, unwilling to let Moscow perish in flames. France was the master of Europe.
