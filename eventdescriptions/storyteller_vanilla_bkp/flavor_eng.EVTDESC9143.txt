In seventeen forty-five, the war of the Austrian Succession was raging in Europe. England had most of her troops busy in the Austrian Netherlands. The time seemed ripe for Charles Edward Stuart to boldly land in Scotland. He and his Jacobite supporters managed to beat an English army at Prestonpans on October second, seventeen forty-five, and entered Edinburgh where he had his father James recognized as King of Scotland. At the head of a small army, he then invaded England as far as Derby, but had to retreat when promised French support did not materialize. Caught up by the British army under Cumberland, he was soundly defeated at Culloden on sixteenth April, seventeen forty-six. This was the best shot the Stuarts ever got to reclaim the throne, but the rash young prince did not listen to the sober advice of his generals, thus forever burying the Stuart cause.
