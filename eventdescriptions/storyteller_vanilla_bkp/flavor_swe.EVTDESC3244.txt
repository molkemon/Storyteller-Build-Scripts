Charles the Eleventh was not a great warrior king like his father and his son, but he was a great reorganizer and consolidator. Apart from his efforts to centralize power and significantly reduce the land holdings of the aristocracy, he also reformed the army. Most importantly, he devised the 'indelningsverk', or military tenure system. The system was a military organization where the soldiers were given a plot of land to live on and cultivate, where the harvest served as their wages. Thus, the country could maintain a large army in both times of peace and of war. If a peasant assumed the responsibility of maintaining an enlisted rider, his horse and equipment, he was exempted from conscription and freed from paying taxes. The peasants could cooperate in groups of 25 farms and maintain a soldier or a sailor. The soldiers were organized into companies of 150 men, and every county should field a regiment of 1200 men. The new system also made no distinction between officers of noble birth and commoners.
