Campaign and the Issues, 1816: There were no major concerns. The current ruler was supported by two well-respected former rulers, Jefferson and Madison, and the ruler was a skilled leader who did very little campaigning. The election was a sure thing from the start as there was only one effective party, the Republicans. The Federalists existed, but were weakened from opposing the War of 1812 and had their supporters in the New England area. Their organization and structure were old and outdated, and they did not even officially nominate a candidate. However, they supported Rufus King who put up only token opposition against the Republican powerhouse. The Republicans, on the other hand, were a nationwide movement with strong bases in New York, Virginia, and the South, from which they campaigned with skill and effect. Four consecutive Republican presidential terms had strengthened the party, while the Federalists continued to decline. James Monroe won a landslide victory, although it was not as overwhelming as his victory in 1820, where he won all but one electoral vote. Electoral Votes: Monroe 183, King 34.
