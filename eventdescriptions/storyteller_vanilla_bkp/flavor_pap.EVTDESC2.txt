In 1540, Pope Paul the Third recognized the Jesuit Order, created 1534 by the Spaniard Don Iñigo López de Recalde, better known as Ignatius of Loyola. He becomes one of the most devoted counter-reformists and enemy number one of the Protestant movement. Loyola, having a soldier background, makes his mark on the order, which is organized almost as an army. High priority was given to educating missionaries as well as counselors for European royal courts. This made the Jesuit order very powerful in the political scene. In 1773, the Jesuit order was dissolved by Pope Pius the Sixth due to pressure from Christian countries in the age of enlightenment. In 1814, the Jesuit order was reinstated, however, just as a faint shadow of the powerful order it once had been.
