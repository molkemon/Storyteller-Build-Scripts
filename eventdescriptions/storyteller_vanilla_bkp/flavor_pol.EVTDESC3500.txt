In 1787, King Stanislaus proposed a deal to Catherine of Russia. Poland would assemble a significant army and assist Russia in her conflict with the Ottomans, and make strategic maneuvers against potential attacks from Prussia or Sweden. This plan seemed wise, as it would allow the king to have a military force that could protect the country from external and internal threats. However, Catherine was not convinced. She preferred to have Poland as a buffer zone between her and Prussia and Austria. As a result, the king was left without a plan and a decent foreign policy. The following year, members of the so-called 'Patriotic Party' took matters into their own hands during the Great Sejm. They increased the army's size and entrusted its control to a Sejm Commission. They also placed the conduct of foreign policy in the hands of another Sejm Commission and abolished the Permanent Council, which had governed the country since 1772. Additionally, they imposed a tax on income from lands, with a 10 percent rate for the nobility and a 20 percent rate for the Church. In 1791, the king returned from his isolation and, along with Stanislaw Potocki and Hugo Kollataj, drafted the New Constitution of May 3. This became the first written Constitution in Europe and a compromise between the republicanism of Potocki, the radicalism of Kollataj, and the monarchism of the king. However, Catherine the Great reacted once again, and as circumstances turned against Poland, a peace treaty was signed between Russia and the Ottomans. This left 89,000 seasoned Russian veterans facing 37,000 newly recruited Polish soldiers. Unfortunately, no one came to Poland's aid, and the outcome was disastrous. The effort to end stagnation ultimately led to the Second Partition of Poland.
