The hunt for the spiders of Gaweton grew long and cruel, for the queen of Alen was much unlike field or forest. Man-made trees housed malicious eyes, and the wingmaidens of Vængheim were little loved by the Gawedi. Even as the queen of Gaweton found few friends and many foes in this place. Weary of this, she said, "When this city fell under my wing, I saw it, but I did not know it. I did not then and I do not now, but were I to see with my eyes and hear with my ears, I would judge that it be burned and unmade, and that a great field be grown here, where men may toil the earth free of it." Yet all the same, Vængheim's eyes unwound the webs of Gaweton, and with each thread they picked at, spiders struck at them, but so too did the web come undone, until it seemed a frail thing, swaying in the wind. Godric and Cassan came to her and told her of a man named Welyam, who stood at the heart of this web. And she rose and flew to him with her host. And many men swore fealthy to Welyam, but their hearts belonged to his gold, and as the wingmaidens descended upon his hall, they broke and fled like mist before the sun. As she and her wingmaidens entered the hall of Welyam, they saw a man, bent with age. By a great window did he stand, and the wind tugged at his heavy cloak, clad in furs and gold. For he was no fighting man, and his best days were behind him.
