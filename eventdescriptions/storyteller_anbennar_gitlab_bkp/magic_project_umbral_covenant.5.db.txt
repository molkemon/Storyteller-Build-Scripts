The portents pointed us back to a part that stands for the whole. That's when I thought perhaps the zatsarya design was repeated elsewhere. Cheni's expression is triumphant as he indicates the table before him, where a complex little tower made of brass and wood stands. Deeply etched grooves cover its surface. In one moment, the entire system resembles the bendbark gutters of a lizardfolk outdoor bath, flowing to the base of the tower. In the next, it forms a many-branched tree, intersected at odd, harmonious points with metal. All the varied channels and rods meet at the bottom, where a set of brass pipes hang from slender threads.

"It is a device the naga use to hone precision," Cheni explains. "The device is essentially passive—the naga manipulate magic towards the top of the tower, the receivers channeling the magic to the bottom with enough deftness that the pipes stir and make music." Cheni demonstrates, ringing out a vaguely Raheni sounding melody that only the Vile One recognizes. "One shows their aptitude by playing skilful tunes."

Nobody speaks.

"It's what the zatsarya is doing, you see?" Cheni's eyes are bright, a victory the Vile One recognizes, a good little boy who has found the answer. Still, she must insist—“The point, Cheni?”

He nods rapidly. "The zatsarya is passive. It isn't a self-running system that siphons magic from the air. It's receiving the magic from elsewhere."

The Vile One frowns, processing the information. "And so you suggest...?"

Cheni grins. "Do what the naga do—send magic back through it, and see where it goes."
