Red dye, made from the petals of Corinspoppies ground into a fine powder, was thrown into the air, coating the grand table. Taking up most of the town’s center, the table had enough chairs for each and every villager; and a few extra, in case there were visitors. On top of the powdery dye, cloths were laid out; onto each and every cloth, every family in the village placed a homecooked meal to be shared amongst their neighbors. Some brought drinks; fine ales made from wheat or fermented milk or even Corinspoppies.  Each and every one took a seat at the table, and they all bowed their heads. The couter, with booming voice and righteous conviction, called out hymns; once the hymns were done, he led the whole village in song. With the end of the prayers, the villagers got up from their chairs, grabbed plates, and went dish by dish, each taking some.  It was Father’s Day, one of the most joyous days in Corinsfield, the day which Father Godwin first proclaimed the Corinspilgrims had found their new home. It was a day where absolutely no one was expected to do their work; in fact, doing so would have more than likely gotten a few sideeyes. It was a day when young men and women were expected to vanish, along with their beloved ones, and no one would complain if their clothes were stained with grass upon their return. It was a day for Corinsfielders to truly feel like a community. Any paranoia or terror could be left for tomorrow; for today, there was no suspicion or inquisition; only celebration.
