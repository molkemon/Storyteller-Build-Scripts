A wedding might not be a religious ceremony per se, but the witnessed signing of the marriage contract between a sovereign ruler and the heir apparent of Irrliam is an event of such vast significance that any other location but EduzFalanzar, the paramount temple of all of Irrliam, is out of the question. The high kasra Taelarios himself was set to bless the union, and a highranking member of the Order of Judges had travelled all the way from Azkabar to witness the occasion and officially certify the marriage contract.  The assembled worthies of both Irrliam and Birzartanses, as well as guests hailing both from vassal states and independent neighbouring realms were assembled beneath magnificent silk pavilions erected for this purposes on the grand plaza in front of the main temple, awaiting the arrival of the bridal procession with barely contained anticipation.  When the procession finally began its stately advance along the temple road towards the platform at the base of the temple ziggurat, where our heir, the groomtobe, the high kasra and judge awaited her arrival, it was a spectacular sight comparable only to those legendary wedding ceremonies told in bazaar tales and legends. Keladora, the resplendent queen bride, was clad in a sumptuous yet airy set of layered silken robes in shades of crimson, amaranth, scarlet and ruby, elaborately embroidered and ornamented with argent thread and gemstones. Our heir, the royal groom, matched her majestic opulence with a sheer silken long tunic of glistening white intricately adorned with gilded thread and a velvet cloak in shades of gold, pale yellow and citrine.  Once the ritual exchange of gifts to Surael and mutual dowries was completed, the judge officiated the signing of the marriage contract, negotiated in painstaking detail over the course of the past several months, and the marriage was blessed by high kasra Taelarios, who invoked the divine benediction of Surael Himself with tears of joy and pride visible on his face.  The wedding feast that followed the ceremony lasted for three whole days, and even beyond the palace gates, the common people of Medurubar joined in the celebrations, with public banquets and entertainment for all provided by the crown.
