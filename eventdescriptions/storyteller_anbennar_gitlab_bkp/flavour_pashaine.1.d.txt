How many in the history of Cannor have comforted themselves with the inevitability of imperfection? All in their position must compromise, all must give way and corrupt themselves eventually. Such thinking has allowed the conniving and the weak to slip in amongst the works of government, to undermine the good functioning of the state.  Put simply, the virtuous must aim for virtue – we who seek a new kind of society must resist the urge to indulge ourselves in our mistakes. This experiment, this Pashaine, is far too important. Recognising that the executive, holding a position of power, must be left free to do what must be done, we also recognise that they require restraint. Thus the virtues of our philosophy become the virtues of our state, informed by the myriad influences on our noble realm.
