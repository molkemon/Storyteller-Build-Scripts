When the first human tribes began to settle in Rahen, the holds of the dwarves were already old. Friendship and cooperation between the burgeoning human kingdoms and venerable dwarven clans led to the rapid rise of human civilization on the northern plains of the subcontinent, the kharunyana's banks transforming from jungle and prairie to cultivated fields in only a few short generations. Dwarven law, technology, and art were adapted by the early Raheni and mixed with their own religious beliefs and cultural practices. The dwarves, for their part, gained new markets for their goods, mercenaries to aid them in squabbles with subterranean monsters, and access to a vastly exapanded food supply. Though the relationship has not always been without squabbles, humans and dwarves began and continue a relationship of cooperation in Rahen to this day.  One uniquely human institution that never took hold among the dwarves is the Caste System. Even after their mighty empire declined and they were forced out of their holds, living alongside humans did little to crack the dwarves' stubborn streak on this matter. Though highly integrated into our society otherwise, the dwarves continued their clan based, nepotismridden customs, and were always viewed as an oddity for refusing to join the rational order of the castes.  As of today, that oddity is ended. With the signing of the Amendments of Rayavhashapal, the caste laws have been rewritten, and the dwarves formally folded into the superstructure of our society. Professions and bloodlines have been catalogued, wealth counted, and censuses taken, and each dwarf now has a place as solid in Rahen as a harimari or human. In this, the harmony of the Raj increases, expanded like the blossoms of a blooming lotus, to encompass ever more of the world.
