The High Kavalal of Rakkabuttai shook his head, as indignant as he was incredulous. "I can't believe we're even considering this! How long has it been since the Bloodcloaks were dispersed? How long since the garrison at Kannagarendi was dissolved? We've made peace with the Chendhyans around us—half of this lot have never even met a Yodhanpir of my city!"

A Durasyar delegate, whose ancestors were renowned for fighting the Gophirans, rose alongside his jaguar mount, a staunch and powerful Tydhzegaeda. "On the stones on which you walk, Chendhyan slaves were once carted in bondage! From the stables once rode blood-cloaked riders, terrorizers against our people! Only by returning these places of shame to us can the wrongs of the past be righted!"

More shouting, and arguing, and debating. This argument had been a fierce one, the conclave dominated by discussions of punishment and restitution, of vengeance and reconciliation. In Taychend's past, many empires had settled the steppe with frontier cities—the great ones, Oremvand, Rakkabuttai, and Kannagarendi (though the latter had fallen far since the Second Gophiran Empire)—were to many reminders of the bloody history of Taychendi-Chendhyan relations. The Kheionai's Empkeios, too, was targeted, though for now discussion of that city was one of intent, of future retribution.

But things have changed much since the Oren Nayiru arrived in the Guaraddhi. Taychendi and Chendhyan slaves alike have mixed with the old Yodhanpir warriors of Rakkabuttai, and the Thekvrystana merchants of Oremvand—more agreeable in the past, even though to their shame they had still trafficked non-Chendhyan slaves—had made notable efforts to make peace with their immediate Chendhyan neighbors. Do these places deserve to be punished for the fell deeds of their ancestors?
