A merchant watches as his cargo is unloaded, eager to see his work off and vote in the city election. A Fotistrepepi stands guard outside his general's chambers, safe in the knowledge that he shares in the grand designs of his officer's homeland. A philosopher debates another, stunned by arguments drawn from a millennium of strife and conflict, the wisdom of those who once stumbled. A landowner dines with a settler's family—note the way they look askance at his blonde hair, despite the fact that his skin is only a shade of blue away from theirs.

Since Laskaris led his soldiers into Taychend, Ambhen rule has relied on Taychendi collaborators. People who, even if they did not believe in Kheionisation, acquiesced to Ameion's rule, upheld its laws—then, as time went on, and they began to value security over cultural liberty, began to place their faith not in the gods, but in the elven mind, in reason. These collaborators began to wear Kheionai clothing, speak the Kheionai language, adopt Kheionai philosophy—and marry Kheionai women, granting their children the ruin-marked teal skin of their subjugators.

The latter isn't a requirement for joining the Klereyen yet—though there are those in the Synkletos who would like it to be. There are many Ambhen—settlers and officials, generals and administrators—who view this new Kheionised class with suspicion, questioning if they truly hold Ameion's ideals in their hearts, if they truly can become Kheionai. The Klereyen, however, already know the answer. They already give their all on the battlefield as Fotistrepepi, fill Ameion's coffers and granaries as landowners and merchants, vote in local elections as citizens of their Epadía. In every way, those who were once Taychendi have finally taken up a vital place in Ameion's Empire—whether the Ambhen like it or not.
