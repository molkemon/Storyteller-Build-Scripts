The transition was not gradual. One moment, our lord was trekking through the drylands of the coast, and the next, they stood before a wall of green. The humidity hit them first, a heavy, wet blanket of air that smelled of loam, sweet rot, and aggressive life. Craning their neck upwards, our lord grew dizzy; the trees did not merely grow towards the sky, they seemed to hold it up, their canopies lost in a verdant twilight miles above the forest floor. This was not a forest of wood and leaf, but an ancient organic cathedral, alive and terrifyingly sentient. Stepping onto the boat and travelling down the river was like stepping into the lungs of a god. Every inch of the Effelai crawled with activity. Vines shifted when not looked at directly; the buzzing of insects created a drone that vibrated in their very teeth. As our lord navigated the tributaries, searching for one of the Paruran trade platforms, they felt eyes upon them; not of beasts, but of the trees themselves. There was no silence here, only a cacophony of existence, resonating in the air. It was overwhelming, a sensory assault that threatened to drown the mind in pure noise. But our lord did not retreat. They finally found the Chahinanito people. While with them, partaking in their hunts and their rituals, they reached out with their spirit. Over time, the noise organized itself. The chaos became a rhythm. They could feel the song of the jungle, partake in her magical reagents and boons. It had been an exercise in harmony, in connection, in the raw mechanics of concentration and meditation. And when our lord returned home, those lessons would stay with them, and the song would continue to be sung.
