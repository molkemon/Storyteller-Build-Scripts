The light of the evening sun shines with a fiery glow on the many bronzeclad domes of the Temple of Ruby and Bronze, a most fitting image greeting our lord and their entourage after such a long, arduous journey. The vast plains of the savanna, bathed in the warm light of sunset, are sharply contrasted by the gloomy woods of the Khawit jungle just across the river, where night already holds sway and only the glow of fireflies paints the trees in a ghostly light. Approaching the gate set into the outermost wall protecting the sacred precinct, our lord notices a group of pilgrims patiently waiting to be granted entry into the holy temple of the Ujun Sisters. Among their number are a cattle trader, a widow and a warrior. While waiting in line, our lord hears their tales. The cattle trader has brought a dozen of her prized animals with her as a sacrifice, to thank Yaboke Alabaale Iyyodo for keeping her herds safe and wellnourished while others suffer from drought and famine. The widow carries her wedding bracelets with her as an offering to Oyuno Aboyuu Iyyayuu, hoping to entice the goddess to protect the children of her village from the plague that ravages her clan's lands. Meanwhile the warrior's offer to Dojawo Iru Iyyagba is nothing more or less than the next ten years of his life, spent as a temple guard in her service, to show his gratitude for her blessing which allowed him and his fellow warriors to keep gnollish raiders and human cattlethiefs far from his clan's lands. For such sacrifice lies at the heart of the faith found not just within the sacred halls of the Temple, but in the hearts and souls of the people roaming the vast savanna between Fangaula, Horashesh and Kheterata. To truly serve your community, your kin, clan and realm, you must be willing to give up something cherished, to offer something of yourself so that the greater whole may prosper.
