To Ameion and Rezankand, victory meant order. It meant safe roads and a calm society. It meant discipline in governance—it meant the bringing of civilization. To the Kheionai, this also meant obliteration—of bharbhen law, society, culture. The traditionalist Taychendi would have scoffed at this, of course. To them, victory meant strife and success in equal measure—the raising up of great cities, magnificent artworks, vainglorious heroes—and the tearing down of greater numbers of people. Of course, they would all compete in the same way, for the same Emperor.

But to Orenvalyam, what is victory? What is Dawn?

The only possible answer, of course, is that there is no one answer. In the Kheions, the Kavalali are elected by the masses, vetted for candidacy by a tribunal of impartial religious scholars (read: lawyers wearing different hats). Among the Devandics, the shamans espouse both the myriad paths to Dawn and some of their old ideas—Munazhlan is worshipped as one of Nayiru’s greatest champions. To the Advakhalla, the Kavalali are delegate priests, utterly beholden to their faithful flock in all manners except religious ones. In Sthanan ith Vussam and Nanru Nakar, their High Kavalali—ancient families, adopting new ways—are more like theocratic kings... but not tyrants, kept in rigorous check as they are by both their subjects and fellow Kavalali. In Uesrennu and Rakkabuttai, the most zealous soldiers return home to take the rites by popular acclamation and military experience, and return to lead their troops in the matchlock-and-mage banners that guard Orenvalyam’s advance, each with their own tradition, history, designs, and desires.

In Orenvalyam, the rays of Dawn shine in a million fantastic colours... some dimmer, some brighter, some agreeable, some downright strange, some perfectly sensible, some odd and self-contradictory. But they all shine.
