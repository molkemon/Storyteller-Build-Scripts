In recent years, a gifted administrator skilled in tax calculation and judgment has gained renown. In just three years, he has reorganized granaries, surveyed lands, repaired canals, and reformed labor and levy rules—each task meticulously overseen to completion. Grain yields have risen, rivers run clear, and accounts balance. Merchants and assemblymen praise him: "Ten years' work in three—truly a statesman." Some urge his methods be compiled into a new Administrative Canon; others see him as a model to solve the shortage of capable officials. Yet concerns grow. Power concentrated in his hands leaves little oversight; he commands appointments, levies, decrees, and judgments alone. One warns, "A man may nourish the realm; but if he becomes its lifeblood, who restrains him?" Another says, "When rule by man outweighs rule by law, statutes bend to one will." Shall his authority be embraced and spread, or curbed before excess takes root? The choice is yours.
