The dialects of Naamjyut are notoriously intricate—tones shift from province to province, and the melodies of children's songs differ even between neighboring forest realms. Every so often, the issue of "language unification" resurfaces in local governance—some regional advisors propose a standardized pronunciation system to improve administrative efficiency and coordination. Yet the elders always resist. They say: "If my mother's lullaby is sung in a different tune, it's no longer the song of my home." Must language be made uniform? Or should songs remain shaped by the mountain winds?
