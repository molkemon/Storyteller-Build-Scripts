Into the domain, three High Kavalali advanced. Bramashdar, of the Kamrayakvalists. Cara Lafquén, of the Kavalalists. Lokemeion, of the Monasticists. Some time after they had entered, the lords of the domain made their move. Vines sprouted, trees twisted, and the Kavalali were soon separated, entrapped within a judgement-court, a brilliant chorus, the seasons assembled. To each priest, a million different voices spoke, in swirling union: “We look out as dawn advances—” “And worry how your cause denounces!” “For even as you speak for day—” “For fire-flame your soldiers pray!” “Now answer us, these questions three—” “So we may see sincerity!” “What do you value most?” Bramashdar: “Liberty!” Lokemeion: “Peace!” Cara Lafquén: “Whatever the Kavalakuttam decides I must!” “Why do you spill blood?” Cara Lafquén: “For justice!” Bramashdar: “For vengeance!” Lokemeion: “Because we are fools, who do not know better!” “How can we trust you?” Lokemeion: “I hold no ill intent!” Cara Lafquén: “My peers guide my actions!” Bramashdar: “You can’t! If I had my way, I’d burn you to the ground!” At each answer, the Fey chorus chattered amongst itself, pixie against faerie, nymph against dryad. And at each difference, at every contradiction, the forest's song grew more discordant, more divided. The domain descended into delirium. It stayed there for hours, before the Kavalali realized they could not make up their minds. And so they gave their own chorus' pact: approved by the chamber, benches packed. They spoke as three, but dealt as one— “You have naught to fear, when we are done!” “For we are legally obligated to state: we cannot act without debate!” “Thus we offer you a shield—and where you forbid, we will not build!” “So let us preach, and let us proffer—We have many gifts that may fill your coffers!”
