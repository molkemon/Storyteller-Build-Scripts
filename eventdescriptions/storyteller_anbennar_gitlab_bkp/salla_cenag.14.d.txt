"So that," I pointed to the Sun, "is Surael. He isn’t an archfey. He isn’t the Sun God. He is actually the Sun."  The two brightly garbed, dark skinned "Sun Elves" nodded. My mind throbbed. No, it ached. This whole Sun Cult business was far more complicated than I had thought. There were so many rules and details. How could anyone figure this out?  "But what if it’s cloudy?" I asked, "Does that mean he’s busy?"  Lanhara nudged me in disapproval. Probably too direct a question, but I ignored her and watched as the two elves each consulted their own dusty texts. From what I gathered, it was something like our own compilation of fey rituals.  After some furious page turning, one answered, "If the sky over the temple remains dark for 64 hours, our priests need to sacrifice six pomegranat"  The other interrupted, "It’s 72 hours, Kelmonias."  Kelmonias shook his head and pointed to his own text.  "Are you doubting my readings again, Amarien?"  "Yes, in fact," she replied tartly. "Also, you didn’t mention the olives. The olives come first."  Kelmonias’ text slammed shut. "Oh, I get it now. You’ve not learned from the priests of EduzVacyn, yet. Surael help you."  "Hah. Groping for tradition again? If you traveled more, you’d’ve learned Taelarios’ recently stressed the importance of olives."  Powerlessly, I watched as the elves began to wrestle. They then screamed what were either random verses or crude curses. Something then came up about Jahar, Cinder Wars, and Chosenhood. I felt Lanhara pinch me and I was made aware of her seething gaze. Fine, I thought. I’ll do something.  "Uh… by Surael." I called out trying to grab their attention, "What are those?" The two elves both look toward me, murder in their eyes then toward the random jar I was pointing at.  They both looked and then straightened up.  "Lentils," they answered, their scuffle entirely forgotten.
