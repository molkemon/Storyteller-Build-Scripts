We have walked for several days, and our forward scouts have reported a remarkable change in scenery. The trees here slowly give way to giant, cylindrical monoliths of fungus, the blades of grass are supplanted by colonies of whitecapped mushrooms, and the verdant smell of flora is replaced by the unmistakeable stench of rot, creating a surreal and otherworldly atmosphere. It wasn't long until we had found the source of the scent of decay; hollowed out trunks of ancient trees, corpses of large jungle beasts, and the occasional bone denoting the resting place of one of our own; the remnants of those who had previously perished during their own pilgrimages, their bodies and possessions claimed by the mycological flora. It is evident that this land was a place where all things went to die, a graveyard for those who had succumbed to the relentless grasp of the Effelai.  Strangely, despite the eerie surroundings, this land seemed to be beyond the reach of our Goddess. Tswohvwoh'iii seems to have a bitter aversion to this place, making it an almost paradoxical sanctuary. The native inhabitants who reside here, the large and strangelyarmored La'i, are also surprisingly welcoming. They came to us with open arms and with an eagerness for trade, and some of our young ones have come away from the experience with a new affinity for the fungal hats so common in their culture. It was an encounter that filled us with a strange mix of emotionsrelief to find fellow tribespeople and a sense of unity, but also a nagging unease that The Tumor represented the final resting place for many who had embarked on the same journey. The Effelai is indeed a place of contradictions, where the line between life and death, friend and foe, has become increasingly blurred, and we are left to navigate this enigmatic realm with a mixture of trepidation and hope.
