Our ruler knew darkness then, but light returned to them, and they found sight and their wits, and they awoke, not in the house of the dead, but in their own hall, abed and in the grip of the dull pain of those who fought the cold and lived. Then the one-armed skald came to them, for he had heard of their wakening, and he said, "Yours is a curious house, o queen. And the songs of your people are stranger still. For my sister would have woken, and in her wrath, she would have ended me. And I did not think to find friends there, in the deep places of the earth." "I have done what I can for your host, and where my songs were found wanting, I have shared with your house what I know of mundane mending. We may meet again, for I have seen your house and found it fair."

Then the skald left, and Vængheim fell silent for some time, for many had been taken by the cold, and many hearths were struck dumb by their absence. Yet those who lived did in time recover, and they were honoured by kith and kin. And with the coming of spring, the hall of our ruler awakened, and shook from her mighty shoulders the chill of winter. And as the days grew long, the people of Vængheim rejoiced, and they drank from the warmth of the sun, as those dying of thirst would from the river.

Our ruler took the name "the Wingless," for the ice had bitten their wings deeply, and they would never fly again. Yet their reign was a blessed one, and they were dear to her people, who saw an earthbound monarch and found them fair.
