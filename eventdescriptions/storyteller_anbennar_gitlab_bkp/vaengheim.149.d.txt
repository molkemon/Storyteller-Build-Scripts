Then our lord did hunt, not for Grandmother Kraka, but for a battlefield. And they chose a glacier, and sent their wingmaidens with bow and arrow, to draw the trolls to them. Thus did the host of Kraka clash with the host of our lord on the glacier, and blood flowed so freely as to dye the ice red. Yet Kraka sang her evil songs, and great crows fell from the sky to peck at the eyes of Vængheim's host. And madness and fear gripped their hearts, and they fled the field in shame. And Kraka said, "Look at your flock, oh queen of Vængheim, and feast your eyes now, on the hag-queen of Gerudia. For I have taken the field, and I will take your throne and your people." But our lord, who alone stood on the red field, answered thusly, "Much have you taken from me, but I am yet queen of Gerudia, and I am generous. You will have more than you have asked for." Then our lord took flight, and their skalds, who were concealed in the mountains, began to sing a cruel song of breaking. And the glacier split in twain, and a hundred trolls fell into a hundred yawning mouths of ice. Last to fall was grandmother Kraka, who was light on her feet, yet was felled by our lord's spear, which fell upon her from the sky and pierced her, and she uttered a terrible howl, and fell to join her foul children in the crumbling glacier, and she was no more.
