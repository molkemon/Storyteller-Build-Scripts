Prominently displayed within most of the courthouses throughout the land is the judicial seal which features the hand of Dipar holding up a silver rod. As the ruler enters the courthouse, they stop to ponder the origin of the symbol. On the outskirts of the High Temple of Bubhagama, there is a small ruined stone structure within which is an immensely deep well. Around its border is an elaborate set of carvings which coils the well like a snake. These images depict a story about the great cleareyed Dipar, and how he passed judgment upon the shade known as Tuunkiichaaya.
Tuunkiichaaya is brought before a podium where they gesture wildly; perhaps pleading with an unseen audience. Dipar contemplates Tuunkiichaaya's words, but then, in the fourth panel, a great commotion erupts. Jaghut, the High God of artists, runs into the room carrying several stone sculptures and begins to argue with Dipar. The next 3 carvings expand in size in step with the energy of the argument; each new image larger than the last. As the conflict comes close to blows, Dipar holds up his right hand, and then gives Jaghut a small metallic rod. Jaghut then leaves the room, never to be seen for the rest of the tale.
In the last two panels Dipar levies a sentence for an unknown crime. Tuunkiichaaya is then sent into an all consuming darkness; as though they are falling into the neverending darkness of the well. With their memory refreshed, the ruler considers the artwork and its meaning.
