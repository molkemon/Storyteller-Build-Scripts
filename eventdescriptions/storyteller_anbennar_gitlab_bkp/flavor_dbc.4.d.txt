There is a storehouse to the west of Balanka-bo, and in it there are many gifts. Granny Ilfa brings her granddaughter there in the dim light of dusk. "You have a choice," Granny says, and the little girl glances at her, blinking. The little girl does not make choices very often. Understand, she does not crave them—her life is an endless, stimulating procession of new knowledge, new magic, and new understanding. Her beloved Granny, stern and demanding, nevertheless provides for every curiosity a child might possess. Not a day in the little girl's life has gone by without learning, and she does not fear boredom, even as she drills again and again a cantrip or a ritual or the history of a long-fallen kingdom. "This place contains things from your mother," Granny continues, her six long fingers waving lazily in the air. "We have a little free time, should you wish to take a look." A glance. "Otherwise, we will continue with your runecraft." 

The little girl's face goes very still. She has learned to read for moments such as this, moments when her good sense and studiousness can thoroughly please her Granny. Granny is her teacher, her mentor, her closest friend; on most days, Granny is the only other living being she sees. She schools her expression into a sneer of aristocratic disdain, shrugging—how strongly she resembles the aunts she will never meet! Is there a struggle beneath that little face; is there any conflict at all? Even a hag cannot tell, and it is this more than the decision itself which pleases her Granny. "No need," the little girl says, her eyes squarely on her work, her gaze cold. "Let's continue."
