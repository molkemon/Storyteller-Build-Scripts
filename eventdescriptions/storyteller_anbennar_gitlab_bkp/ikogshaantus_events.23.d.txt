You are negotiating with a chieftain from a distant province, in the company of a few officers from your realm. The topic of conversation has caused much tension in the room, for the chieftain has summoned you to admonish your government, specifically the officers, for conducting conversions among the pagan Khudi. "Why won't you listen?" exclaims the chieftain. "This belief is central to our people! Your repeated desecration of shrines and temples is an act of destruction, not labor – do you intend to destroy our people and settle our land as your nation did with the centaurs?"

The eyes of the officers fill with anger at the sacrilege. If you do nothing, they will surely have this chieftain arrested and replaced with a loyal puppet. Exasperated, the chieftain rises from his seat and bids you follow him into a back hallway. Expecting some kind of confrontation, the officers remain in the meeting hall.

Instead, you and the chieftain emerge into a large pentagonal atrium filled with religious artifacts—no doubt the heart of the palace complex. At the midpoint of the atrium lies a large geometric shrine, depicting a scene with spirits, rising to the arched ceiling. Straddling the walls of the atrium are various models of Khudi monuments, some of which you recognize.

"You see now," the chieftain's words shock you out of your observations. "Our peoples are not too dissimilar. We both recognize the value of good architecture!"

"How does this relate to anything? Stop playing!" Whatever trick he is playing, you want him to stop.

"What I am trying to say is that the relationship between our two realms does not need to be so unequal. I assure you, if you allow your missionaries to destroy our faith, you too will lose something great in value! Our belief in the great spirit is what has allowed us to construct all these wonders. If you allow us to keep it and tell your officers to desist, we will assist you with your great projects to the south. That is what I propose to you!"
