Since the initial status report on Pain Wards, a great number of variations on the ritual have been tested. The original version simply siphoned the life energy from the soul powering the ward to create a field of pain in a straightforward but inefficient manner. Iterations on the core concept quickly led to improvements in duration, but the most promising innovation came from another promising young necromancer, unburdened by Magisterial norms and propaganda. It was realized early on that the Pain Ward does not need to generate pain from nothing; it can simply project the pain that the soul powering it is already experiencing. However, this line of research was discarded because initial experiments showed that this projected pain was not sufficient to deter determined vermin.

The breakthrough lay in deliberately mangling the process of binding the soul to the granary, while still managing to succeed just barely. This significantly increased the pain suffered by the soul and projected by the ward. While this process does speed up the unraveling of the soul—and is thus usually undesirable, even for orc souls—it allows the wards to project the pain felt by such souls for two to three times longer than those siphoning life energy and turning it into pain.

While it will likely take decades before the ritual is considered perfected, the lead necromancers reckon that a few more years of testing and iteration should be sufficient to make the Pain Ward a viable option for widespread deployment. Of course, this timetable could be sped up with additional resources.
