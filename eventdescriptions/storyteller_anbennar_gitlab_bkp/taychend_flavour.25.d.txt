Our lord stumbled through the forest trail, clutching his sabre with white-knuckled hands. Blood was spattered across his armour—but it coated the edge of his sword as well. His bodyguards, Galinpan and Varamesh, were dead, but they did not die in vain. He slashed the beast across the back with his sword as it was mauling Galinpan, a sure hit, though it darted away before he could attack again. Injured but not mortally so—but the actual damage didn’t matter. He’d hit it, and that meant blood. His heart pounded as he searched the forest floor, moving from one blood-specked leaf to the next. It had to be cloaked, its fur invisible—but not untrackable, not anymore. The lord stopped, tracked the trail with his eyes but did not move. The blood drops grew heavier as his gaze followed them—he had to squint, but he could just make out a break in the light foliage that carved the forest floor. The brown of the dirt looked wet, darker than that beneath his own feet. Our lord silently thanked the gods for his good eyes. Now, he needed to pray that they granted him courage. The lord took a deep breath, raised his sabre, and charged with a cry.

He had only made it a few sprinted steps before the Dey Vettaga pounced from its cloaked position. With an ear-splitting roar, the forest in front of the lord was replaced with white fur and piercing blue eyes and terrible claws. The lord swung his sword with all the might he could muster. Slaver-Noble steel flashed—then the beast crashed into the warlord with enough force to barrel him over. When he finally came to his senses, his front was covered in blood—and when he pushed the Dey Vettaga’s corpse off him, he found the source, as a gaping hole stood where the beast’s head had once been. Our lord laughed as he collapsed from the exhaustion. The ghost in the hills had been slain.
