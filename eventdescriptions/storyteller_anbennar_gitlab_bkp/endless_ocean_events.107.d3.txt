The optimal location for a House of the Sea is at the junction of high tides, great winds, and strong currents. They are monuments to travel: boats and floating platforms meant to offer shelter to pilgrims with no specific destination in mind. Believers who chose to live in one of these domiciles find themselves carried where the current would have them: the whims of Moa test the seaborne’s ability to change with their circumstances. Perhaps these nomads will drift to the shores of a village in mourning, and their task will be to comfort the aggrieved. A different gust may strand them for some time on an isolated sandbar, and the resident may need to spend time studying the ways of the natural world in order to survive until the next leg of their journey. At any point, adherents may depart from the House; surely Moa will eventually bring the shelter to another, longing to begin a voyage of their own. As a result, there is no set group who embody the clergy; rather, membership is temporary and voluntary. Regardless of where these tests occur or who completes them, during a tenant’s stay, they accrue proof of their travels. Villages offer tokens of gratitude, skeletons of strange marine creatures, and sandblasted refuse– it’s custom to give some gift to a helpful pilgrim to facilitate their dedication to the will of Moa. Likewise, the expectation of tenants is to take souvenirs from natural places along their odyssey. Eventually though, even the most resident tenant departs from the House of the Sea. When they do, they leave behind all these trophies, and integrate them into the vessel. Over time, each House of the Sea becomes a unique amalgamation of driftwood repairs and forgotten idols– an utterly unique temple.
