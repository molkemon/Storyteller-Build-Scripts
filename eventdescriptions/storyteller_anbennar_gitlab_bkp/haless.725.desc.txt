In the borderlands between our country of North Yanshen and their realm of Shuvuush, hills roll across landscapes of forest and grass. This region has long served as both a frontier defense and a haven for migrants—a place of both cultivation and conflict. Ruins of old forts lie scattered, the flames of war extinguished, yet the land still waits for revival.

Recently, an elderly retired military settler in his sixties gathered old comrades and kin to reclaim a patch near a ruined border fortress. They dug irrigation canals, diverted a tributary of the Yanhe, and raised earthen dams and wooden bridges. Over ten families have now settled, bringing their own oxen and tools to build homes and till fields—a budding village in the making.

This has now caught the attention of local officers. Some praise their diligence in both defense and cultivation, hailing them as examples for frontier recovery. Others, however, warn of disorder if unofficial settlement continues without state sanction.

A border commander now petitions the court: should we recognize this act of reclamation, grant official registration, and award them state farmland as a reward for their self-driven frontier revival?
