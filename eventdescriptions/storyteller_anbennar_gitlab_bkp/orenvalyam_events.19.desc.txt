Today, the Kavalakuttam assembled on the plains of the Chendhya. There were thousands of Taychendi there, as well as many Devandics from the Guaraddhi – still, even with their soil-and-flame banners dancing in the wind, the assembled leaders of Taychend could not hope to match the display of the Chendhyans. Atop their jaguar mounts, they rode at the head of a great delegation that seemed an army, proud – equal, in a way they had never been to any Taychendi realm before. And today, this was not empty rhetoric. The Chendhyans, alongside delegations from the Mteibhar, steel-clad, and the Basobhar, dressed in Kheionai cloth, had come to negotiate their new place in the world, their power and their liberty within a new age. It would be tough talking. There would be difficult choices, necessary concessions, and barring an Intercession from Nayiru or Munakles themselves, not all the Devandics would bow to the results. To those assembling on the steppe, however, this was worth it – who could deny, after all, that the children of Munakles were the friends of freedom? That they had fought for Dawn, even if sometimes obscured, at times when no Taychendi had? 

The first day set the tone. After the pleasantries were exchanged, the Chendhyan tribal leaders, the Adezhegs, stepped forward with their first demand: that the Kavalakuttam would respect their nomadic way of life, and let the title of Adezheg be one set apart from that of Kavalali-priest – likewise, this would mean that High Kavalali would not be needed here. This would not be a deal-breaker if denied – after all, the transition to a priestly role was not such a difficult one for an already pious and strong Chendhyan leader. Still, the Chendhyans are proud of their historical ways of life.
