The mountains of Szitu stretch endlessly, their paths winding through the clouds. Since ancient times, this has been the land of sects and martial orders. The great schools of the Xianjie realm each claim their territory here, befriending through blade, debating through path—earning the land the name: "the realm of martial clans." Here, poetry does not follow rigid rules. Scholars draw from swordplay to compose their verses—in mountain wanderings or drunken debates, brush strokes fall into place. Their lines do not chase rhyme or couplets, nor cling to regulated tones. They seek only to express will and emotion—where thought leads, words follow. Some shout their grief, some proclaim joy; some lament the vast heavens, others swear upon life and death. These are called the "short verses" of the martial world.

Their poems are free yet ordered, rising like a sudden wind, running like a rainbow's arc. A single page may hold a lifetime of feeling; a few words can seal the heart. As they say in the martial world: "One who has no fixed form but holds true spirit—that is the true Xia."

This year's Xianjie poetry gathering was held atop a cliff. Disciples from many sects came to present their verses. Some wrote of parting from friends, others of honor repaid with bold action, others of secluded practice among lonely peaks. Their tones are proud, serene, solemn—each worthy of being passed down among the Jianghu.

Three such verses now rise to the forefront, all widely acclaimed.

But tell us—which of them best represents the spirit of a true Xia this year?
