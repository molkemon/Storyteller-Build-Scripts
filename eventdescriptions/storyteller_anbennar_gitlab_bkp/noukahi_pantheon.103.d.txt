Our lord comes across two men who block the path, irately arguing about a religious controversy. The eldest of them argues that the gods are just and fair, but they show no mercy to the undeserving, and that those who sin in this life will be punished eternally in the next. His younger counterpart argues that no temporal deed warrants eternal punishment, and as such all torment in the afterlife must be only temporary, a penitence to pass before transitioning to a pleasant eternal existence. Some from the retinue join the debate, supporting either position or offering one of their own. Eventually, with agreement between the arguers seeming impossible, the lord is asked to settle the dispute. They decides it's wise to reflect in the question before giving a verdict, and as such orders the men to set camp in the area, inviting the two debating men to be their guests for the night. Our lord has barely laid in bed when the eldest of the men bursts into the tent. He reveals that he and his young opponent bet a non insignificant sum on whether the lord would support their own side of the argument. He is, however, having doubts about whether his bet will ring true, and as such has decided to offer our lord half of the amount for supporting his side. The issue is, our lord had already settled in supporting him regardless, and now is placed in a moral conundrum. They cannot in good faith support a dishonest man, but it would also be dishonest to lie about their true opinion. On the other hand, being unable to offer an answer at all would reflect poorly on their position as ruler. What to do?
