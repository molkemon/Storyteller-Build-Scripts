An assembly of scientists has gathered in a small, white room adjoining the palace at our capital, accompanied by our monarch. Carefully, they inspect its dimensions: height, width, length. They measure the distance from the door to the opposite wall, then the distance from corner to corner. Mathematicians run the numbers, measurements predicted exact to within onehundredths of one percent. It is a small, white room. There is nothing in it. They impress on you this fact. What you are standing in is a small, white room. There is nothing in its nature that would seem to change this. Having made certain of this fact, all but one of them leaves. Our mages enter next. They take a moment to inspect the room. The room, they resolve, is perfect. It could seat several hundred, on a grand stage, with more than enough space for all the tricks of lights and mirrors that make performance great. The room is, if anything, a little too big. They relay this information to you. The room is a theatre. The theatre is grand. The theatre is, if anything, too big.
