The rift opens… and I can see. Hireet is an enormous, ludicrously so harimari, yet she is the only harimari these rural village elders have ever seen in person, and they have no reason to question her appearance. She explains, in her blunt terms, the awful truth. They can continue to observe the ancient regulations on Mengi slavery, the traditional Mogzeta system which raises the indentured above mere property, and they will lose. Their rival villages will grow fat and rich in comparison, and will eventually conquer them, making slaves of them all. Or, just like those fattened villages, they can embrace the practice of Shegde slavery: the trade of souls will be as mundane as that in silks. Hireet’s argument is irrefutable. It has the horrible, leaden feeling of a reality to which one simply must resolve oneself. She watches as each elder is crushed under it, watches as their resolve finally crumbles. This is what morality counts for when survival is at stake. Hireet’s tiger-smile spreads.
