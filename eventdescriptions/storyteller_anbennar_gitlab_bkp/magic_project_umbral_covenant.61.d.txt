Ikomeye invites them into her place of filth; the stench is overwhelming. The walls are daubed in blood, the few windows boarded, and the cracks in the boards sealed with tar. Ikomeye says it is sensory—she works best with disgust, with continually renewed rot, with the powerful sentient reflex to avoid the putrid and the fear of sheer darkness. Today, though, there is a lamp. Today she casts the portent bones.

Komina is incongruous; she is a beautiful Mengi woman, willowy, her hair a cloud of finger coils. Innumerable bangles on each forearm glint in the light. Were one not to know she was one of the Wise Three, one might expect aristocratic revulsion. Instead, Komina finds it all great fun; she hides her beady attention behind frivolity.

Ikomeye is in love with her in minutes, naturally. When she throws the bones, it seems to the Vile One that she puts rather too much theatricality into it. "The first portent is the clay figure," Ikomeye begins, her voice deep and resonant. "Malleable, unformed, unfaced, it will change only if and when your thumb presses deep into its flesh. The clay figure embodies potential; it is the terrible responsibility and the wonderful privilege of influence." Komina allows a faint smile.

"The second portent is the tree," Ikomeye continues, keeping her diviner's even tone; her face does not move. "The tree is the root, is the thing which emerges from the soil. Know our soil, and know who we are. We cannot escape the soil in which we grow."

Ikomeye clears her throat. The smoke from the hattiatia censer is thick and choking, and she sways a little as she sits. "The third portent is the vulture. It represents opportunism, whim, and fickle intent—the idle fancy of the powerful."

Komina eyes the Vile One. She asks, "which speaks to you most, Niece?" 

This is the Vile One's chance to connect with her Auntie, to win her over. She senses her answer matters to Komina.
