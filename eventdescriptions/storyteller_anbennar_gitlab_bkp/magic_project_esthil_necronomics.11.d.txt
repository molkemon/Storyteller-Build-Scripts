The first steps towards deploying Undecaying Zombies involved establishing a baseline for efficiency. Thus, a series of weak souls were used to power both the classical Animate Dead and a Death Ward just strong enough to produce the phenomenon. Generally, this reduced the time taken for the soul to unravel by about a third. This was far from negligible and, if it was the best that could be achieved, it might have questioned whether the increased strength was worth the reduced time of activity. Fortunately, this was merely done to set a baseline for future iterations.

To improve these results, various experiments were conducted, with most researchers falling into one of two camps. The first sought to create a ritual combining both the animation of the corpse and the creation of the ward, hoping that the two effects had resonances and redundancies that would lead to a combined ritual being less draining on the soul. The second camp observed that the fields created by basic Death Wards are spherical and that trying to change this would decrease efficiency. They proposed making humanoid zombies crouch perpetually, which would significantly reduce the area that the Death Ward needed to cover to stave off decay. While the sight of orc zombies crouching and crawling around was quite satisfying, their labor effectiveness was significantly reduced. A more promising solution was to distribute several smaller Death Wards at different parts of the body, reducing wasted coverage.

Fortunately, it was possible to combine the two approaches, resulting in a ritual that only reduces the expected duration of a soul by a tenth. The next stage is field testing, which would be simplified by a stipend to ensure cooperation from peasants.
