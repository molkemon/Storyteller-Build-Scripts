Day 14: They caught me crying over the journal, and confiscated it. Not to worry, I have traded for more paper and ink. The woman I traded with is named Kunya, and she is a hobgoblin herself, somewhat older than me. She tells me that we are being caged for our magical talents, because long ago the hobgoblin Shamans were tyrants who claimed to be scions of the Gods. A human named Himmat disagreed with her, saying that this is being done because of the recent Disarmament policy, as a way of "disarming" the mages. The more I meditate on our plight, the more unjust it seems. Why should I pay the price for tyrants who ruled centuries before? Why should I, who has never taken up arms against the Command, pay the price for other traitors?

Day 15: I decided to explore the camp; it will be my residence for the foreseeable future. The camp seems like a makeshift village, one of those military installations the hobgoblins live in. There are hard mats for sleeping on the floor, and although our valuables were taken away, there is enough space to keep a small garden and some animals. We are ten people in a tent, and three tents form a cluster. Around the whole perimeter, there is a wall, and the hobgoblins watch us carefully so that we don't escape. The guards are all women—I suppose they couldn't spare men for this task.

Day 16: Today, there was an escape attempt—a mage managed to break his chains open. Half-mad and full of rage, his magic conjured up storms of fire and wind that seemed to have no effect on the guards. Then he turned his attention to the roof of the shelter, and it caved in on two of them. Afterwards, they slew him, and now they are calling for reinforcements. I hear them through the thin walls: some of the women want to go back home to their families, while others were adamant about remaining to watch over us. Needless to say, the guards are not invincible. Our present arrangement is only temporary.
