When the Zatsarya ruptured, all four of them saw. Umorozi the General frowned; the pillar blew apart, great chunks of zatsargakh raining into the fog-gripped realm, and she was already stalking off to issue orders, to coordinate a response, to make sure no subjects of the Vile One trespassed upon the places where the strange stone landed. There was still a hope the stuff could be made again, its secrets unraveled for the fortresses of her mistress.

Ikomeye the Diviner grinned; she had come to know much of lizardfolk arrogance while studying the pillars, and had judged it much the same as that of her village elders so long ago, all now dead. Ikomeye was the first to realize the hatred this destruction would brew in every independent lizardfolk state on Halann, but she felt no fear—she exulted at the thought of their seething outrage, and had utter faith in her mistress.

Cheni the Apprentice gazed in awe; all moments of great magic stirred this in him, and he let the feeling of the magic, pulsing from so far away, wash over his skin and clothes. After a moment, he closed his eyes—he felt where the magic was going, up and away and out and partly down. Finally, it flowed into the solid core reservoir of the now destroyed pillar, where it might be of use to his mistress.

The Vile One merely stared—this was a failure, after all, the loss of a mystery. She had hoped to discover a secret her grandmother knew not, and had learned in the end only how to destroy it. But as her eyes fell upon the now-exposed magical core of the zatsarya, she remembered perhaps the greatest wisdom of the hags—how to remain patient, how to endure a setback, how to cultivate a resource out of an apparent loss.
