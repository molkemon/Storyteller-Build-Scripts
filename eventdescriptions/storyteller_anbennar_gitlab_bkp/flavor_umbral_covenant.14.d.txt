The Vile One meets the Coven of Yezel Mora in a place of secrecy. Mama Hireet is lambasting Her once more. It has been twenty minutes of the screaming—She wants to scream back, boast of the grandeur of Her realm, of the thing She has built, so much prettier and more ordered than Her Mama’s horde of savages. She imagines such an argument, Her Mama’s face bulging in rage, and for the first time in Her life, part of the Vile One thrills at the idea. The panic which follows quickly extinguishes the feeling—Her face does not move.

Next there is Granny. As the Coven and Covenant grow, the prospect of a final merging—the descent of shadow into this plane—becomes ever more real. It is the culmination of a millennium and a half of Granny’s planning, her foresight. It still fills her with awe, the span of her Granny’s mind, her cunning, her ability to conceal herself. Such control Granny Ilfa has, always.

And then there is Komina. How could She never have seen? Komina is different, always different. At summits like these, Auntie barely speaks. Her expeditions have the tone of assignments; she does not dwell, as Mama and Granny do, on the stuff of shadow itself. All three of the Coven have metamorphic tells, of course, parts of their nature which show regardless of transformation. Granny’s long, extra fingers. Hireet’s sheer size. But Komina’s, the Vile One suddenly realizes, is the only one to cause her real pain.

Some part of Her, the Vile One realizes, sought to join this Coven. Sought to make three into four, and stand beside them: Granny, Mother, Auntie, Daughter. Yet even Auntie bows to the other two, really, and look, for a brief moment, how Granny glances at Hireet as if suspicious.
