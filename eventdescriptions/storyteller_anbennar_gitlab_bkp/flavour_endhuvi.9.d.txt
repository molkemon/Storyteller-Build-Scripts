It had once been thought impossible, and to some, it still hadn't sunk in. So long had the great city been under the thrall of the gangs that such an antigovernment had become normal. Yet, today.today a harimari, shrouded in battered guard's armor, strolled down the street. He carried only a simple truncheon at his hip, a grizzled look on his face and a pipe between his lips. What years ago, amounted to a death warrant for any lawbringer in this city was now becoming commonplace. His footpaws gripped the cobbled street stones as he walked; he knew every one of them. A shopkeeper hawking his wares could be heard on the opposite side of the street, and carts trundled along, clattering on the stones.  He came to a stop at the end of the block, where the river marked the edge of the district. Before him, riverboats came and went from the vast array of small docks and piers that lined every meter of riverfront. In front of a house a stone's throw away, a pair of elderly harimari sat, hunched before a gameboard, muttering quietly to one another. The city breathed a great sigh of relief as life flowed into it once more; the guard quietly lit his pipe as he stood there, taking in the view.
