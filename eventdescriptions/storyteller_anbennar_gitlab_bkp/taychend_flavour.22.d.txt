Our lord stalked through the jungle, white-knuckled hands gripping his spear. Blood spattered his armor, and as his heartbeat pounded, the loudest sound in the still forest, a thousand regrets raced through his mind. He should have taken a larger hunting party. He should have brought those Chendhyans he saw on the way here. He should have taken a mage. He should have struck the beast when he had the chance—while it was devouring his bodyguard Galinpan. He should have kept a better grip on his sword—shouldn’t have dropped it. He should have retreated after Varamesh died. He should have never tried to hunt the beast at all. But it was too late for that now. Our lord was on his own, far from rescue, with a spear in his hand, bloodied armor, and a monster beyond comprehension on his tail. It all made sense now, at the end of everything. He was not hunting the ghost in the hills—it was hunting him. Our lord thought he heard a faint breath to his right. He snapped around, heart catching as he readied his spear—but there was nothing. There didn’t seem to be anything. But the beast was a ghost—it could hide in plain sight, pounce from anywhere. Our lord stepped forward slowly, carefully, one foot in front of the other. He brought his spear low and began to sweep before him, his eyes darting from place to place, hoping to see any sign of disturbance.

The ruler's gaze was turned to the left when he suddenly felt the end of his spear dip down. A heartbeat later, wood cracked, and the weight lifted. Our lord's eyes snapped back to the now broken spear—and the feline paw laid over the speartip on the ground. The warlord looked up to the Dey Vettaga's head, as it stared at him, its camouflaged coat replaced with a brilliant white, its piercing blue eyes narrowed, its teeth bared. It looked like it was smiling.
