In the far northeastern reaches of Yanshen, the people dwell along narrow strips of land, with mountains at their backs and ocean winds at their front. Old frontier ways taught that "mountains and rivers do not block kinship"—travelers often settled in villages, garrison farmers married locals, and records tell of Shuvuush women tending winter fires and Lakeborn merchants docking in spring. Their descendants shaped the region's "frontier norm." Today, Shuvuush riders roam the highlands, descendants of the Heavenly Kingdom farm by the lake, and treasure fleets trade year-round. The border remains active, and interethnic marriages—though now rarer—quietly persist in garrisons and villages, accepted under the saying: "each village keeps its own ways." But in one settlement, a cluster of such marriages recently drew public notice. As word spread, debate erupted. Some praise the practice as binding hearts and fostering border peace. Others, especially gentry voices, call for restraint to protect household order. More critical voices denounce it as a loss of propriety, warning: "if the heart flows like water, the levee will break."
