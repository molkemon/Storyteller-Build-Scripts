What a bounty we have before us today. Yams, meat, and bread: life is good in the lands of Fangaula. And it is only because we have such an ample feast that I will sing to you of hunger, and my words will remind you to show respect for all those who have starved.  The land was once stricken with drought. The people danced and prayed for rain, but the skies did not hear their pleas. The crops died, and the people began to die with them. Brother turned against brother, all for a mere scrap of food, and doors were bolted against neighbors.  Then the people thought to wake the Ujun Sisters, who were said to slumber in times of famine. They went to the plains and beat their drums and screamed until their voices broke, and the sound of their desperation echoed across the barren land.  They thought they had not been heard. They thought they had been abandoned. But then the last famished calfalmost too frail to standwobbled into the middle of their circle. It looked out at them with eyes full of wisdom and kindness, and it began to grow into a hale and healthy cow. One cow became two, two became four, four became eight.  The people ate their fill and were no longer starving. But they had lost more than food in those dark months. Women had lost the children they carried. New marriages had begotten no children at all. And now the second sister moved among them unseen, and those losses were undone: For every woman who wished for a child found one coming.  Then the rains came. Not the thin rains of an ordinary season, but rains that soaked the earth so deep that seeds pushed up through the mud, almost as if they were planted. Everywhere the people sowed, the land answered.  And for many years after, the grateful people ensured that the shrines to the Ujun Sisters overflowed with milk and meat.
