Thus we argue that the state is a series of relationships between powerholders, and the quality of the state derives from the quality of those powerholders. Casmil Vacq states that the state is a guild, an organization of members which makes promises and demands of those members, and whose goal is their protection and enrichment. Tomás of Vallusna suggests that the state is fundamentally a mirage, a thing which disappears as soon as we do not believe in it, a thing we must conceive exists, or we all get very scared indeed. Yael Estella describes it as a profusion of certainties and outfits and men too ugly for the stage, who nevertheless by dint of their willingness to suffer judgment are rendered terribly beautiful, and also, ultimately, something of a bore. Lucius síl Vallusna calls it a mantle one is called to, a moral demand. Edoro Meniá remarks that it is the saddest thing in the world, an indelible record of our failed ideals. Genaia Cadesna sina Bellacaire suggests it is a party we throw so we don’t all kill each other, and she thinks it is a choice.
