Caleb Danforth sat in the seat of the accused with the demeanor of one who did not fully understand what was happening. They had hauled him in from the furthest parts of the backcountry in the hopes that he might be able to shed light on the witchery of his wife, but he appeared to be in such a state of shock that the possibility of that occurring did not appear likely. The judge began, “Goodman Danforth, you stand accused of the aid and comfort of a known witch. If you have any information on the witchery of Eliza Danforth.” At the mention of the name Eliza, the accused began weeping. The judge waited for a while in the futile hope that he might volunteer some information beyond his snivelling. When the weeping finally stopped, the glassy film returned to the man’s eyes, and he began swaying back and forth gently. At this point it was obvious to everyone in the courtroom that the man was some manner of simpleton and that he simply could not withstand the rigors of justice, or indeed much of anything else ever again. The bailiffs led him away, for as it says in the Blessed Book.
