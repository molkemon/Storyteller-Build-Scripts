If nothing else, to believe in the Challenge of the Ship is to throw oneself ceaselessly into the boundaries of possibility; to scrape at the very boundary of the Kiamoa in preparation for the journey to the afterlife held beyond it. While ship racing has always been a favored pastime among the children of Insyaa, the sport reaches its apogee among the believers in the Challenge of the Ship. Here, the definition of sailors and ships approach their breaking points. For the short distance sprint races, the dancing gusttip kiteships almost entirely escape the confines of the sea, ever so barely skimming the surface of the water to reduce friction as much as possible. Their crews are scant, and the skills demanded to pilot such a vessel overlap more with the skills of a jockey or dancer than a typical sailor. By contrast, the epic islandships of the Kiamoa Round care little for speed as their fleets attempt to circle the continent without ever making landfall. One might argue that turning coral atolls into a sailing vessel might undermine the spirit of that mission, if not the letter, but the quartermaster reports of these archipelago armadas would prove no less impressive for the distinction, as each ship grows food on its hull to sustain the next leg of the journey. To undertake such an odyssey requires a keen sense for architecture and agriculture, skills also seldom employed by those with less faith in the Challenge of the Ship. Though if one searches for a conventional paragon of sailing, look no further than the rowing hulks of the Kiamoapushers—vast, sculpted sailors who fight monsoon winds with naught but their muscle and mettle. In their steadfast defiance of nature, they dip oars almost faster than the naked eye can process, overcoming the gale winds to press on clockwise across the continent. Their ships are flexible and durable—if not by design, then by attrition.
