There is an odd tale etched upon the garden wall in the High Temple of Sighajisbid. Unlike most High Temple stories, this one does not start with a depiction of one of the High Gods, or their many children, but instead a small red squirrel. The creature digs through the leaves and detritus around the roots of an oak tree, but despite his efforts he comes up empty handed. He looks up and watches a great ashen darkness cloak the heavens. Embers fall about him until finally, with no prize in hand he is forced to flee. The High God Vadham arrives some time later. The oak tree is reduced to a smoldering husk, and the land around him is in ruin. Yet, the squirrel survived the disaster and approaches Vadham, to which he replies by placing a bag upon the ground. The next tiles depicts the veritable feast which spills forth; seeds of all different kinds and varieties, most still protected inside their shells. The squirrel, of course, takes as many seeds as it can and flees into the surrounding countryside. At this, Vadham smiles and leaves. The final image is stretched out below and across all the others, depicts a new forest. It is covered in fresh growth and abundant wildlife. In the middle of the scene, Vadham sits on the stump of the burned oak as he looks over a small vegetable garden, and seems at peace. The lord stares at the replica of the mural and considers its meaning.
