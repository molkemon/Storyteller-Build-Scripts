The Rzentur elf stood watching through the barred window, hands clasped behind his back. The Kamrayakval looked around the now lounge-like cell. "You were warned," he said. And they were. When they allowed his old subjects to send gifts, the room was redecorated overnight. Rich furniture and banners and quilts of green cloth, emblazoned with bladed suns and sun orange trim. A message. "We make mistakes," the Kamrayakval said with a shrug, "and we learn from them." And so can he. Varlengielt turned, cracked a smile. "The stories filtered north, over the years. Such mighty mistakes. Such... excellent learning. The courtiers worried of you. Me?" He looked back over his homeland. "There could be worse empires. Subjugators." "Like the domain of the dragons?" The Kamrayakval offered. The dragon prince frowned. "Yes, like them. Do you think you look upon their ilk? Their legacy, unknown to me, hidden in the myths of a dead continent?" "To some it is not myth, but history." And many considered Varlengielt the last remnant of it – a tyrant by faith, not by iron. The dragon elven shape frowned. "Yes... they read of dragons, and so judge them. My people...?" He sighed, turned to one of the banners – a green dragon dancing with the sun. "Do you know how they named me?" The Kamrayakval shook his head. "In my youth, I used to raid the kitchens of the palace." A sly smile. "Nobody could stop me...." He raised his arms, flexed them as if they were wings outstretched, with jest and hidden might. "The cooks named me. Varlengielt. GREEN TERROR." The Kamrayakval couldn’t stop himself from laughing. The dragon prince turned, grinning. "They loved me. Shaped me. A god, see, does not rule over a realm of slaves, nor a domain of the dead. They rule over soul and heart, faith and conviction." And now, to his real point. He looked back to the banner. "A guide. A beacon. Isn't that how your priests lead? How you lead?" And turned back at last. "Do you really think I cannot do the same?"
