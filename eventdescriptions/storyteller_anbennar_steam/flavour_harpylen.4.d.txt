Courtly culture in the realm evolved significantly after the proclamation of Harpylen. While the Queen's Throne had always given legitimacy to whomever roosts upon it, the wealth flowing into the old capital saw the palace greatly expanded. The audience chamber was refurbished into a great circular hall, open to the sky, and decorated with ornate pillars upon which the Queen’s advisors could perch and offer counsel. Every pillar was adorned with a host of plumewoven banners, given as a symbol of fealty from the myriad flocks of Harpylen. In a concession to practicality, the hall also featured comfortable seating for wingless guests, primarily in the form of opulent pillows. The Harpylen Harem grew in accord with the throne, as the queen took spouses from her human and elven subjects. These spouses were invariably employed as ambassadors to and from their respective communities, and joining the Harem was widely seen as a means of social mobility. This naturally gave the Harem a great deal of political power, and while this influence was often satirized, it remained a pillar of Harpylen imperial culture. Running counter to the Harem was the Harpylen House of thought, which formed the administration. Unlike the Harem, this institution was almost entirely run by women and races unfit for the harem. A number of especially cerebral harpies also partook in the House of thought, but these were generally seen as odd hunters. Closely entwined but legally distinct was the Harpylen treasury, who innovated the widespread use of paper money in Harpylen, due to ease of transport in flight. The treasury shared administrative structure with the House of thought, but tended to secrecy, largely due to the enormous amount of gold they held in storage to guarantee the value of the currency.
