The Explorer feels something roll under his foot. Only that, and he is on the ground. A silent thing, this death. Panting and breathing and the grunt of exertion, then the gentle thud of a body hitting padded ground. Leaf litter and insects and fungi, a smell of decay that, even now, is strangely pleasant. Across the border between animal, insect, fungus and plant, curiosity replaces disgust. The feeling is mutual, of course. The beetles who will soon come to strip the flesh from the Explorer’s bones have nothing against him in particular. To them, his death too will smell sweet. The Explorer wishes he held the Relic – wishes he had made himself indispensable. It is barely anything, a little sphere intricately carved. He can imagine the blurb it will receive in some lesser exhibition hall of the vernissage. Ritual purposes, it will elegantly suggest. His colleagues are well ahead of him now; he can hear them making for the clearing, for the little port on the coast and the boredom of the ship passage home. He can’t quite bring himself to hate them. His foot has been sliced open to the bone. He is transfixed by the pulse of his own blood, up and out of the strange, fleshlipped gash. He tries to imagine the flow feeding the soil – tries to imagine something good coming of this. He does not try to stop the vines as they curl around his arms and legs; when the long length of thorn punctures his neck, he wonders if it is meant as a mercy.
