A bright moon, drenching the forest in light; just like the night of that strange patrol, the night Kavu had met Arya. Kavu looked up from the dwelling he had made in the canopy, here in the Flower Grove they had made home. A small place, just for him with his bed and a few shelves for his ranging gear, strewn between branches with rope and designs that made sure the weight distribution kept stable. He looked up at the night sky and the freedom it indicated. They had never expected any of this. what Escann had been like, so green and vast, and now what the Deepwoods were like, the astonishing profusion of varied life. Kavu himself had a long and varied life ahead of him, and yet he could not help but remember that woman, Arya. Unlike the others, she had seemed to view him as a person—an enemy, to be sure, but not just a monster to be killed without mercy. The reports of others in brief captivity described elves for whom the idea of bothering to ransom back orks was seen almost as a cruel joke or a brief humouring of monsters. “How thankful I must be,” he said aloud to the stars. “But why then must I feel these pangs…” Was it guilt, or something else? His feelings were strange, something like that which Gordak said people felt for those they loved.
