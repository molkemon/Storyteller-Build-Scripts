The soldiers tear through the tower, knocking over shelves, ripping open doors, and searching for any valuables. The ruler follows calmly behind, flanked by two guards. "What are we even searching for, my ruler?" One of the soldiers asked. "Something. Anything. Any evidence that this witch had allies, and who those allies are." The ruler. The ruler. A young, freshfaced soldier ran up. "We’ve found something; you have to see this." The soldier wheels around and starts running up the stairs of the tower, not even checking to see if the ruler is following. The ruler follows, charging past rooms of overturned bookshelves, rooms bare save for a single scorch mark, rooms of horrors the ruler would rather forget, vile experiments with no clear purpose, as if more for training than research. The ruler does not want to comprehend what crimes this woman would have committed had she won. Eventually, the ruler arrives at an untouched study, where Immarel must have spent long hours. "We found this," the soldier says, handing the ruler a leatherbound journal. The ruler cracks it open. The book contains pages upon pages of dense technical information, so advanced as to be indecipherable. It includes designs and schematics for some sort of device. It is smothered in notes and comments penned in Immarel’s hand. Her thoughts are piecemeal and hard to follow, but one point stands out: much of this knowledge is not Immarel’s own. "What does he mean by this?" "This should not work." "Needs clarification." "Proven by practice, not by theory." Annotations to that effect are everywhere. But if not Immarel’s, whose? The answer is found at the corner of a page dedicated to some incomprehensibly complex schematic: "Soul capture device. Preserves, projects, possesses. He calls it: Phylactery, the Prince’s impossibility. Yet to it I entrust my undying life." The ruler drops the journal in disgust. "The Prince." That can only mean one figure, one archfey.
