Do not mistake me; I do not mock your father’s wish. He hoped to put an end to the mischief and pain of the creature, close such a door forever. But the spirits are not some aberration, even ones such as this. They are a part of things, a part of a healthy world. We did not know what the elder intended, you see. We thought he simply wanted a little more coin from the merchant, that he painted his vases simply to make them look fancy. We thought it would do what any other spirit vase should do. put a limit, draw a boundary, here, and no more, like shooing the creature from a vegetable patch. Instead, the elder’s vase leapt at the creature, captured him completely. It tortured him, fed on him for the elder’s benefit. And such an act demanded terrible vengeance. Do not call my merchant stupid. So desperate was your father to undo his folly that he gave his life in our defense in the awful days that followed. The mistake follows the attempt, as the creature’s mischief lurks in the shadow of a beautiful glade. But not all amend their mistakes—your father did.
