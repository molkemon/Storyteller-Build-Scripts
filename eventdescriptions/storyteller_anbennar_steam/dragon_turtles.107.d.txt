As you sit where a ruler sits, you must consider exactly how your actions will impact realm stability. Making some enemies is unavoidable, but a ruler must be careful never to let their enemies band together, or ideally even know about one another. Consider the example of the Thousand Sails Confederacy. They proclaimed themselves in four hundred and seven, but the very nature of a confederacy allows for multiple centers of power, which allows for powerholders to emerge and begin working against consolidation. Consequently it was the case that the First Thousand Sails Confederacy of a domain was held together as much by its personalities and a kind of desperate idealism as by any kind of enduring structure, and I tried to tell them this at the time. They unsurprisingly collapsed sixty years later, but if they had just made some unifying reforms during the apogee of their political power, they could have lasted another sixty at least. And all of an empire’s history is made of these inflection points, where empires either endure or crumble. I told you about realm stability, but the great secret of stability is that it’s simply about enduring one crisis after another and ideally emerging in a stronger and more centralized state in the chaos. Maintain the loyalty of your smaller landholders with appropriate incentive structures, but seek to centralize away their power just as soon as you possibly can. Eventually you will hold all the power, and though this state of affairs never lasts, you can use it to ram through reforms that will convey you through the next crisis. This is how stability emerges, and this is the great secret of empires. With it, you will last millennia. In fact, the example of the Second Thousand Sails Confederacy may illustrate my point quite well.
