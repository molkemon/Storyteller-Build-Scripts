As it turns out, they don’t really seem to eat much at all. They are in parts curious and shy, dodging in close and then retreating at the slightest unexpected movement. Since we have set up camp in their territory, an expanse of three vast connected chambers, the bravest among them have arrived repeatedly to fix us with the clever, distant gaze of the wild hound. Despite being as tall as a human, they are skittish and fearful; was this bred into them by the Precursors? What fascinates us beyond the enormous vulpine dogs themselves are the prey they eat. Engineered little balls of fat and fluff which come up just below the human knee, these creatures reproduce with prodigious speed, just enough to support the hinuidingo population on what seems like very little food. The potential applications for deep sea travel alone are myriad, but the hinuidingos are protective of their food. Still, we are making progress; the big lugs only seem to do us real harm out of a sort of playfulness; perhaps Precursor experimentation never got quite as far as breeding the things for aggression. Once we have shown them that the samples we take will not meaningfully compromise their food supply, perhaps we can leave without any bloodshed at all.
