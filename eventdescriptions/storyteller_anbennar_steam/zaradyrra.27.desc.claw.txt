Captain, it strikes me that morale among the troops must be extremely high. While ostensibly the observation described a positive phenomenon, Nikzarak clocked the tone of her General as brimming with hostility, and consequently elected to play ignorant. How so, ma'am? Well, the regiments were so enthusiastic in their songs and battlecries during today’s march that they were mistaken for enemy troops. Friendly fire nearly broke out. I had not realized your soldiers were so thoroughly drilled in the art of performance. Nikzarak tried her best to reveal no expression at this news, even as she inwardly cursed. Nevertheless, silence wasn’t an option. I suppose so, ma'am. Suppose, do you? Their pageantry struck me as rather obvious. I’m sure every enemy regiment in forty furlongs overheard their war chants. And likely our movements. I will immediately see to correcting that error, General Zundara. It’s unacceptable conduct. Oh Captain, there’s no need to be so hasty. In fact, I would prefer to hear the song of your troops as you continue your march tonight. Come again? Your troops, Captain. I want them to sing as they march to a new position tonight, and thus draw attention away from the bulk of our forces. But why? We’re already encamped. I know permitting these excesses was inadvisable, but they’re merely trying to exert some control over themselves. And I cannot fault them for the desire, but we are at war and pragmatism demands greater self-discipline than they have exhibited. Through their frivolous displays, they’ve compromised the stealth of our entire advance, so they must take responsibility to restore some measure of safety. Or is there an alternative course of action you’d recommend to address this merrymaking, Captain? Do you find this lack of professionalism acceptable?
