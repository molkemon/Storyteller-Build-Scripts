Over the centuries, many traditions of the Ahati have become extremely rigid, and whilst some have now been enshrined in law, others have been put to rest as relics of a more tyrannical age. The ruler and the People’s Council see a more liberal Ahati order as a healthy development, though of course there has been resistance. Some senior figures have even left the order in recent years, though they are not numerous enough to cause trouble. At the moment, a significant point of contention is recruitment; while the Ahati have always been an admirably meritocratic organisation, they did and still do draw a hard line of preventing so-called monstrous races from joining. Gnolls, trolls, harpies, lizardfolk; all are barred, no matter their skills or character. As the council comes to the tail end of the reforms proposed for the Ahati Brotherhood, some ministers argue that opening up recruitment is the capstone change they need to bring the Ahati into a new season of greatness. Others, however, contend that such a drastic change at this stage will damage the fabric of the brotherhood irrevocably. In what many thought would be the last debate on the future of the Ahati, the monarch ended up with the tiebreaker vote, deciding that.
