A stranger comes to live among your people. Over half a decade, she becomes one of you; she learns the dance you do on that one special evening of the spring, the phrase you use when you wish to condemn something politely, that one dish you make when your friend is ill. A thousand moments of connection and contact, a relationship as much as a process. Imagine that this could be done in days, or weeks, with the equivalent effort of reading a pamphlet or wearing a hat. Perhaps your first response is that this would feel less meaningful; this is folly. When after only forty-eight hours your mechanic friend shakes their head and says, "no matter," in exactly the right tone, just like your grandmother did; you will double over laughing just the same. None among us wish to be strangers to each other, not truly.
