Ntwari moved deeper into the town, his eyes searching for his quarry, but he saw nothing besides his fellow tribesmates. They had come with a regiment of Ibekisedaran troops, attached as scouts to help hunt a warlock, one of Kuende’s acolytes who had fled BalankaBo with three score of soldiers. Some locals claimed that she and the soldiers had entered the town a week ago, and that none had left since, so the scouts had been sent in. The town was a charnel house; corpses carved with arcane symbols filled its streets, some posed in profane positions, others simply discarded in the dirt. The bodies were eerily pristine, lacking the stench that usually followed any corpse left in the wet heat of Bamaliwuni. It was as though they had been killed mere moments ago, as if they might breathe at any moment. As Ntwari and his warriors entered the market square, the day seemed to fall into night, shadows smothering the sun itself. The square itself was a mosaic of corpses, a vast undulating pattern of townsfolk and soldiers alike, each carved with bloody runes that pulsed with a dull red light. At the center of the nightmare knelt the warlock, crouched in a pool of blood before a single remaining soldier. Ntwari fought to move forward, to cut down the warlock and end this nightmarish ritual, the shadows resisting him with every step. But even as he went, the soldier leaned down toward the prostrated warlock and spoke, its voice a raspy tone unfit for its body. “I think not, child.” The warlock screamed; Ntwari did not understand why, until he saw that her skin was sloughing; she was melting, layer by layer, into the deep pool of blood. All the while the soldier watched her die, a last duty. As soon as she was gone, the soldier fell limp to the ground, leaving Ntwari and his warriors alone amidst a field of corpses.
