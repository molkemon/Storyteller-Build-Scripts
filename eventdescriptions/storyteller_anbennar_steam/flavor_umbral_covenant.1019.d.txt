The rift opens, and I can see. I have so rarely watched Granny teach others. She is exacting, of course, but not a drillmaster, not in the manner of Hireet. She paces the room, shielding it from the senses of others in the palace. I see the hatred in young Dogbeda’s face, the eagerness to learn, the insistence that he simply will not be overlooked. It reminds me startlingly of myself. Can one call this smithing? The magic in the air is so heavy that it pulls at my focus; I am impressed by Dogbeda’s skill, his singlemindedness, his mastery of the necromantic arts. And yet it is that singlemindedness which dooms him. He does not see it. Just as he binds his brother’s soul to that first blade, the blade for which he feels such boundless envy, so too does Dogbeda bind his own soul to the second. She has planned it this way, I see. These brothers' words, suffused in hatred for one another, will dance across ten generations of blood.
