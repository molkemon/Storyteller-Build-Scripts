When a leader dies, it is tradition that their subordinates come to pay respects. It is, the shamans say, the meaning of that sacred rite; lifted by the chants of their followers, the spirit of the departed might drift high enough to be caught by the wind, and rejoin the great spirit in the wide open sky. That is what makes the sparseness of the ruler's funeral such a stark sign. Absent are the subjugated, those who still reject the truth of the faith. Absent are their clansmen; did they lie when they knelt and swore fealty, or are they simply weaklings, following the vision of anyone with conviction? There are barely enough of us here, the loyal, to ensure that the ruler's place in the afterlife is secured; let us chant until our throats are hoarse, drum our hooves until our knees give out. Let us make such clamor that it reaches all corners of the land and fills the ears of those who think they can escape our wrath. For now, those of us who are here, who believe yet still in a vision that shines with gold, raise our voices and cry out.
