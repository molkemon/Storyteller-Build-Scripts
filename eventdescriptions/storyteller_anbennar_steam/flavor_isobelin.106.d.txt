Tower Street has never been a quiet place. Hardscrabble stall vendors hawk their wares, fervently believing that the louder they yell the more business they will draw. Buskers play lutes or flutes or sing for their supper. Wagon wheels creak under the weight of new goods fresh off the docks. One such commodity adds a new sound to the cacophony. the clink of chains, and the groans of captives chafing in their shackles. The marketgoers are momentarily silent seeing the first slaves ever to be brought to the Tower Street auction blocks, prodded by an overseer into pens shared with pigs and cattle. A child cries at the sight of the orks in chains. As if on cue, the market resumes its gritty symphony, as if nothing strange or tragic had just happened. Added to the chorus are the slippery aristocratic drawl of a merchant, bidding "ten crowns for the lot of brutes", followed by the squeak of a competitor raising five crowns more. For the orks standing silently amidst the noise, their proud warcries and boisterous songs around the campfire are a distant memory, their home back in their domain as out of reach as a dream upon waking. One more dream in a city full of them.
