The Vile One works for thirteen days. The physical exertion of the rite is itself a balm; She pours her rage and grief into the movements, summoning and dismissing shifts of incanters to aid her work. Umorozi’s body helps; it is solid, memorial, something to reference, something to touch. Five times the Vile One wipes a strange drop of saltwater from Umorozi’s stomach before She realises it must be a tear. She seizes Umorozi’s spirit, yanks it back. There are arts, She knows, of fleshcraft and growth, wet with blood. What is done here will not be so crude or grotesque. The wound in the stomach She closes in shadow; the breath of life is also returned in shadow. Hours pass. Then, Umorozi says, “Kitame?” The Vile One exhales, huffs relief; it is the endearment only three people have ever used for Her. More saltwater drops appear on Umorozi’s torso, heedless. The drops trickle down towards Umorozi’s sealed wound; now a line of greyblack fog, a strange unthing which confuses the eye. When the drops pass over the wound, they are swallowed completely.
