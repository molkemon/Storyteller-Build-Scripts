In the hills and valleys of northwestern North Yanshen, fortresses and mountain towns dot the rugged terrain. Isolated and hard to reinforce, the locals have long developed a habit of selfreliance. Martial gatherings are a common tradition. at seasonal festivals, villagers compete in bouts held before temple courtyards or inside walled compounds. These duels are not simply tests of strength but follow inherited codes of respect and technique, bowing before each match and ceasing before injury. Lineages are preserved through master-disciple registers, earning this region the name "Crucible of the North." Recently, a routine meet was disrupted by a military officer, claiming unauthorized assembly and disrupting order. In the resulting scuffle, a young martial artist was injured, nearly inciting a riot. Since then, unrest has stirred among the fortress towns. The elder master who led the gathering has submitted a petition, asking for official recognition of these martial societies. He proposes a registry system and oversight to integrate them under state rule, preserving traditions while preventing disorder. Shall we legitimize this legacy of blades and discipline?
