The throne room seems to glow in the morning sun, light reflecting off the sheets of polished copper and gold that plate every surface. At the center of it all sits the ruler, seated upon the throne, robes glistening almost as brightly as the metal. The throne room itself is more a temple than a hall, its grand domed ceiling baring the room to the elements, a statement of confidence in the power of the city’s rainpriests to avoid any unplanned showers. The supplicants are admitted in small groups, and one by one they step forward to plead their cases. In the quiet of the throne room their voices are small, the sound carrying well but nonetheless smothered by the enormity of their surroundings. When the Mezhi responds, the ruler's voice is full and echoing, amplified by the meticulous arrangement of those same copper panels, and easily fills the space in stark contrast to the petitioners. Listening to the judgment passed by the glowing figure upon the throne, their voice echoing from all around, each seeker of the Getametaki’s grace understands that the Lord Below is truly the first below Amilak, a figure touched by the divine, and that to have experienced such a presence is a blessing they shall treasure for the rest of their lives.
