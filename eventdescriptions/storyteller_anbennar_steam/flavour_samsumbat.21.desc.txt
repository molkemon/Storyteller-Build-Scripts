The sound of drumming filled the air as hundreds of festivalgoers danced, laughed, and ate. The monarch thought, "These Taafi certainly know how to celebrate, and it's certainly the time for it." The freedom of their cousins was a dream of generations of Taafi that had now been realised, and the monarch, as Steward of the Sorrow and Liberator, was the guest of honour. At some point the ruler made a short speech about us all being one family, but it's hard to say who was really listening. The ruler didn't mind. There were displays of strength and agility, cooking contests as people competed to come up with the most elaborate dish, and drumming; lots and lots of drumming. The Taafi Chief announced early in the evening that they were feting the monarch as the avatar of their god Makan who had come to make their people free and happy. Someone had to quietly explain to the monarch that Makan was most often depicted playing drums, hence the overabundance of percussion. The celebrations went long into the night; a microcosm of Sarhal at large, the monarch thought. Liberty has come, and the people react with joy, knowing their children will grow up in a brighter world.
