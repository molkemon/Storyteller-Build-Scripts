A pair of elbows desperately dig in against the stained mud, and the ragged sound of uneven heaving is only interrupted by the occasional thrumming pluck piercing through the night rain. Amidst a foul pile of offal, a massive, contorted Ashhana fights full-body convulsions while their tail twists in agony. A guttural crocodilian hiss escapes their maw as their teeth attempt to refrain from nipping at the carcasses surrounding them. Pink mucus drips from the end of their lacerated snout while thick fluid trickles from their bloodshot eyes. Just as the convulsions threaten to overrun the prone lizardfolk, a single clawed finger caresses a length of sinew, and pulls. A sharp sound. It could not be called an instrument. It could hardly be called music at all. Yet for a moment in the rain, the Ashhana’s convulsions subside as a single note cuts through the dark. Whatever reprieve could be found from the madness depended on this single, fragile string. A sharp sound. What a miserable savior, some soggy piece of tendon. How long can it withstand the force of its harper? When this sorry ligament snaps, will the musician as well? A sharp sound. When the song ends, how does it restart? How does the rage abate?
