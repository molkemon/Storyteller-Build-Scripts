It was a curious order. Jadu, the shipwright, had fashioned many vessels in his time, all splendid, sturdy craft that plied their trade up and down the great river. But the order currently sitting on his desk was a first, even for him. He had to ask the harimari who delivered it to repeat his request at least twice, and he had outlined the costs and work involved with such an endeavor as clearly as he possibly could. All the same, he had taken the contract; in a few months time, everyone would be treated to a sight as perplexing as it was impressive. Two months later, a building floated down the great river, guided from the shoreline by teams of elephants and their handlers. A great barge bore the structure, the widest barge the river had seen yet, spanning nearly shore to shore at the Kharunyana's narrowest points. The building's roof was plain wood, though a large dullred lotus flower was proudly displayed above each of the four doors. A promenade ran around the entire perimeter of the hall, wide enough for three abreast to stroll comfortably, with seating beneath the large roof awning. The barge lumbered its way down the river until finally bumping comfortably into posts sunk along the shore, dwarfing the former meeting hall beside which it had moored. Ropes were lashed, and a gangplank set in place. Teams had scarcely begun detaching the elephants before the eager philosophers began climbing aboard. The Cinnabar Lotus Society's new debate hall was open for business.
