I find her absurd, master. Explain. I understand the wisdom in seeking to excel at one’s tasks; that is the cultivation of the self, of one’s inner chi. But to make no use of any of it in the end… one reads of such physical activities hiding a secret movement in battle, and so on, but Wen never went to war. She shied away from battle and then died anyway. But she perfected the sweeping, didn’t she? So? To what end? Well… her chi, no? The building of her chi? But she didn’t do anything with it. She withstood the pressure of others; she offered an example of pacifism and peace, apparently important to her. I don’t think she was a coward at all; three times she had to speak against her whole temple. She whittled her chi as she saw fit, pursued her goals with determination. What goals? I think the point is that they remain esoteric to us. The timid student’s voice quavers, but she holds her ground. We need not understand to what purpose she carved her chi, merely admire the totality and discipline with which she carved it. I think. I don’t see anything to admire in what she did. Even her students rejected her in the end. Are we students always wise? Do we grasp everything? We have eyes like anyone else.
