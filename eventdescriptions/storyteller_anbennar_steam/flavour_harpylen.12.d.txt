As Harpylen grew, so did her ambition, and many of her children heard the glory of the song today, yet found in it an echo of the song of the past. In this manner, many realms were taken by fervent debate. The topic? Which was the song, and which was the echo? Were the deeds of today echoes of glories old? Or were glories old simple echoes of this new golden age that had enveloped the Queendom? Nowhere was this debate as lively as in Heunthume, and even the Queen's Throne heard this question, sung by her many children far below. The throne took no stance of course, but called to the Ivetasananthor, who had hoarded many songs from the past. A question was sung, an envoy returned, an answer was given. For it was true that the song of the past was strong, stronger perhaps than the song of today. But this strength lay in the tools of that age. One word was sung over and over, by harpy and human both, and the word was "Kuresim". Kuresim was the art of the wish, the greatest magic of the ancient world. It was wielded by the sedu, the djinn rulers of ancient Bulwar. With it, it was sung that they had shaped the very land of their domain, changing simply by the power of their word, the very nature of that which was. But where the sedu had vanished, their craft had remained, and the greatest of these tools were the Menumsar, Kuresim bound in forms of matter. Their method of creation was impossible, their powers beyond imagining. And according to the old songs, our August ancestor, Queen Firanya, had been the owner of one such treasure. It bore the name Asmalil, yet nothing else was known of it. So did the Queen's Throne, the Heunthokharivor and the Isutoqir come together in song, and a choice was made. Asmalil would be found, for it was our legacy and our future. And if the song of today were to rise above the song of yesterday, Harpylen would need tools equal to this task.
