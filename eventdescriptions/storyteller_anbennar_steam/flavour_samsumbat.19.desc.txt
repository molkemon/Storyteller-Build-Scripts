The crowd stood silent. Rumours had been swirling for days that the delegates had come to the end of their deliberations. The servants who moved to and from the house had not said anything to outsiders for a week, obviously sworn to secrecy, but this served to only pique everyone's interest further. On this day, the streets were packed for several blocks as everyone with time to spare from the day's labour awaited the announcement of their new constitution. An hour later, the big doors at the front ponderously opened. The murmuring stopped. Inorkan, Minister for Infrastructure, well known in the city for his tireless advocacy for public works, stepped forth. Behind him, another dozen councillors came to occupy the threshold. He pulled a sheaf of papers from his satchel, and raised his voice: "Citizens of this realm. Today, in the current year and month, it is decreed that a new constitution be enacted. By the authority of the ruler, rightful Steward of the Sorrow, the People's Council, and the House of Equals, let the first constitution be rendered void, acknowledging the good purpose it has served us all. By that same authority, let the new constitution come into force." With that, he fell silent, and turned back inside to organise the other delegates to return to their usual occupations. Some in the crowd whispered to their companions, asking what the big deal was with a piece of paper. From various small gatherings, though, a great cheer rose up, and it caught on and spread like wildfire through the crowd. Many understood what this meant: A great step forward for the Republic, a binding document that properly enshrined their paramount rights and freedoms, fulfilling the dream of Maracout for every citizen in the prosperous years to come.
