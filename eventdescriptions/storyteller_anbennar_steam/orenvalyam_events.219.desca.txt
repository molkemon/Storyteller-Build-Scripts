Nobody saw the ruler die, nor knew how it had happened. He had been fighting on the frontlines for a century; one of the wounds he had received was bound to kill him eventually. They found him watching the sky through one of the windows of his palace, the collar of his armor fixed around his neck. A click, and it came off, so easily. Immediately, panic began. His loyal and fanatical officers, not the High Kavalali, of course, whose remaining number had been thoroughly beaten into submission by the military that ostensibly served them, were the ones to reestablish order. It was to some surprise, then, that when they opened the ruler’s will, they discovered it was not actually their job to. A leader had ordered the armor secured until it was needed once more, and the Kavalakuttam’s normal functions restored, and the most hardened policies of his reign—the mass executions, the uncompromising redistribution, and harrowing retribution—set aside. There was a caveat to all of it, of course: when the invasion of Halcann came, when dawn finally swept over the rest of the world, all of it would be needed again. There was a muted surprise, at first. Some of the officers thought the words were forged, but the old members of the ruler’s Company knew his handwriting well, and it was not. They all understood eventually, though. When dawn came at last, they could not live forever as if they were in darkness. And besides, the ruler was not a fool. He knew the military could keep their relevance no matter what. He knew his people would not shy away from what needed to be done. He knew that he had made his mark. That his actions had inflamed the destiny of Halann.
