The monarch looked across the landscape in disbelief as they wandered aimlessly. The voices around them went in one ear and out the other. The ruler had been at the forefront of the conflict, at the wall of flame that had put the Effelai to flight and then slain it. For months, the monarch had lived in a world of smoke-choked skies and roaring fires, bloodied vines and mud-soaked steel. The war against the Effelai was fought in darkness, but now, they saw its aftermath in the light of day, dimmed, of course. It still snowed ash. Before the war, the Effelai had been a forest of tall canopies, webs of vine and silk and exotic plants and flowers, the green of the leaves fighting with red and pink and blue for dominance. Now, the ground was covered in a field of light grey, ash carpeting the soil like snow. And with the towering trees that had once blocked sun and sight gone, replaced with twisted, charred, hulking husks, it was as if the whole of the Effelai was laid out before him. A forest of blackened stumps, each a tombstone to the singing jungle, that went on for miles and miles and miles. Something caught the monarch’s eye. The monarch halted, looking down at their feet. A green shoot sprouted through the ash. “The Effelai…” the monarch asked, as a chill ran down their spine. “It’s gone? Completely?” The warlord to their side raised an eyebrow. “That’s… what I was telling you about, sir.” The monarch just stared at him blankly, and the warlord started again. “We believe it has been crippled. Any above ground presence has been incinerated, but the Jungle’s roots run miles underground in some cases. Whatever we find we burn, but…” The monarch laughed. All this, and it still persisted. They had burned the world, burned half the continent, and it still survived. The monarch shook their head. “No, no—we’ve had enough burning. If you find any remnants, separate them, stem their growth, but keep them alive. We may have defeated the Effelai…”
