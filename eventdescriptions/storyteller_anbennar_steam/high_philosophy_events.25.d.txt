The current conflict has been taking a toll upon the monarch, and after much pestering from their advisors, the ruler has decided to take a step back for the day and try to relax. They have chosen, on the advice of a philosopher, to spend this time in meditation. Procuring a Brahmkalaas from the ruler's private collection, the monarch sits down in the morning light and turns to a collection of painted replicas from the Godswall. The set of replicated images depict the High God Anjita’s journey to save his daughters Tikhaaphula after they are captured by the black dragon Hakataamadiihai, which is another tale unto itself. After a number of trials and tribulations in the surrounding hillside, his quest leads him into the depths of a great mountain. Entering into a cave, the land begins to slope into the soupy darkness. As Anjita’s chariot rushes forward, the High God battles what seems to be the shadows themselves, and is only able to prevail with the aid of a blue torch. After escaping the shades, he reaches the bottom and enters into the lair of the beast. A great battle erupts between the two as Anjita charges his chariot into clouds of green smoke that smothers all life they touch. The conflict stretches out over three paintings, ending only when Anjita, after patiently waiting for an opening, pierces the dragon’s belly with a spear of pure light fashioned by the High God Gunjit. The monarch finishes looking through the painted replicas, and then, sipping from a cup of tea, ponders the story’s meaning in relation to the ruler's current conflict.
