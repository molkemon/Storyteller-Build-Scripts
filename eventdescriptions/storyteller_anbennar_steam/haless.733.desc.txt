In recent days, a breeze of invention has stirred once more along the Yanshen Coast. Inspired by coastal night rituals, some have crafted airborne beacons from whaleoil heat bladders and lightweight wooden frames. Others, salvaging discarded ship planks, have dried and blended seaweed with softshore grass into fibers in an attempt to weave a new fabric—light, flexible, though not yet durable—that has already sparked interest in the small markets along the shore. Inland, village carpenters have been tinkering with old mining bellows and stream pulleys, gradually devising a prototype pressurespray rotor in hopes of using water power to propel simple wooden boats upstream. Though these ideas are far from fully realized, they have already stirred lively discussion among workshops and societies across the Yanshen Coast. Lanternmakers boast of height, weavers praise translucence, and carpenters argue over rotation speed—yet all say the same. "The craft may be unshaped, but the intent is clear." Technique moves like the wind, and its sparks are slowly lighting the hills and shores. Such is often the way of Ganggim folk—every so often, they spark bold new dreams in the realm of outward exploration.
