Years and years ago, the Hinuiplants were simply something to be feared. The communities bordering the Hinuiplants erected great barriers of salt to protect against their growth, while they themselves sought to coax out meager growths from meager plants, which were weak and illsuited to feed anyone, but at least were trustworthy. Into one of these villages was born the girl then known as Lilinoe. She saw the potential of the Hinuiplants, and one day she crept beyond the barrier of salt to retrieve the saplings of the Hinuiplants, which she brought back home to grow. Her father who worked the salt was furious. Her mother who worked the soil was afraid. But Lilinoe grew those plants with the ambition and wisdom of an empress, and soon her empire of domesticated Hinuiplants stretched out before her. Then she began selling the plants to the villages beyond, and her empire stretched across islands. One fateful voyage, Lilinoe went on an expedition to the Taga’Ala mangroves, where she sought to spread more of her wondrous and lifegiving plants. A kraken attacked the ship, and all aboard would have died were it not for Lilinoe, who knew just as much of plants of death as she did of plants of life. She withdrew a vine from her pocket, and it grew up to the kraken. The vine thrived on its blood, and toppled the dying kraken from the ship into the open sea. The ship made it to the Taga’Ala mangroves, and then home again. And when the sailors stumbled back to their homes with thankful legs, the one they praised for their salvation was Waiko’lilinoe, the Great Gardener.
