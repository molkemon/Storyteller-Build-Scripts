When word reached Heunthume of the fate of the Stonewing, nary a citizen lacked an opinion on what had transpired there. Antignollish sentiment exploded, and the people of Akasik were revered as heroes. It was to the credit of the Queen’s Guard that the situation was contained, and riots against the gnolls of Harpylen were largely prevented. It was soon made known that the Queen herself had extended an invitation to the Stonewing that all who wished it would be given royal leave to visit Harpylen, and stay in Heunthume as honoured guests for as long as they desired. It cannot be said what visitors to the Queen’s Throne expected, but certainly it was not who came. For one day, a pair of Stonewing harpies arrived before it, bearing what at first seemed to be a human, but was soon revealed to be a wingless harpy, robbed of flight by her gnollish masters. Though she was old and blind, her song still carried the strength of her flock. And when she sang, the Ivetasananthor at the Queen’s court scrambled to capture her words in ink. For she spoke of her enslavement, and the burning of her flock, how her masters had taken her wings and those of her sisters, deeming them a foul trait in a slave. And so they had taken their wings, but left them their lives. "But," she added, "more was their folly, for now my masters are dead, while we live. And our children will have the wings that were stolen from us. And they will know the sky." Then more Stonewings came, bearing with them the free folk of Akasik, men and women they held in honour. And though the people of the west were hardened by their toil, the warmth of the Queen’s Court chipped away at the stone shielding their hearts. Long did they remain in Heunthume, and in that time they made the Queen’s Throne a gentler place where all who dealt in intrigue were reminded of the strength that only unity can bring.
