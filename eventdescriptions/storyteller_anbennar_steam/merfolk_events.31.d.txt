Storm clouds roll overhead, darkening the peerless sky in ominous gray. Centrestage, the local guard captain stands upright, his brow thick with nervous sweat and hand wrapped in soiled bandages. A general, fresh from the capital, sups on afternoon refreshments, awaiting the captain’s report. I infiltrated the cove last night, braving waves and corrupting music. Dragging myself along the rocks, I saw it. I saw a monstrous, vile merfolk charming a witless woman. When I confronted it, the creature bit me and knocked me out. After I awoke, all that remained was a trail leading inland. I was outplayed once, but I intend to see it skewered for its crimes, general. The general doesn’t question the tale’s veracity despite its flaws: why the merfolk did not kill him, why it dragged itself inland rather than out to sea, or why he hid the blunt bruise on the back of his skull. Instead, the general pouts, his displeasure hardened with bureaucratic indifference. You’ve set the region alight with panic over your blunder on that beach. The people are practically tearing up their own beds to find it, and it’s all your fault for raving about it for pride? Recognition? It’s like you’re asking to be washed away with the tide. But sir, I’ve always been a stalwart soldier. Never have I failed before now, so I ask. We do not tolerate failure, past service be damned. Find the merfolk, or it’s your head.
