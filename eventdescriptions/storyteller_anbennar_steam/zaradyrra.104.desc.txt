The elder sat slumped in his chair, one hand cradling his eyes, pinched in scowl. "The details again, please." The young naga dug through his pack and unfurled a scroll. "No less than a dozen ritually scarred pavers were occupied in the badgering of the merchant convoy. Three eyewitnesses reported observing what they referred to as a shakedown of the caravan leader. Detained members of the outfit insisted they were merely collecting a local toll funding their pay, an account which the local elder later verified. The caravan leader refused compensation—" "Of course." "–to which the posse reacted negatively. Reports suggest a scuffle ensued." The young naga shifted uncomfortably and coughed before proceeding. "Witnesses then say a bard stepped out of his place in the convoy, and declared that he would serenade the beasts to their senses—we have more than ten accounts of that phrasing—which seemed to irk the road workers. Upon the realization of this intent, the posse leader claimed she offered to involve the bard in a performance of their own, after which the incident occurred." The elder waved a hand to cut off his junior. "Hold on, the lout really called them beasts?" The young naga sucked air between his teeth for a moment before commenting, as if to build the courage to speak. "Well, eyewitnesses attest to something similar to that phrasing, though it seems the exact terminology was more candidly unflattering. Perhaps this could partially be attributed to the youth of the bard in question—" "Wait, wait, wait, wait. We don’t have his story? What happened? We have a story from every other party." "Sir, our physicians said the knife wounds reached the vocal cords." "A written testimony, then. He’s a bard, no? He must write." "Sir, following the events of the blood ritual, he is unlikely to keep his hands or voice, assuming he ever recovers." The old minister let a sigh escape from the depths of his heart before he replied.
