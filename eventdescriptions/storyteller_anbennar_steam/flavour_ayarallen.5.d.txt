Atya’s parents wouldn’t let her on the roof, especially lately, but it was trivial to sneak into the cartwright’s block a couple of streets over and clamber up from Hazi’s balcony. At the edge of a forest of laundry they perched, enjoying the windwafted scent and watching their city, greatest and most ancient of the continent, fall.  "My parents want me to go early," Hazi said, quite abruptly, after a while.  "Because of this?" Atya frowned. There had been no great inferno sweeping through the city, at least not yet. The only change on their horizon was the tower. It was just a scaffold now, but it crept further upwards by the day.  "They think the elves will fight." Hazi fiddled with her braid, her legs swinging idly over the lip of the roof. She still had all the affectations of a little girl. Atya suddenly wanted to shake her.  "With what army?" Atya scoffed. "You argued with them, right?"  Hazi stared at her. "They’re my parents."  Atya opened her mouth to speak, but a sound like rustling wind made her wheel around in her seated position. Another patrol of harpy enforcers was passing overhead. Even if she had been the type to take her country’s causes as her own, it was hard not to gape when they flew in formation. From the roof, she could pick out the patterns in the soldiers’ feathers, each different from the next.  "I’ll send letters," Hazi said, finally. "If everything’s good here, it won’t matter. They said I can come back, that we’ll talk later about the temple." Hazi’s eyes were shining. Atya did not want her to cry.  "Yeah." Atya forced a smile. "We’ll write, it’ll be fun. You can’t make too much fun of my handwriting, though."  "Then how will you learn?"
