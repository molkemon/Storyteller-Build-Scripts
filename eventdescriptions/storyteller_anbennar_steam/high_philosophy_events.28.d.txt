Today, the monarch is met with a challenge handed to many High Philosophy practitioners; interpreting the story of Vachava and Sudarkha. A tale carved into the sides of the step well within the High Temple of Shanyutian. The first carving in the series depicts Vachava the High God of Merchants as she worked, moving from mine to forge, from forge to mint, and from mint to carriage to load various objects of gold and silver. When night finally descended, a figure jumped from the carriage and handed Vachava a package. She then mounted her horse and traveled across the land. She rode through mountains and valleys, rivers and bogs, until eventually she arrived at her destination: a small cottage nestled in a verdant glade. In this moment Vachava is shown to be at her most beautiful; bathed in a morning sun, lines of silver traced her hair, and golden outlines graced her footsteps as she approached the cottage. With the brown package in hand she rattled the knocker and waited. The next image is unfortunately lost to history; however, we do possess the carving that follows thereafter. It depicts Vachava face to face with the goddess known as Sudarkha, child of Nermali. She is draped in a bluegreen robe stitched with golden threads, and dragonflies graced her form as they fluttered their prismatic wings. Vachava held Sudarkha closely; their arms wrapped around each other's waists. The package lay at their feet, and was opened to reveal a golden necklace with a blue gemstone poking out of the box. The tale ends there abruptly. No other images survive. What does the monarch think?
