Huddle close, and listen to my song. The drums may sound like thunder, but you need not be afraid. the Makan RiverChild watches over all her riverbanks, and even now she protects us, just as she protected her people so many years ago. I sing of the sieges during the days of the Twin Xhaz, when the people of Fangaula were trapped within their great forts. The gnolls had come down from the east, and they threatened to turn all of us into their slaves, who would be scattered across the plains of Janama like the wind. Yet they did not reckon on Makan. The elders were trapped in our forts too, and they held a great council to discuss surrender to the gnolls. The people were starving, and the elders said to one another, ‘Is it not better to live as a slave than die as a husk? We must not think of our pride, but our people. It is to save our starving, forlorn people why we now surrender.’ But then into the room of council strode Makan, and she bore her drum. Such things were not done. But the elders no longer had the energy to care—until Makan began to bang her drum. The drum of Makan had a great magic within it, and it worked on the sad hearts of the elders. The pallor of surrender lifted from their hearts, and they began to dance and sing despite themselves. They sang of freedom, and justice, and all the songs that their people had yet to sing. They followed Makan from the room of council. The elders went to the people, who watched with fear from their walls at the camps of the hungry, leering gnolls. But Makan’s drum chased away fear just as it drove away sadness, and soon the people were singing too. They danced and sang together, and they forgot their hunger. They sang of the hope of Fangaula, and the roaring floods of the rivers which would sweep away the gnolls. And the river heard their calls, and soon the siege was broken.
