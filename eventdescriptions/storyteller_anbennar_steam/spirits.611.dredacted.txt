I remember him moving behind me. I remember his hand on my shirt, the sound of shuffling as he moves to grab something. I remember the words, dark and rusted against the page. The words that I read in a demon's book, earning his ire. I remember my father's hand or his cane, or his sandal the way it fell upon me, upon my backside, tearing to pieces what little love we had. I remember it falling again, again, again. I remember the words in the demon's book. I remember cowering, curling up into myself, believing I could shrink myself small enough to disappear, I could beg and kowtow until he was pleased. I remember bringing him pistachios, for they are known as the happiness fruit in the local tongue. It was not enough of an offering. I remember the words in the demon's book. I remember the neighbors, the way they smiled at him, the way they smiled at me, the way they told me I looked like him, or like my mother, for he was a great man, and I should be glad to have his nose, his eyes, his ears, his hand on my backside, again, again, again. I remember the words in the demon's book. I remember the words. I remember my words. I remember the flesh. I remember his flesh. I remember his blood. I remember his bones. I remember his childhood. I remember his adolescence. I remember his marriage. I remember my birth. I remember his pride in me. I remember his joy. I remember his fear. I remember the feeling of eating, of being eaten, of the ritual laid out in the secret binding. I remember the words in the demon's book.
