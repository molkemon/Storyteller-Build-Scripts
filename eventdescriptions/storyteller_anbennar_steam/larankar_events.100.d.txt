While Erankar was off on campaign, his son and heir, capable in diplomacy and war alike, accompanied him. And while Seronraha was off on campaign, his wife, a steady and valued influence, accompanied him. Their children were left back in Urvand, to the best tutors around, educated to the pinnacle of what a Taychendi child could be. But with their parents and grandparents not around, it appears they have not developed necessarily as had been hoped. Narathya, the elder sister, is as confident, some say arrogant, as she is innovative and well read. But she has her issues. Holding unpopular, though innovative, ideas about the nature of royal power, her steadfast belief in her right to rule and open disdain for her younger brother has created space for him to build his own support base. Tesheladar is a good person, a relatively skilled warrior, and all know he is, above all, earnest. All also know of the concerns, the murmurs of hotheadedness and ill-judgement. Tesheladar figures himself an elf of the people, who wishes to work alongside, rather than lord over, his subjects. Others figure him weak, a puppet for more domineering figures. If we do not step in, there are widespread fears that should something happen to Emperor Seronraha, the two will tear each other apart in a civil war that would tear our realm asunder. Some say that it is inevitable, but we cannot sit still and do nothing; we must either reaffirm Narathya’s succession or replace her with Tesheladar.
