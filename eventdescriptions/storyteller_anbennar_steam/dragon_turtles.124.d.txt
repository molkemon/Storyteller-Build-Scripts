I am a debtholder, and I am concerned. Let me take a moment to explain why to you. The first reason is my significant counterparty risk. I have lent you some money, but there is nothing stopping you from taking that money and running away except for the deterrents I can impose on your future ability to borrow from me, and from the marketplace more broadly. This is usually only a problem at the end of a time horizon, but you are shifty and I do not trust you. The second is the risk of delinquency. Your commercial ventures are not exactly thriving under your incompetent stewardship, and it is entirely possible that you simply will not be able to repay me, regardless of willingness. I chose to offer you a loan in lieu of the other purposes I could have directed my money towards: the second best thing I could have done with it is what we call an opportunity cost. That opportunity cost is looking tempting right now. I want my money back. Usually, this would be hard for me, but this is exactly why institutions exist: to reduce risks associated with market transactions. If you take a look at your contract, you will see what is called a variable rate clause. This makes an increase in return to compensate for my increased risk contractually legitimate. You will also notice a very strong acceleration clause, which I am going to be invoking now. The wonderful thing about obligations, you see, is that the debtholder can call them in whenever she pleases.
