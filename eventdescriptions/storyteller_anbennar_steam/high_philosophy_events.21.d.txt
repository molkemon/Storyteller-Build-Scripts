Rain clinks onto clay roofing tiles. The monarch peers out of the brick shelter and watches as the dirt road slowly becomes potted with puddles. A guard follows the monarch’s sight towards the water and dejectedly raises his voice, “It’s as if Liilaamora herself sheds tears today…” A perfectly normal idiom, but today, trapped in this roadside shelter, the monarch chooses to meditate on it. The Tragedy of Liilaamora is a well known tale; laid out across a series of small ceramic cylinders deep within the High Temple of Sarilavhan. The tale begins as the High God Jaghut approaches a village where he distributes the contents of a small food basket. The people, shorter relative to Jaghut, hesitantly partake, with one exception; Liilaamora. She stands defiantly, gazing daggers into the High God. In a scene which has since been carved away with a crude knife and melted in several places, Jaghut seems to get on his knees. The two then speak across several images, with each, Liilaamora’s expression softens, and grows warm. Eventually they leave, walking down a path of lotus flowers. They mark their journey with floral garlands as they walk in peace. They eventually reach the High God Dipar, who shouts down to the pair. The two stand defiant in a manner normally not fitting the quiet Jaghut, yet Dipar does not budge and points away from the path. Liilaamora grows weaker and weaker as they approach a small grove of trees, until finally, upon a bed of lotus flowers, she is put to rest. Jaghut weeps with her in unison; their tears forming two rivers which snake through the valleys of Shamakhad. Hidden within the final carving, resting gently upon Liilaamora’s chest, is a small silver rod.
