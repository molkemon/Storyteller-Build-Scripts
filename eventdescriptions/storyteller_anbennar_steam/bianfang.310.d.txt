Decades have passed since the first Yan settlers arrived in this area, paving the way for more recent waves. Second sons, unwanted daughters, petty criminals, retired soldiers, lesser aristocrats seeking new subjects to lord over; they each had their reasons pushing them from Yanshen and pulling them to the district of this region. The natives of the region, displeased to find foreigners occupying the lands and houses of their fathers and brothers felled by the ruler's armies, greatly outnumbered these first settlers, who had to rely on those very armies for protection. In the years since, we have been able to compel the natives’ obedience without holding them at gunpoint. We have opened compulsory schools to teach our language and culture to their children, and given stipends to local men and women who marry into Yan families. We have banned the use of the culture language in commerce and religious ceremonies, and have curtailed culture celebrations and festivals, replacing them with Yan equivalents. Most importantly, the first Yan children born in the realm have grown into adulthood and begun families of their own. The Office of Integration has dispatched a memorandum to the capital, reporting that census records and tax rolls show these nativeborn Yan and recent arrivals from the heartland now constitute the majority of this area’s population.
