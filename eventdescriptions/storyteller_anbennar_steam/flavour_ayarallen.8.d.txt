They crossed into a sunlit atrium, the doors thrown open to a rare sandless breeze. Yadri’s voice kept up with his quick, jerking steps; he fired his words rather than spoke them.  "This space is being used for deliberation at the moment." He indicated a large oval table. "But we can clear all that out, turn it back into bedchambers if you prefer. It was a spiteful choice in the first place."  The harpy’s hand went up. She had done that a couple of times already; it was annoying, like the sign one made at a waiter. "No. Useful this way." She insisted on wearing her armour everywhere, which Yadri knew had to be making her miserable in the summer heat.  "This was the local governor’s?" she asked, as they moved into a central courtyard brocaded in vibrant greens. Only the pool at the centre felt direct sunlight; beyond the colonnades, a huge desert juniper cast its shadow across much of the open space. Yadri blinked a little at the harshness of her tone.  "Yes. We have turned it into a suite of offices."  "Good," she said. "Government should be prestigious."  Yadri was long used to keeping his face neutral in the presence of his betters, but that almost got him. He had mentally resigned himself to the loss of this beautiful place, the end of this happy period between one master and the next. All that harpy rhetoric about selfrule seemed pretty transparent.  "We will need to begin a levy for a spire," she said, and he nodded, scratching another item into his wax. Of course. The harpies had no need for the pleasures of the sunken garden; their servants would raise them high instead. But Yadri couldn’t help noticing that this would get them firmly out of the way.
