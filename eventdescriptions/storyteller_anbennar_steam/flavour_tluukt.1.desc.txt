The priests of EduzVacyn shuffle nervously, awaiting impatiently their uncertain fate in the wake of the lord’s announced visit to the temple. When a conqueror visited the temple, they came to pillage and ruin. When another conqueror visited, they came to pray. The lord does neither; they merely observes the fading murals and the cracked edifices in scornful silence.
Soon, under their direction, the face of the temple changes; the damages caused by the first conqueror are repaired and the ancient edifices are restored to their past glory. But the people change too. The elven highpriests, many presiding since the days of the second conqueror, pass away one by one; illness and weakness, supposedly. They are replaced not by elves, but by men of no distinguished education and of dubious backgrounds. These men begin to rally against the elves, against the second conqueror, against the very foundation of the new teachings. They clamor for a return to the old ways, a return to a cult that glorifies Surakel and not mortal elves.
The rest of Bulwar has unsurprisingly condemned EduzVacyn, seeing the taint of the lord’s influence for what it is and censoring the teachings of the temple. But, in private, many from Bulwar must wonder. How can the words of the First Temple, the place where the light of Surael first truly descended, be ignored so easily? No one speaks these thoughts aloud, but the tension of their presence can be felt all the same.
