I found my lord in his chambers, such as they were, a cleared annex of a domain. Such was his statement of intent at first, to live in the great fortress of his ancestors regardless of local superstition. I did not find him jubilant. There is of course a strange exhalation which happens after any great battle, any great achievement; the surge of purpose leaves the body, and one is left spent. But merry Crovis had always been given to such moments of doubt; it is difficult to live a storybook. I was a foreigner here as much as he, of course. I had the correct combination of traits to serve in my strange, nebulous role; noble birth, sufficient connection to the Adshaws that I might maintain the link in the event of Crovis’ success, and a willingness to contemplate the underhanded. Had we not been friends since our youths, I believe Crovis never would have tolerated my presence. I made him very angry on campaign, numerous times. He thought me dishonourable and patronising, and perhaps I was. I told him, “You should be celebrating, friend.” I dared to put a hand on his shoulder, just briefly. “I am,” said he. I smiled thinly. “But you’re wondering what to do with Frida.” He looked at me in surprise. Crovis never did understand this. how easy he was to read, how direct and noble and simple. It was admirable and it was terrifying; it was the sort of nature which made duplicitous wretches like myself follow him blindly, which made us fret endlessly over him. “We need not kill her,” I said, and his surprise was palpable. I laughed. “Do you think me such a butcher?” He looked at me, evenly, with those very blue eyes. “No, no… but, realistic.” I shook my head. “Not tonight. Tonight you are like Corin triumphant, the hero who saved the realm, and we must do nothing we will regret.” I think that moment was the happiest I ever made my king.
