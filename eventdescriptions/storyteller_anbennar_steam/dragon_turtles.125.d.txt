The economy does not simply keep growing. Investment is heavily tied to consumer demand, and this demand itself is subject to factors within the economy, including employment, which itself is a consequence of investment. When the economy expands, capital flows to efficient ventures, which can afford to return higher rates. But as capital accrues to market-optimal ventures, the opportunities that are left get worse from a return standpoint, and the market becomes saturated with firms. Competition drives prices down, but as this happens, capital efficiency collapses, which collapses investment, which collapses demand. When this contraction happens, individual firms often are the victim of macroeconomic circumstances, and as businesses fold, their employees are laid off, which broadly causes reduced consumer demand, which causes more businesses to dip into the red. You might think all is lost: this isn’t necessarily the case. The government’s role in the economy is to ensure broad prosperity, and if the state is willing to accept upfront losses, it can stimulate demand through some wise fiscal policy. Behaviorally, individuals often respond differently to unexpected cash influxes, and this increases the rate at which they are willing to spend. There are incentivization problems, but that’s the basic theory. I have some extra money. Would you like an unexpected cash influx? I’ve accounted for my own time preference, of course.
