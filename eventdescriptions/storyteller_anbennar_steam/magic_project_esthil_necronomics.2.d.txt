As expected when breaking new ground, experiments yielded not only expected results, but also unexpected ones. Among the expected results is that while the animal bodies are indeed more suitable for labor, it seems that the souls of orks are not as well suited to being bound to animal bodies as they are to humanoid ones, resulting in their souls unraveling faster than if they were simply bound to ork or even goblin bodies, and also seem noticeably less responsive. This is fortunately not an issue for most straightforward tasks, such as simply pulling a plough or cart, but anything more complex than that is infeasible. One unexpected result is that while all souls used in necromantic spells suffer for it, this suffering seems to be significantly amplified when they are bound to bodies different from those they inhabited in life. When interrogating them, significantly more pain is required to compel them to stop bemoaning their suffering and answer questions. It is naturally difficult to quantify exact levels of pain, but by inflicting a standardized and significant level of suffering upon orks before they are executed and used for reanimation, it has been discovered that souls bound to animal bodies suffer roughly four times as much pain as if they were simply bound to their own bodies. For the sake of curiosity, ork souls were bound to goblin bodies and vice versa, but the increase in suffering was high enough to be measurable. Work continues apace, with the latter discovery viewed as a good thing, as it means greater suffering for the orks and goblins, as retaliation for the devastation they have wrought upon Escann. Monthly report of the Black Sepulcher, this month of this year.
