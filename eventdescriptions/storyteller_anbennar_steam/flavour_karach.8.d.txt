The chieftains came to him before the noise had even died down. Tralit was not hard to find. he was standing in the middle of it. "Enough," said the head chieftain. He was used to being heard. "This ends now. You will come back, and you will write what you are paid to write, and we will say no more about this." "I'll come back," Tralit said, "when the songs belong to the people who sing them." The chieftain's eyes swept over the crowd. "Who will put on the fine festivals you perform at? You?" The chieftain spoke with a sneer in his voice. "Yes," said Tralit. "Us." He looked back at the other Hashaz. They were watching their feet. But the commonfolk met his gaze. First Urqe, who had been playing the drum. Then Lazar, who had the horn. "You'll write nothing worth hearing without our resources," the chieftain said. "Without our halls and our instruments and our paper. You'll be nothing but noise." "We already have something worth hearing." Tralit gestured at the crowd around them. "You just tried to silence it." "That wasn't music. That was a rabble." The chieftain stepped closer. "We are the ones who buy your art. We will pay you nothing. You will starve in the streets." The air grew very still. Tralit let the threat sit for a moment. "Then you won't have any songs anymore," he said. Urqe hit her drum. It was a single beat, flat and hard. The chieftain spun toward her, but she hit it again, and this time Lazar answered with the horn, a long low note that had no polish to it at all. Then someone else came in, tentative at first, then stronger. The sound was rough and it knew it. The band was still finding itself, still arguing, each voice pulling against the others. "I am warning you," the chieftain started. Another drum joined. Then another horn. Tralit raised his hand and the crowd looked at him. Then he brought his hand down. What came up was not yet beautiful. But it was growing, each voice learning the shape of the others, the roughness beginning to resolve into something that had not existed before that morning. The chieftain raised his voice, and then raised it again, and then he found he was shouting, and then he found he could not be heard at all.
