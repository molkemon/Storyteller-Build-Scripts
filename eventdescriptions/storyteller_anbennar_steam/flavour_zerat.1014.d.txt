In lands that have not yet been fully subordinated, the naga still refuse to accept their place in service to the state. They protest the tightness of the leash by which we hold them, decrying it as a sign of tsishagya, the madness of the one, the tyranny of a misguided ruler. They seek higher status, and the right to ondemand audiences with our governors and administrators. They wish us to implement reforms to empower councils of the naga. By the ninety-fifth empire, they want pensions. The naga of the palace remain conspicuously silent on these goings-on. These protests are rather small and we could send the army to crush them all with ease, but the question is whether or not it is even worth doing.
