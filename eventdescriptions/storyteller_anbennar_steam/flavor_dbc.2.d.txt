Kuende pads barefoot onto the teak verandah, its boards cool to the touch. This particular villa, snug in a valley of the Dezpa Sedara, is not known to her enemies; it squats flat within a defile of the northrunning ridge, hidden like a snake in the grass. Looking out from its perch, Kuende’s palm presses gentle circles into her big belly. besides the distortion of pregnancy, Kuende’s stretched skin is odd, mottled in places and drained greyish in others. The hag Ilfanill’s magic works upon the child even now, even before she is born. Kuende does not smile as she strokes her stomach. She feels a sharp pain, the eighth this hour; the baby’s kicks are vicious, as if the little one knows what has been done to her. It is a dreadful thing, of course, a profound betrayal of the girl she will bring forth; for Kuende is certain, somehow, that this vile little thing will be a girl. “Know that the kinsmen of your mother were good folk,” Kuende says, as she paces. She is eleven months pregnant, inexpressibly weary. She stops for a moment to lean against the railing; it is almost scorching, even here in the highlands where the day is not so harsh. Kuende does not care if she is truly heard. “They were not like me,” Kuende continues. “They did not find comfort in moonless fields at night; they did not prefer to go unseen. They did not seek out insight; they did not fear the future. They were not cruel, as I am cruel.” She forces herself to keep moving, to ignore the ache. “When they died, undignified and desecrated, I watched the light and the power leave their faces and limbs. I watched as Kwizera’s men made them beg; I watched as Kwizera’s men made them filthy. My beloveds had no finger to point at the Heroic Beasts, no voice to curse Amilak, no poison to dump in the great river of Chi…” Kuende smiles. “but I do.”
