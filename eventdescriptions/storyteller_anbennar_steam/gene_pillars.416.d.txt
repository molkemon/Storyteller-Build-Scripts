Next, we turned to a flower, which we thought was less mobile but more tractable. Given the operative problem is the capacity for fine-grained or even just more granular control, horticultural traditions demonstrate that plant-based tools can be surprisingly malleable. Acid clouds were the clear choice for the creature’s primary purpose. The transmutation rituals are easy enough to adapt, though the keratinous growths were unexpected, and the method’s efficacy at tunnel clearing is obvious. We would need sufficient capacity to overwhelm whatever ventilation systems exist, but a working prototype could justify the resources required for said purpose. Locomotion was initially challenging, though solving this issue thankfully eased the abjuration requirements of experimentation. Ultimately, we were successful in combining movement systems with target selection techniques. Instinctual patterns to seek local optima could be induced and looped back on themselves to build more complex behavioral algorithms. One issue remains however. The optima could not be shorn away from water, which is unlikely to be of use in deeper delves and is confounded by the presence of a large aquatic basin just outside our most effective staging point against the subterranean enemy. We explored the use of enchantment or illusion as workarounds, but the lack of any real nervous system renders these approaches largely ineffective. The overarching problem remains outstanding, with all its attendant corollaries. I will abandon this foray into flora-based research, and return to the fauna I am most familiar with. Snakes offer many natural advantages, and I do believe there is an opportunity to be found there.
