It began as a whine, unnoticed in the bustle of life in the village, the unsettling call only faintly noticed in places of respite, where silence was necessary to soothe the damaged and weary. Then it grew into a groan, one that changed its pitch like the practice of a novice lyre, audible even in conversations between the close and the hearty. Then it rested, and many tried to ignore it, as if it never happened and it never would come again. Oh how wrong they were. When it returned, it was at night, and it reached past the ears of the townsfolk and into their minds; it was a horrid scream, a cursed wail, the shriek of a baby and the scream of a man in pain all at the same time. The strongest pressed their hands up to their ears so hard they were nearly crushed, those that could not found their eyes vibrating as their noses bled, and the least fortunate were left drooling on the floor, their eyes whited out, their lives lost. When the wails ended, the people closest to the chilling song were changed irreparably, and sought only to leave, to leave with at least some of their sanity intact.
