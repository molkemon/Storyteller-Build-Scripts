They had brought his body to her after the battle. Her soldiers had fallen upon Tesheladar, beheaded him, stripped his body, and hacked it apart. Someone had cut his ears off to get at the gold earrings. She had wanted to spit on the traitor’s corpse. Instead, she just stared down at him, unblinking and empty. When she retired to her tent that night, and as she rode back to the capital, the train of war prizes miles long behind her, she could not shake that melancholy that seemed to overtake her. Nobody tried to console her or talk to her. Tesheladar would have had the opposite problem, she knew. Hundreds of warlords and notables constantly in his ear, trying to sway him this way and that. She sensed no disloyalty, no renewed treason, not yet. Her subjects did what they were told, her advisors gave her their opinion when asked. Maybe they were all still in shock. Maybe she was too. The odds were so against her it must have seemed a miracle that she survived, but her confidence and her genius had seen her through. And now she stood atop an empire of corpses, all opposition broken by her blade. Within her hands, at her command, she had the power to stand against the massed desires of those beneath her and the current of history itself. As Narathya reentered Urvand, watched the silent faces stare at her, in awe and in fear, utterly apart and distant, she understood what she was. isolated and all-powerful. She did not need to rely on their love; she did not need to rely on their support. By her determined will, she could shape the world itself, one battle, one crushed revolt at a time.
