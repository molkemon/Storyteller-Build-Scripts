Tesheladar staggered through the battlefield, disbelieving. So many broken, bloodied people. There were too many broken, living people. Crying, calling for help, for aid, for intercession. It was his fault. He couldn’t bring himself to remember details; it was all a blur of thought, decision, and failure. Terrible failure. He wanted to say it was miscommunication, but it was misjudgment. He had won the battle, dispersed his sister’s army, but his mistake had killed thousands. Ancient steel covered the ruler’s body from head to toe, a suit of Precursor armor worth ten years of Urvand’s gold, but he surveyed the death around him and felt like a swindler. Erankar’s blade, that had once cleaved destiny itself in two, hung on his back, but he’d cut down twenty elves with it and he still felt like a thief. He looked for Narathya’s body, now, so that he could finally feel like an Emperor. He knew what the rider would say before he opened his mouth. He had not found her in the battle, and would not amongst the dead. She escaped. More of his friends—advisors, enemies, whatever—gathered around him. They said he should split the army, go hunting. But there were rebels, enemies elsewhere; he told them, but they just ignored him. Tesheladar looked for the Mudaliars, the Dumruktams, then remembered they were not here. This was a victory he was supposed to win on his own. His warlords closed in, begged for direction, but he pushed them aside, found a horse. “We must go to Sthanan, to Nakar, to Clemetar,” Tesheladar told them, as he mounted. “Secure the empire.” I must go to seek advice, Tesheladar told himself. I must go to seek help from those with more wisdom than me. I must secure the Empire.
