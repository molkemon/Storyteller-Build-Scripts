"I found the tale terribly sad, master." "Explain." "The little water drop is like one who works against the flow of chi. He fears the totality of chi, the idea that he is carried through life as part of something greater. He works against this totality and his nature, and weakens himself. As part of the river, embracing the ever-changing nature of chi, he could have been a great waterfall; instead, he is washed away into nothing." "I but" A second student speaks. "Go on," says the master. "Could he have understood anything about himself or the world, achieved any perspective, if he had not left the river and embraced another part of the world? Is the grass not also part of everything, part of chi? Did he not use the riverbank's stability, the grass's height, to reach an understanding no other water drop had? He took on their characteristics, transformed his position, and existed in harmony with a new part of chi." "But he was washed away immediately, mere vapor," began the first student. "As are we all, in not so many years. And he was washed away, as you say, into the air, whence he could see everything, see a great deal more than his kin. Is it not right that some are vapor and some are river? Is it not also transformative to take on the nature of air, in accordance with one's goals and one's nature?"
