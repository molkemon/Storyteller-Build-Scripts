A snake eating its own tail. Khun stared at the specimen in front of her. As a girl, she had listened to her elders’ tales of the natural world in the hope of one day becoming a scholar, though as her scales rustled against her warleathers, that era of stories and youth seemed terribly distant. She had learned. She had learned plenty, but the demand for ablebodies surpassed the need for scholars nowadays. Nowadays, no one could appreciate her knowledge of reptiles. They cared far more about her skills in combat than her knowledge of oddities like the snake in front of her. The snake eating its own tail. She knew from her upbringing that snakes only attempted to eat themselves under extreme duress. At times, when the critters were confused or distressed, they would forget who their tails belonged to, and they would start eating themselves, as though some way, somehow, the act would make their problems go away. No healthy snake made the mistake, yet nevertheless, the phenomenon had been described by many scholars. Even though a snake could not survive eating itself. Khun could sympathize. She had killed another boaga earlier that day. It wasn’t even in battle; he had been bathing. She remembered how frightening it was; even though she wasn’t in the throes of the Bloodcurse, she had gratefully watched the life leak out of his twitching body. Maybe, in another life, he might have appreciated her insight into snake behavior. Maybe instead of slaying one another, they could share a moment of silence to mourn the animal twisted in death before them. Instead, she felt sickened. The Bloodcure’s violence was involuntary; she had chosen to murder today. In many cultures, the snake eating its own tail represented infinity. It symbolized endless cycles. Khun wondered if the people who had dreamt up that metaphor understood snake behavior. She wondered if they knew how afraid and pained the snake must be. How totally out of control it must feel, as it choked on itself.
