The composing chambers sat empty, save for the frustrated scrawling noises of the central figure in the room, hunched over a conductor’s pedestal as they scratched a handful of unsatisfying notes onto a music staff. Kuyata cracked the joints in his fingers, slumped back onto a stool, and considered what work he could neglect. Five, no, six battle hymns left. Those were essential. It would be a disaster to lose control of a vital position due to a midoperation Curseflare. A well-sung verse could trigger the Bloodcurse in the enemy while keeping one’s own soldiers sane. Ten marching lullabies. Not optional if the troops intended to go anywhere. They had to be quiet enough to avoid notice, but strong enough that some halfwit in the rearguard wouldn’t fall asleep. Nineteen training chants. Self-explanatory. Couldn’t slack on those without ruining the next crop of recruits. Six songs of soothing. The most important work of all. Without their influence, an Ashhana was lower than an animal. What use is a person who has ceased to be a person? Skimping on variety wasn’t an option, either. Too much monotony allowed the Curse to creep in and set hold on the unsuspecting. No cuts to writing then, and Kuyata would still need to train his musicians to play the songs. Outside the window, the sound of drums rose into the air. There was an existing supply of ready musicians. Kuyata walked over to the balcony window and disdainfully peered down into the town below, where a small troupe of bards played their instruments in the central square. It seemed to Kuyata that every week the bards donned yet more colorful clothes and longer belts. At a time when every purse was shallow, you could reliably identify bards by the jingle of coins in their pockets. Hiring those vultures would cost a fortune, Kuyata thought bitterly. They still may be worth the price.
