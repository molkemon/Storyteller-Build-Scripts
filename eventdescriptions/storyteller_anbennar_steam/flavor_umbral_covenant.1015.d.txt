The rift opens, and I can see. "Tsishagya," the naga councillor hisses, and I recognize the slur; the 'madness of the one'. It is the greatest sin of the realm's ruler, to become heedless of advice and tradition, convinced of the superiority of one's own singular understanding. It is synonymous with tyranny and misrule. It is a taboo which will save this Empire from the machinations of my Granny. Across the great stone hall I feel a constellation of powerful naga marshaling their magic; the naga train together, of course, and are deeply familiar with the pooling of magic for mutual ends. One cannot accuse them of lacking solidarity; there is even the touch of the Coven about them. I watch in faint surprise and amusement as Granny calculates her odds; I see her slip into shadow, leaving her unsuspecting protégé at the mercy of his advisors. The magic of the naga builds. Behind them I see the shadow of countless millennia, the ghosts of an ancient siblinghood of study and statecraft. Having generally dismissed the naga as a stuffy, self-serving lot, I must suddenly reckon with their heroism.
