The sound was deafening and all-consuming. The cacophonic neighing of the horses, the cries and shouts of the drivers as they whipped them forward, the crash, jolt, and rattle of the chariot frames, every bolt, screw, and lashing leather smashed and thrown about in their places, struggling to keep the Rattalai together. Thirendesh had never taken part in a charge like this before. Thirendesh looked to his partner on the frame for a moment, seeing the mad terror in his eyes. He understood why. But for whatever reason, he just had a good feeling about this. Even as the bristling, impenetrable wall of Kheionai pikes drew closer and closer. If they charged into that, they would all die. Simple as that. Instant death, at least, he hoped it would be. But then an almighty twang from behind him, and the whoosh of stone and firepot. Thirendesh smiled as he saw the wave of projectiles fly over his head. The driver looked back at the Ambhen before him, beginning to grin as he registered them looking up themselves. Then he finally saw the terror on their faces, close enough to relish. He gave an inaudible prayer to the Dread Charioteers of ages past and readied himself. There was a crashing sound, and fire and blood and shattering spears and smoke and bone, and then rattling, screaming, slicing. Maybe a few seconds of it. Thirendesh’s world was smoke and sound, but then he left it, and an open field stretched before him. He had lived; the plan had worked. When he looked behind him, he saw first the bloodied remains of the charge—holes ripped through the Ambhen line, as severed limbs and chunks of gore littered the path of the Rattalai’s entry. Then he saw the Rakulacan cavalry pouring through, following up on the knockout blow. He turned back and whipped his horse to turn as best it could.
