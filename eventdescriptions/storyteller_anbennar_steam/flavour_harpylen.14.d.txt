The night was unlike any other at the Queen’s court. The Ivetasananthor stood mesmerized, unable to tear their gaze from the fluid harmony that unfolded before them, and neither could the Queen herself. Masks of every imaginable shape; demons, dragons, and other mythic beasts from far Haless; drifted and swirled through the air like a gentle tide, concealing the disciplined faces of Harpies beneath. The melody they wove was near impossible, many among the Ivetasananthor later thought. For how could a song be at once so profoundly soothing, yet so vibrant with life? The authors of this vision, of this enchanted night, were none other than the so-called Righteous Masters; the name our scattered sisters in distant Xia had come to call themselves. The mysterious Pzerangi. The matter of the P'ezarangi was a peculiar one. The Ivetasananthor found themselves in the role of students, at least temporarily, for the wise and learned Pzerangi Shifu of the mountains, who made their own understanding of song seem meager by comparison. For many days and many nights they shared their understanding, and for many days and many nights the Ivetasananthor listened. Even when the songs spoke of things beyond comprehension, like the strange force called "Chi," or the somewhat eccentric "Divine Wind," they listened and learned. And by the end, they couldn’t help but feel they gained more than just the old songs returned to them.
