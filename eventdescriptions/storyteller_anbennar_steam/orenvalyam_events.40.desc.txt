The High Kavalal of Royakottar nearly jumped out of his seat as he saw it. “By Nayiru—it’s one of—” Oktikheion smiled, as he welcomed the Mechanim. “This is the Insyaan leader I was telling you about.” It looked like an elf, only built of rippling silversteel. A living relic, standing before him. “Greetings,” it said, in a toneless voice, offering a hand. “My name is quite long, I have therefore shortened it. you may call me Roaring Defiant Against My Makers.” The Kavalali rose, giddily accepted it. What a brilliant name. The Taychendi turned to Oktikheion. “This is just incredible. I mean, it can speak.” “Slightly offended. I can speak,” the Mechanim replied, “but I am not an it.” Oktikheion nodded. “they have a strong sense of self.” Royakottar rubbed at his beard. “I have to wonder how this fits doctrine… if they’re a Precursor relic, are they not a product of—” Then, epiphany. “By Nayiru, this doesn’t contradict—it proves….” He fell deep into thought. Roaring Defiant turned to the Kheionai. “Confused. your colleague speaks and I do not comprehend.” Oktikheion explained. “The Elvenness of Relics. Our belief that magic is a living thing, and the process of relic creation entraps it—just note how similar the enchantment of objects and people is, for instance—and that artificery simply prolongs that grim practice. Who knows what living energies you’re enslaving in your new relics, after all. You can’t ask them.” “But what if we could.” Royakottar whispered, as his mind unravelled the future. “Flesh of iron, hands of steel… and with these hands that will not break…” The Mechanim backed away. “Remembering. I have heard that sentiment.” Oktikheion chuckled, nervously. “Come now, Tesh. You know the history.” “Bitter. I lived it.” But Royakottar just looked up, eyes of guilt, eyes of hope. “But we won’t make the same mistake again.” He turned to Roaring Defiant. “As equals, as partners… we could build Dawn, together. You could be heroes.”
