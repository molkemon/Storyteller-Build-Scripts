The mages in the room stared at the scrying mirrors, wide-eyed and dumbstruck. The Vernmen in the dragon chamber looked to be doing about the same thing. The Cannorian mages had thrown themselves into the fray, sending a barrage of spells slamming into the beast’s side. It was an impressive display that awed the lower-ranked mages and even made the archmages tip their proverbial hats. Even better for the Vernmen, it had worked. Just as the Vernmen had exhausted their mana, the clerics completed their spell and exorcised the specters within the dragon’s bonewhite frame. The Challúmaton golem lay lifeless on the chamber floor, well, more lifeless than it already was, being undead and all. Ramujith had been glued to his seat as he watched the Vernmen’s victory, seeing fifteen hundred years of work evaporate in an instant. But Jalinwathi, who had overseen half a century of that work, had leapt out of his chair, hands on his head, disbelieving, shocked silent, but in a different way to the others. They didn’t know it at first, but they did. For the archmage could contain his pure, unadulterated happiness no longer. “FREEDOM. I’M FREE. I’M FREE.” The archmage looked at his colleague, as did the mages around them. “But Jalinwathi, the dragon. The beast Sulathrezhil subdued, the great work of the Blue House?” Jalinwathi turned, shook his head softly. He had tears of joy in his eyes. “Ramu, be honest. We were never getting anywhere anyway.” The two archmages turned back to the scrying mirrors; an army of guards had rushed in now. The Vernmen held their hands up, thoroughly and inescapably caught. Captured. “So,” Ramujith said. “What happens now? It’s just a hunk of dead metal, now.” His colleague simply shrugged. “Well, we both have projects we need to fund, right?”
