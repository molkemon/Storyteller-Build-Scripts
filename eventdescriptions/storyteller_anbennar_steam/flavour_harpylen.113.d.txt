The sundering of Siadanlen seemed a nearly endless source of tales and songs for the Ivetasananthor. These songs were helpful in consolidating a cultural identity in Harpylen, and though their actual historical reliability was lacking, some would argue that what they lacked in factuality, they more than made up for in flair and dramatics. The harpy migration north into the Serpentspine, therefore seemed more fanciful than truthful, and most hunters initially dismissed the notion. The Serpentspine was infamously hostile, then as now, and conventional wisdom held that, if such a migration did occur, it would almost certainly have ended in their doom. Yet some hunters persisted, and gathered many tales from old Siadanlen, all singing the same song. A migration could have occurred, and a large one at that. While most had fled the phoenix south, some daring souls could have chosen to brave the peaks of the Serpentspine. With sufficient sources pointing to such an occurrence, an expedition was prepared. This was an unusual task, and only the hardiest hunters were chosen to partake. The Serpentspine would not suffer weak wings, and it was known that the cold, the air, and the winds would readily kill a harpy in flight, as truly as an arrow. Preparations were meticulous, and the Queendom's dwarven subjects proved invaluable, teaching the hunters the way of climbing on foot, as flight would not be feasible in cruel weather. And one day, all was prepared, and the hunters set out, taking wing and flying up along the very walls of the Serpentspine. The mountains were predictably inhospitable, and the hunters were soon forced to advance largely on foot, as the mountain's icy breath made flying at length both cold and dangerous. Yet supply lines held, and while progress was slow, the hunters climbed steadily up into the Serpentspine.
