Swing, step. Swing, step. Swing, step. The slave advanced, scythe of sharp iron in hand, as the two slaves behind him picked up the maize he cut down, like clockwork. He did not even pause to move a stray bang of black hair out of his face. It was hard work, boring work, tiring work, but it was work he was good at. He would be whipped for not being good at it, after all. Today, the sun was beating down on them. The air was hot and dry, though the sweat made the wrought iron collar around the slave’s neck a little less uncomfortable; it was a small consolation. Only the overseer’s whistle could give that. When it blew, the slave stood, stowed his scythe, and helped the others bring the corn stalks back to the carts. The slaves kept their break in the shadow of their master’s statue. It towered over the field, giving them a little shade at certain points of the day, like a sundial. A constant yet temporary respite, given at the same time each day for the past seventeen years of the slave’s life, just like the break he received halfway through the day. He looked up to the statue, for a moment, to the aging elf who owned him, then to the base, built of different stone. A statue of a different, immortal master had once stood where he did, a reminder that the cycle of the world had once been broken. But the Slaver Nobles were but a memory now, fallen and faded. Swing, step. Swing, step. Swing, step. The slave advanced, scythe of sharp iron in hand, as the two slaves behind him picked up the maize he cut down, like clockwork. This time, though, something did give him pause—an extraordinary shadow. The slave looked up to see an airship sailing across the sky, his warlord returning from some venture. He wondered, for a moment, if the warlords would fall, just as their predecessors once did.
