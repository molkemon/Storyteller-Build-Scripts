I was stuck with the chattering lovebirds, and yet the singular strangeness of our mission transfixed me. Ideas about ancient BulwaroDamerian layout and architecture which had once been theoretical were now physical, real. I found myself doing the best work of my life, understanding the logic of this ancient place as though it were my own. I thought only of the strange, switchback labyrinths through which I trod, the recesses in the walls for which I could ascribe no specific purpose. As so often, the theoretical was a balm for the real. I did not have time to think about my petty loneliness, about Emondo’s future, about the life he would live which was complete without me. I remembered, for a perfect while, that I could possess a future of the mind, an existence no less real. We arrived in a great chamber, in which the corpses of the dead had been arranged as if at council, six bodies seated in six stone thrones. Their heads had been replaced with those of animals – here a leopard, here a crane, here a horned bull. They wore all the finery of ancient Bulwar, their linens pristinely preserved. One could still make out the gorgeous threading at the hems, the symbols which, to my eye, looked almost priestly. I wondered if even the moths avoided the strange song that even now filled our heads, louder than before – the sympathetic ringing of old magic. I whispered to myself, “Erragal? ”, but my comprehension came too late.
