As Marshal Jianlin Shi finishes the kowtow, he pushes the ornate sandalwood box toward the ruler of the realm with a look of triumph. Inside is a scroll addressed to the monarch from another ruler, written in sharp, clawlike characters. As in the ruler’s letter to the commander of the Tiger Fort, the ruler of Hubao heaps praise and accolades upon their counterpart, fellow shield of Yanshen against the monsters and barbarians to the north, whose warriors are as strong and swift as the mighty Yanhe. With gratuitous humility, the ruler of Hubao laments that Hubao cannot reach its full defensive potential, lacking the coin to feed its soldiers, purchase weapons, and make repairs to the fortress. It poses the question of whether a generous benefactor could restore Hubao to its reputation as an impregnable fortress. Seeing through the flattery and pleasant falsehoods, the monarch understands perfectly what is required for Hubao’s conditional obeisance. Dispatching a courtier to make arrangements with the treasury for the bribe, the monarch turns his attention to Jianlin Shi. The Marshal has proven himself as a shrewd diplomat. Perhaps he would be more useful sipping tea with foreign dignitaries than spilling blood on the battlefield?
